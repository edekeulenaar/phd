# **Part V. Modulation and consensus**

==In a separate page, in front of every chapter, make clear what my contribution for each co-authored was.==

If Part IV showed the cost of moderation that only enforces, Part V turns to what platforms have begun to do about it. The story it tells is one of changing technique: away from adjudication — ruling content acceptable or not, item by item — and toward modulation, and, latterly, toward the agnostic, consensus-driven methods that hand the hardest judgements back to users. The two chapters follow this movement on a single platform across two regimes: the long modulation of Twitter (Chapter 12), and its mutation into X (Chapter 13).

Chapter 12 traces a history of objectionability in Twitter's moderation between 2006 and 2022. Using digital methods and web history to follow how the platform's policies and practices defined and redefined objectionable content over sixteen years, it argues that Twitter was not so much abandoning an Americanised view of free speech as building a crisis-resistant speech architecture — one able to withstand external shocks, public criticism and shifting speech norms. This is what the chapter calls modulated moderation: a form of normative plasticity whose aim is not, in the end, to settle whether a given piece of content is acceptable, but to moderate it according to evolving and ever-contingent public conceptions of objectionability. The censorial function, in other words, learns to bend. Through dynamic techniques such as demotion, the platform stops trying to draw a fixed line and starts managing visibility against a moving threshold of tolerance.

Chapter 13 follows the same platform into its life as X. Moderation has not disappeared under Musk's tenure, but it has become unclear and inconsistent. The chapter examines the transition across three registers: the policy changes before and after the acquisition; their enforcement, particularly through demotion, removal and Community Notes; and the part that newer methods such as Community Notes play in shaping debate around polarising issues in European public discourse. What it describes is an apparent shift from top-down adjudication toward more agnostic, consensus-driven moderation — an attempt to settle contested questions not by ruling on them but by surfacing the agreement that emerges when users who usually disagree converge. It weighs the potentials of this move against its limits, and is accompanied by an open dataset documenting the policies, enforcement and notes on which the analysis rests.

Read together, the two chapters describe moderation drifting back toward the dialogical, consensus-building function with which this thesis began — the older work of holding a plurivocal exchange together, now delegated to architecture and to the crowd. Whether that drift amounts to a genuine recovery of that function, or only to its appearance — a way of relocating the labour of adjudication without resolving it — is the question the Conclusion takes up.
