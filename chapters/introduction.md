# Introduction

<div style="text-align: right;">The deepest feeling always shows itself in silence;</div>
<div style="text-align: right;">not in silence, but restraint.</div>

<div style="text-align: right;">Marianne Moore</div>

One common denominator of studies on social media is an interest in how platforms reconfigure conditions for public debate. Depending, of course, on the geopolitical vantage point a given study inadvertently adopts, many platforms were once celebrated for "liberalising" political expression from the editorial gatekeeping of legacy media. The Arab Spring was for a time an emblematic case study of how platforms would turn individual expression into a form of democratic participation [@howardDemocracysFourthWave2013]. Decades later, these same mechanisms would liberate "problematic" actors and ideas until then relatively marginalised in public debate [@mungerRightWingYouTubeSupply2022a], to name far right political forces that straddled the norms of public expression in places long assumed to be mature democracies [@eatwellNationalPopulismRevolt2018].

One important diagnosis of this transformation has tended to frame platforms as producing *excessive information*. On this account, the relaxation of editorial gatekeeping allows bad actors to re-emerge in public and at times corrupt public opinion through manipulation and coordinated influences, all while pushing the boundaries of platform policies as a proxy for Overton windows. The critique, here, is that the gatekeeping forces of the past, though often arbitrary and forbidding, did ensure basic standards of informational intelligibility, accuracy and social cohesion. Their absence set the scene for a variety of "information disorders" [@socialscienceresearchcouncilHateSpeechInformation2020] where incommensurably diverse perspectives clash and produce a general atmosphere of toxicity, particularly through misinformation and hostile language.

From a regulatory perspective, a good deal of responses to these disruptions have consisted in honing a more decisive answer to what counts as acceptable speech in the first place. Much has been said of EU and (as of late) Brazilian platform regulation as quests to recover a basic sense of normalcy to public expression, particularly by reinforcing editorial norms against discrimination, sedition and attempts to overthrow key institutions [cit]. But with the level of public debate around such legislation [cit], one can place these agreements within an otherwise constantly evolving and contentious timeline of speech norms that have shifted across time and place. One of the few certainties to emerge from such processes is that speech norms remain "in constant negotiation" [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990] over which aspects of history may or may not be allowed to recur through language and behaviour (see @chongCompetingNormsFree2018b.

A third diagnosis operates at a more general level: it contends that, by virtue of their "massive pluralism" [@weylCommunityDesign2026a], social media have reconfigured the means to moderation and speech governance more generally. One frequent culprit is centralisation [@sabloskyDangerousOrganizationsFacebooks2021]. Except for federalised platform systems [@rozenshteinModeratingFediverseContent2023] and user reporting, social media platforms continue to detain most of the means to define and enforce their content moderation rules. Much has been said about how this power has befallen on private companies at the detriment of otherwise "traditional" speech governing actors, to think of the state and once perhaps of the church. And much more has been said about the implications of this power concentration, be that against users [cit], institutions [cit] or states [cit].  

But while they do detain many means to speech governance, these very same platforms also redistribute governance mechanisms across other actors [@rbadouardPlateformesNouveauxCenseurs]. So emerges a strange paradox: while platforms may well have   given participative affordances to self-expression, they also distribute censorial capacities amongst themselves and the actors they host. For one, different platforms may choose to delimit speech through different ideological axes [cit]. Users have also been granted their own means to moderate speech, be that in Facebook groups [cit], YouTube and Instagram comments [cit], subreddits [cit], and separately online fora. Throughout, there are algorithmic and AI systems with a fair share of autonomy in their decisions to filter, demote, and reformulate information based on curated datasets, normative thresholds and definitions. And over these exist of course a plethora of traditional actors aiming to influence moderation policies in their own way, be those regulators or different kinds of civil society organisations [@gorwaPlatformGovernanceTriangle2019a]. 

Human or human-made, these actors create infrastructures (and thus conditions) that reconfigure and redistribute the means to speech governance. In doing so, they also demarcate their own public spheres and the "speech cultures" they produce. This taps into a fundamental definition of moderation as an framing or *constitutive* force. That is: moderation "materialises" the conditions and boundaries of expression of online public spheres as "discursive architectures" [@mecheverriaArquitecturaDiscursivaPlataformas, 2], as do other speech governance techniques from censorship [@postCensorshipSilencingPractices1998]; to debate management [@habermasNewStructuralTransformation2023] and gatekeeping techniques [@shoemakerGatekeepingTheory2009]; media regulation; journalistic and editorial conventions; AI alignment [@gabrielArtificialIntelligenceValues2020]; or other. Each of these form informational environments that "accommodate some expressive modes and not others” [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990] by enforcing specific norms of expression. They also create the streams through which less permitted speech flows towards wherever it is best  expressed [@dezeeuwTracingNormieficationCrossplatform2020]. These environments may be "legacy" media [@martinCensuresDansMonde2016], platforms of all sizes [@gillespietarletonCustodiansInternet2018] and kinds [@gorwaModeratingModelMarketplaces2024b], or more fundamentally language itself [@allanForbiddenWordsTaboo2006; @bourdieuLangagePouvoirSymbolique2001]. 

Speech governance techniques also manage information in broader societal processes. One may think of memorial laws and anti-discrimination norms as cases in which the management of historically-bound public speech constitutes a form of remembrance, reconciliation or retributive justice [@ricoeurMemoireHistoireOubli2006a]. Speech is seen as conjuring, and thus re-legitimising circumstances from the past [@butlerExcitableSpeechPolitics1997] that the state [@levinsonSilencingPublicMonuments] or communities of people [@matsudaWordsThatWound2018] no longer wish to be repeated. There are post-war norms to "never again" [@adornoEducationAuschwitz2020] allow monopolies of violence to return, and to guarantee it there are doctrines of democratic "militancy" or "self-defence" [@loewensteinMilitantDemocracyFundamental1937]. There are also decommunisation, derussification [@greenLanguageLullabiesRussification1997], denazification [@vincentPunirReeduquerProcess2008], or Latin American processes of (re)democratisation foreclosing military regimes [@vizquadratHistoriaMemoriaPolitica2016]. A key part of such phenomena is to modify, redirect and otherwise transform speech in ways conducive to prospective historical transformation [@mcintoshLanguageTrumpEra2020]. Crystallized in legislation, institutional frameworks and political culture, these techniques constitute memorial levers by which individuals act upon their collective past. 

Consensus-building processes remain fundamental building blocks of speech moderation norms. Unless they are decided and enforced by authority [@allanForbiddenWordsTaboo2006], speech norms can result from long deliberation processes that produce a sense of common understanding in the definition, the justification and measures taken to abide by common norms [@habermasContributionsDiscourseTheory2020, 14]. These constitute (or originate from) background premises or "lifeworld" of potential dialogue (ibid). The process can of course be contested and fundamentally unresolvable, as have highlighted responses to Habermas' framework for deliberative democracy [@mouffePolitical2005; @fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990]. At issue are norms that are in fact *essentially* contested [@delaatCoercionEmpowermentModeration2012] and subject to deep disagreements [@fogelinLogicDeepDisagreements1985] that can only result in partial or no consensus at all, if there ever is one. This is what is often observable in the creation of "counter publics" [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990], particularly those emerging from unresolved tensions between opposite understandings and applications of fundamental norms — a "restraint" that guards "the deepest of feelings", as evoked earlier by Marianne Moore. 

But the role of moderation is not antithetical to producing the level of consensus required for platforms to function as aspirational public spheres — it can in fact be a means to it. In political and communicational philosophy, moderation is sometimes described as condition and an ensemble of mechanisms that make dialogue possible. A moderator is there to ensure equal opportunity to participate in deliberation — and deliberation, in turn, to legitimate the basic norms by which individuals engage in dialogue [@cbousquetLimitingRightModerate2025, 2]. It is also a political ethos grounded in dialogue, mutual adjustment, and compromise among opposing camps [@craiutuFacesModerationArt2017]; less an agreement on substantive ideas than a way of defending them so that contradiction remains possible in the first place [@cthuderozModerationTout]. Indeed, early writings on *content* moderation spoke precisely of the conditions in which moderators would ensure the basic conditions for exchange and intelligibility in online fora [@edwardsMODERATOREMERGINGDEMOCRATIC; @wrightDemocracyDeliberationDesign2007b]. 

That definition elapsed over time as platforms needed to reinforce moderation "at scale" [@gillespieContentModerationAI2020] against major scandals [@hillDefinitelyNotBusiness2024] and varying levels of objectionable content. What occurs in platforms, then, is less an abuse of moderation than a reconfiguration under fairly different logics of compliance, brand safety, and scaling solutions for cleaning up dirty, noisy and otherwise unacceptable content. The consequence of this reconfiguration is a general conflict not merely about specific issues over what is and is not acceptable, but about what moderation means and should be carried out in the first place. 

## Guiding questions

This thesis explores five aspects in the transformation of platform content moderation. It explores how speech norms have formed platform content moderation practices; how the struggle to form consensus around such norms affects the wider geography of online public spheres; and how content moderation strategies evolve in relation to various crises of consensus and legitimacy.

In [Part I. What is moderation?](#/part-1), I ask: what kind of speech governance technique is moderation in the context of other techniques, such as censorship, gatekeeping, or debate management? How does the literature theorise speech moderation beyond new media studies, and what light do such definitions bring about how else speech moderation has been practiced in history, or otherwise today? 

Given its theoretical background, how does one set out to study moderation practices online? How does one capture moderation when, like censorial practices, it tends to only leave *traces* behind rather than explicit interventions? 

In [Part II. Speech affordances](#/part-2), the guiding question is: how does moderation come to constitute platforms and platform speech cultures? How does it shape the circulation of more or less acceptable information across platforms and platform spaces (for example, commenting spheres)? 

In [Part III. Moderation crises](#/part-3), I ask: how does moderation become subject to disagreements across users, platforms, different media and institutions? What happens when such actors disagree about, or fail to share, the basic definitions of categories of unacceptable speech? 

In [Part IV. Ruptures](#/part-4), I then ask: what are the consequences of such disagreements in creating "counter publics" and "counter spheres", including alt-tech platforms? What is the long-term impact of this process of platform fragmentation? 

Finally, in [Part V. Modulation and consensus](#/part-5), I explore how such disagreements have transformed content moderation techniques and broader strategies, be it demotion or including Community Notes.

Each of these parts and their chapters are detailed below. 

### Part I. What is moderation?

[Part I. What is moderation?](#/part-1) establishes the conceptual and methodological ground for the thesis. 

[Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1) begins from a confusion its object has come to embody — that "moderation" and "censorship" are now used almost interchangeably by platforms and their critics alike — and asks what kind of practice moderation is once that normative opposition is set aside. Reading content moderation studies alongside the literature on adjacent speech governance techniques, especially censorship, it proceeds through a distant reading [@morettiDistantReading2013] of a large corpus of scholarship on speech governance, from which I build a taxonomy of how moderation and censorship are defined, by whom they are practiced, why, and through which techniques. 

[Chapter 2. After deplatforming](#/chapter-2) then asks how moderation can be studied empirically, developing a framework for reconstructing the "scene of disappearance" of moderated content, later extending the concern to the moderation behaviour of LLMs in [Chapter 5. LLMs and the generation of moderate speech](#/chapter-5). Having mentioned various works from censorship studies in the previous chapter, it draws from the methods of censorship scholars. I focus on five components of moderation: its documentation in the form of platform policies; how such documents can be used to determine what is moderated and how; reverse-engineering various moderation practices, such as removal, flagging or demotion; and exploring how users react to such effects.

### Part II. Speech affordances

[Part II. Speech affordances](#/part-2) examines how speech norms come to be materialised in the technical and (sub)cultural fabric of platforms. By "speech affordances", I mean the policies, techniques, and user speech cultures that together signal what may and may not be said in a given space and thereby govern how content circulates within and across more- and less-moderated regions of the Web. 

[Chapter 3. The affordances of extreme speech](#/chapter-3) theorises this through the affordances of extreme speech, following the migration of a meme across 4chan, Twitter, and YouTube. Such affordances materialize what may and may not be said in a given platform (and how), and thereby forms the relation and information circulation across different spaces (for example, from “mainstream” to “alt-tech” spaces). 

In turn, [Chapter 4. The affordances of replacement narratives](#/chapter-4) develops a case study of "replacement" narratives, attending to the linguistic subterfuges — displacements, dissimulations, coded "transcodings" — through which a taboo is signalled without being stated. Both describe how speech norms materialize into speech affordances and form counter-spheres with competing affordances.

[Chapter 5. LLMs and the generation of moderate speech](#/chapter-5) explores how speech norms are operationalised in LLM text generation. It is about how five eight models (GPT 3.5 and 4, Llama2 and 3, Claude Sonnet, Haiku, and Mistral) respond to the top controversial questions posted on multilingual Q&A subreddits. I explore the discursive techniques each LLM uses — or what I call “moderate speech” — when responding to questions with varying degrees of controversy and risk, as defined by each model's moderation API. 

### Part III. Moderation crises

Having explained how speech norms become a formative component of platforms as public spheres, in [Part III. Moderation crises](#/part-3) I describe what happens when such norms diverge across (1) platforms and their users; (2) platforms and legal institutions; and (3) platforms, users and media organizations. The main focus on this chapter is to describe how dissensus emerges across these actors with regards to what they consider to be acceptable or truthful, and eventually what the impact of such dissensus is on the formation and use of "alt-tech" as examples of online counter-spheres.

[Chapter 6. Moderation in crisis](#/chapter-6) examines conflict between platforms and their publics through the case of YouTube comments between 2004 and 2022. The study is about how YouTube commenters define and dispute what is hateful, misinformation and otherwise unacceptable in the platform throughout 2004 and 2018, and how eventual disagreements shape the way users view the platform from an “open sphere” to a corporate entity. 

[Chapter 7. Of migrants and strangers](#/chapter-7) turns to the dissensus between platforms and the editorial norms of *legacy media*, following how Dutch broadcasters and their social media audiences diverged over the "refugee crisis" of 2013–2018. Here, my co-authors and I explore how social media publics and Dutch broadcasters begin to diverge around Dutch speech norms about discrimination in relation to the crisis (2015-2018). As users and broadcasters frame the refugee crisis in different normative lenses, they also began to produce mutually alternative facts about the crisis.

[Chapter 8. Twitter as an accidental authority](#/chapter-8) considers platforms and public health organisations, showing how Twitter became an inadvertent or "accident" authority while trying to surface authoritative information about COVID-19 transmission, treatment and prevention information through its fact-checking systems. As local health organizations and state authorities failed to maintain stable consensus over time and amongst themselves, Twitter steps in as an authority in its own right by moderating those public health authorities for allegedly spreading falsehoods. 

Finally, [chapter 9](#/chapter-9) addresses the dissensus between platforms and broader processes of transitional justice. It introduces the concept of *normative dislocation* to explain how US-rooted moderation standards failed to register local Brazilian prohibitions on militaristic speech during the 2022 elections by prioritising electoral "misinformation" over open calls for a military coup. It shows how this “dislocation” allowed the spread of content once contained by legacy media, and threatened to maintain a fragile but key historical consensus against military political violence.

### Part IV. Ruptures

[Part IV. Ruptures](#/part-4) considers what emerges from deep disagreements about content moderation norms. 

[Chapter 10. Platform ruptures and the formation of online counter spheres](#/chapter-10) defines “alt-tech” platforms as counter-spheres that emerge from unresolved disagreements about speech norms. It charts a brief history of a few major normative ruptures that have seen the constitution of multiple alt-tech spheres across the Web, namely cloud hosts,  blogging sites, wikis, social media and messaging applications. It proposes the notion of "platform ruptures" to describe a complementary trend to "platformisation" [@helmondPlatformizationWebMaking2015].

[Chapter 11. Epistemic ruptures in the encyclopedias of alternative facts](#/chapter-11) turns from technical and platform ruptures to epistemic ones, following how editors banned from Wikipedia fork its content into rival encyclopaedias like Metapedia, Infogalactic and Rightpedia — Grokipedia did not exist just yet. Reading Wikipedia through Lyotard's notion of postmodern conditions for knowledge production, it argues that the platform's consensus-and-neutrality ideal meets a limit wherever disagreements prove irreconcilable. Alternative wikis arise at those fault lines not as a symptom of "post-truth" indifference to facts, but as attempts to give alternative epistemic viewpoints a platformed, and thus "universal", format.

[Chapter 12. Deplatforming, demotion and folk theories of Big Tech persecution](#/chapter-12) examines the obverse dynamic — it  analyses how deplatforming and demotion become entangled with the conspiratorial "folk theories" users develop about moderation itself during YouTube's handling of COVID-19 "borderline content". It is placed under this section because it illustrates how the users YouTube deplatforms do not stop using the platform, but simply change their uses in a way coherent with the narratives they were deplatformed for. Users sanctioned for COVID-19 conspiracy theories may post their original content on Bitchute but still use YouTube as a primary source of study — i.e., the place where conspirators dwell, including YouTube itself. 

## Part V. Modulation and consensus

[Part V. Modulation and consensus](#/part-5) looks at how platforms dealt with the crises of consensus and legitimacy addressed above.

[Chapter 13.  Modulating moderation](#/chapter-13) explores how objectionable content has been defined by Twitter content moderation practices from 2010 to shortly after Musk’s takeover. It argues that Twitter has transitioned from an adjudicative to a modular form of content moderation, which uses dynamic techniques, such as demotion, to build what my co-authors and I call a "crisis-resistant" speech architecture that is largely normative agnostic. 

The final study, [Chapter 14. From Twitter to X](#/chapter-14), is about how X, in turn, extended Twitter’s policy of modularity to “consensus-building mechanisms” such as Community Notes. Read against the broader call for "prosocial" alternatives to punitive moderation, it asks whether moderation is now drifting from adjudication back toward the dialogical, consensus-seeking function with which the thesis began — returning to some of the questions posed in [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1).

## Methodologies

Throughout, the thesis operationalises notions from political philosophy and other fields that have contributed to the study of speech governance into digital methods [@rogersDigitalMethods2013]. Because moderation is ephemeral and frequently invisible, capturing it requires a layered approach: *trace research* that reconstructs what has been removed, demoted, or relabelled from the residue it leaves behind; *dynamic archiving* that captures content before and as moderation acts upon it; distant reading for the comparative analysis of large textual corpora; natural-language-processing techniques such as word embeddings, word trees, and subject–verb–object networks for comparing how different publics frame the same events; and close, qualitative reading of platform policies, interface changes, and the conceptual literature. 

Visualisation in this thesis is treated as a method in its own right. This follows from the digital methods tradition the work inhabits, in which the making of a figure is itself a stage of analysis: deciding what to count, how to bin and scale it, and what to place beside what are interpretive acts that shape and sometimes determine what can be concluded. I have tried, accordingly, to make those choices as visible as possible, which is why almost every empirical chapter opens with a method diagram that lays out the pipeline from collection to analysis. 

The figures in this thesis pursue two aims that occasionally pull in opposite directions. The first is to give justice to express the complexity and richness of platform data, particularly when it involves controversy mapping. Controversies, especially around moderation, tend to resist tidy summaries: they involve fairly unstable actors and ideas over time, oftentimes more nuanced than sociological and political categorisations propose. This is why several chapters reach for forms built to carry distributional and relational nuance, such as alluvial (or Sankey) diagrams that trace relations between multi-categorical data; beeswarm plots that can show individual posts arrayed over time rather than collapsed into averages or categories; word trees and word-embedding maps that make visible how user discussions branch through contested terms. In most cases, the point is to visualise the transformations and texture of normative disagreements.

The second aim is to render legible the workings of moderation systems themselves. Moderation is, by design, a process that prefers to remain unseen. The thesis treats this invisibility as something to be remapped, or "reverse-engineered". A heatmap that places unmoderated posts in one colour and moderated posts in another turns an opaque enforcement pattern into a readable surface; a timeline of a platform's evolving COVID-19 policy makes a moving target hold still long enough to be examined; a beeswarm of flags and suspensions shows not just that a platform intervened but when, how often, and against whom. In this register, the visualisation serves as partial evidence of what remains after moderation. In some cases, reconstructions take the form of an interactive web visualisation to allow a reader to traverse a full dataset for other potential conclusions outside of those made by a given a chapter.

## The writing process

This is an article-based thesis. It assembles a body of empirical and conceptual work produced from roughly 2019 (before the exact start of a PhD programme) to 2025. 

The intellectual trajectory tended to run from the particular to the general. It began with concrete studies on how normative or epistemic disagreements result in "platform ruptures" [@dekeulenaarAltpediasPartisanEpistemics2019]; how moderation is practiced (via deplatforming, demotion or other) and how it transforms specific platform uses (e.g., conspiracy theorisation) [@dekeulenaarDeplatformingDemotionFolk2021a]; how moderation systems constitute "speech affordances" [@dekeulenaarAffordancesExtremeSpeech2023] and mould the itineraries of deplatformed content across platforms [@dekeulenaarAffordancesReplacementNarratives2023]; how policy concepts are shaped [@dekeulenaarModulatingModerationHistory2023a]; how moderation intervened in public health confusions over COVID-19 [@keulenaar6TwitterAccidental2023]; how, after a series of public crises, emerging platforms changed their underlying moderation philosophies [@dekeulenaarTwitterDemotionCommunity2025]; and how speech norms are implemented in new information management systems like LLMs [@dekeulenaarLLMsGenerationModerate2025]. [Chapter 6. Moderation in crisis](#/chapter-6) is a study presented at the Association of Internet Researcher's Conference in 2022 but not yet published, and [Chapter 10. Platform ruptures and the formation of online counter spheres](#/chapter-10) a presentation given at a workshop at the University of Copenhagen in 2024. [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1) is an updated version of a presentation given to a conference on censorship in 2023, in Lisbon, which later became a book chapter [@zotero-item-46680]. 

The conceptual apparatus presented in the opening pages is, in that sense, a retrospective synthesis: it names what the case studies, taken together, turned out to be about.

## A note on bibliographic readings

Finally, a word on sources. Debates about platform regulation have an unmistakable centre of gravity in the United States, and a corresponding tendency to universalise one particular legal and cultural settlement around free expression. This thesis deliberately reads as internationally as it can — drawing on Francophone, Lusophone, German, and other scholarly and legal traditions, and on cases situated in Brazil, the Netherlands, and beyond — in order to reflect histories of speech regulation that exceed the US-centric frame, and to take seriously the locally embedded conventions that moderation so often dislocates.

## Transition

With these orientations in place, I turn first to the conceptual question on which the rest depends. Before one can study how moderation constitutes platforms, fractures into crises, or reorganises the geography of online speech, one needs a working account of what moderation *is*, and of how it differs from — and overlaps with — the older practices of censorship and debate management it has come to resemble. That is the task of [Part I. What is moderation?](#/part-1).
