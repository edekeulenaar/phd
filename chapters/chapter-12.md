# Modulating moderation: a history of objectionability in Twitter moderation practices

Half a decade ago, social media platforms were widely perceived as modeling themselves after nominally liberal, democratic institutions (Klonick, 2018). At the heart of this view was a newfound opportunity to open the “the floodgates to online expression” [(Keipi et al., 2016, p. 114)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3D3JWMPq&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682217295&usg=AOvVaw27AjmJn-Bmq8zt3wBWQrHM) with affordances that circumvented strongly gatekept “legacy” media networks. One of these affordances was a largely non-interventionist philosophy of content moderation, according to which platforms do not bear the right to adjudicate the moral, political or epistemic validity of user-generated content in order to host the most diverse possible conglomeration of ideas, information and user cultures. But when the same platforms were also accused of opening the floodgates of hate – allowing, for example, the return of ideas, speech and actors long thought to be relegated to the dustbins of history [(Fœssel, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DVEGb4F&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682218631&usg=AOvVaw0Dax3voOkJCOWs76geAdGy) – they were pressured to consider the many ways in which the link between “online speech” and “offline harm” could become “demonstrably real” [(Dorsey, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?vPRPey&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682219283&usg=AOvVaw2Y9Zfmj8Tr9zsOp4Z_huCt).

This question alone invites many different answers, encapsulated within different ideals of social media platforms as public spheres and speech as a public good. Twitter, in particular, went through several approaches to moderate what it called “hateful conduct”, “abusive behavior”, “violent threats” and other more granular forms of verbal abuse. It invested in more human moderators, expanded systems of automated speech control, and had legal experts contribute to content moderation policies for an increasingly complex array of problematic situations. At the core of this process were considerations as to what amounted to objectionable language, a question reflected in ongoing battles of ideas as to what behavior or words constitute offenses to religion, race, gender, and other forms of identity. By adopting some definitions over others, and thereby removing certain content over others, Twitter became an intermediary to comprehensive but highly contentious transformations of public speech norms during a period of considerable political instability. In the process, its speech boundaries as an aspiring digital “public sphere” have broadened, retracted and become altogether more fluid.

The general contours of the modulations of Twitter as an online public sphere are relatively clear. But partly because they involve an opaque and private organization, little scholarship has systematically documented, examined, and theorized the evolution of Twitter’s definitions of objectionable speech. This article contributes to this research by analyzing how Twitter’s conceptions and approach to objectionable speech changed between 2006 and the early moments of Musk’s takeover in December 2022. Methodologically, we look at both concepts and techniques that aim to define and discipline objectionable language linked to what Twitter calls “hateful conduct” and “abusive behavior”. This implies a systematic web history [(Brügger, 2010)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DawfzXw&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682221181&usg=AOvVaw2M06ciB2_DtqO_vxhg4YF_) of several content moderation policies published by the platform[, taking note of what is deemed problematic, specific examples offered for each type of content, and the techniques used against them](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DawfzXw&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682221764&usg=AOvVaw2wsVDKNFnkzI5NtkMrMCpM)[.](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DawfzXw&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682222141&usg=AOvVaw0xX5QpqjnKKaY_ooLN_PO7) [](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DawfzXw&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682222469&usg=AOvVaw0gD7D-zXEd4Q8rPGRSmKbq)We then use a combination of digital methods (Rogers, 2013) that rely on both scraping and Twitter’s Academic API to trace the moderation of objectionable speech in practice. We examine how a dataset of Tweets linked to the U.S. elections of 2020 and online slurs were flagged, removed and (some) eventually “redeemed” after a period of temporary suspension. This means tracing instances of updated, overwritten, removed and “replatformed” content as various “platform effects” [(Malik & Pfeffer, 2016)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?FdjruM&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682222914&usg=AOvVaw2n5Zl_yg6UvXas5mECYmSF).

In reconstructing the evolution of Twitter’s conception and moderation of objectionable speech, we can help explain how different regimes of speech regulation have come into being.  Our analysis suggests that, after abandoning a minimalist stance toward content moderation, the platform was faced with a problem of partiality, having to make essentially political choices over what is objectionable in the context of highly contentious public debates over hate speech and misinformation. In their attempt to keep their function as “platforms” (Gillespie, 2013), i.e. “intermediaries” to most kinds of user-generated content, Twitter aimed to construct a crisis-resistant speech architecture adaptable to the agonistic ways in which speech norms come to be deliberated in public. We term this architecture modulated moderation. At first, a modular moderation meant that Twitter’s policies on hateful, abusive and extreme language bore interchangeable logics from different stakeholders. Then, content moderation techniques themselves began to take a modulated form, in the sense that demotion, flagging and temporary suspensions allowed the platform to conceal content with much more flexibility. Beyond a proportional adjudication of what is objectionable [(Douek, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?broken%3DfPZvmR&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682224262&usg=AOvVaw3YuzEzwHCqQ19FR81cqvdk), what marks this new moderation regime is a normative plasticity, whereby the platform can exert its unilateral power while freely adapting to volatile speech norms.

In what follows, we first discuss current definitions of content moderation and the issues that have shaped it. This includes providing an overview of its history, combined with comments on the literature that has emerged to make sense of it. We will then lay out the methods used to examine Twitter’s moderation of abusive and other problematic behavior in practice, and then close with an analysis of three identified types of constant, variable and emergent objectionability.

# Content moderation and objectionability: history and transformation

Characterized by the personalized entanglement of dataveillance and algorithmic decision-making, platforms’ visibility regimes are designed around two main mechanisms (Magalhães & Yu, 2021). First, sorting and filtering algorithms like recommenders look for an agnostic form of relevance, or what users would want to see (Gillespie, 2014); and second, content moderation engages in the labor-intensive and value-laden determination of objectionability, or what users should or should not see.

Conceptualizations of content moderation often focus on the deliberate, negative and moral nature of this practice. Moderation is used to “prevent abuse” (Grimmelmann, 2015, p. 47) and “intervene” in what is “unacceptable” (Gillespie & Aufderheid, 2021, p. 1); it is a way of “screening” disturbing and concerning materials (Roberts, 2019, p. 33) or information that “harms” and “produces wrongs” (Brunton, 2013, p. xvi). As these excerpts suggest, moderation is also a technique of control intermediaries use to redress what they deem objectionable.

As a practice involving the definition and control of objectionable speech, content moderation is as old as the Internet. From infrastructural components like domain registries and interoperability protocols, to foundational applications like email, the Web is as much a product of what its designers and controllers wanted to be visible as of what they thought should be concealed (Brunton, 2013). Before the emergence of digital platforms as a central online space, this was indirectly acknowledged by discussions on the politics of network topology (Braman, 2011) and made explicit by digital censorship in autocratic regimes (Deibeirt et al, 2008). In liberal West, however, these  architectural “arrangements of power” seemed initially less relevant than the post-factum and more traditional actions of state and supranational institutions (DeNardis, 2012, p. 721). The need to moderate was perhaps more visible in early virtual communities. Relatively small and controlled by users from largely similar cultural backgrounds, these groups deliberated on how to assess and punish the abhorrent behavior of their members (Dibbell, 1993). Outcomes could be controversial but were relatively limited. It makes sense, then, that until recently, content moderation could remain largely invisible (Gillespie, 2018, p. 7).

This began to change during the 2000s and early 2010s with the consolidation of a few American private platforms as social spaces for billions of people, a shift that engendered immensely consequential changes for public speech control. The few works that have attempted to explain this shift seem to agree on a general outline marked by two macro processes: the intensification of platform interventionism and an adjacent complexification of their governance structures (Magalhães & Katzenbach, 2022). These changes can be read as involving a transformation of how objectionability was defined and their moderation justified.

The literature seems almost unanimous in suggesting that platforms had for years a minimalist approach to moderation. These companies, and the people who built them, were partly forged by the libertarian culture of early American Internet culture. Surely, organizations such as Facebook, YouTube and Twitter always imposed some limitations over what could be said by their users. Yet, these limitations were aimed at enabling speech, and in practice relied heavily on users to decide what was objectionable (Crawford & Gillespie, 2014). In Douek’s account (2021), the initial approach of platforms echoed that of the First Amendment of the US Constitution, which entailed a fairly dualistic view of objectionability. Some few expressly defined categories of objectionable speech should be taken down (e.g., inciting violence, child sexual abuse material, copyright violations), while others (including hate speech) could be tolerated in most circumstances. Importing the logics of the First Amendment was also politically convenient: it allowed budding platforms to falsely profess “neutrality” (Douek, 2021, p. 759). After all, platforms claimed to have been merely operationalizing categorizations that had already been consented by courts and, presumably, users in general.

Yet, as platforms succeed in becoming global social structures, the First Amendment-based pretense of impartiality grew unsustainable. Conflicting understandings of what counts as objectionable content increased, as did the social significance of these disputes. Scrutiny intensified as the “free speech” affordances of social media – once thought to foster democratization – were now seen as enabling the “mainstreaming” of extreme ideas and political cultures marginalized, to a certain extent, in past media environments. The election of Donald Trump in the US and Brexit referendum vote in the UK, often framed as shaped by false and hateful content, accelerated this criticism.

Platforms responded by scaling up their moderation structures, deploying ever-more specific content moderation policies and enforcement mechanisms, including deplatforming. This approach became obvious in high-stakes cases, such as the permanent suspension of Trump in early 2021, but was already discernible with Twitter’s participation in a number of anti-extremism inter-governmental initiatives (to which we return below). At the level of content moderation techniques, this shift has been documented by Paßmann, Helmond, and Jansma (2022, p. 16) in their analysis of comment hosting services like Disqus, as well as Magalhães and Katzenbach (2022) in their longitudinal study of copyright structures in fifteen platforms. The latter study demonstrates how, even for a topic that is tightly regulated, companies have consistently concentrated their power by increasing the number of rules that gave them control over moderation.

Interpreting this transformation from a legal perspective, Douek (2021) argues that platform content moderation evolved into systems underpinned by proportionality, typical not of the American legal system but of international human rights law. This paradigm involves a more contextual evaluation of objectionability, whereby several concerns (such as dignity and safety) are adjudicated against speech rights. Rather than playing the “taxonomist", as in the First Amendment tradition, platforms would act like a “grocer” or “epidemiologist”, identifying and weighing “multiple interests” before deciding on what to do (Douek, 2021, p. 78). This approach traditionally privileges the least restrictive punishments, which should be proportionate to violations. In the context of platforms, this has translated into a wider range of granular remedies, from removal to demotion to visual warnings, which could target not only information but also accounts and the functionalities that allow these accounts to define themselves (e.g., verification badges) and make profit (monetization). In Douek’s view, this turn to proportionality is not just what platforms did, but what they should have done (2021, pp. 815-818). As a statutory interpretation principle, proportionality aims to produce fair outcomes – an apparent truism to which we return below.

The abandonment of dichotomic speech categories, the differentiation of remedies, and the very creation of massive private bureaucracies of speech regulation are suggestive of how complex content moderation has become. This increasing complexity has been empirically documented in the mushrooming of policies, norms, and corporate discourse on moderation, as well as the implementation of new technologies (Magalhães & Katzenbach, 2022; Paßmann et al., 2022). Techniques ranging from content hashing to more sophisticated and data-intensive machine learning systems play a central but ambivalent role, as they both address and deepen this complexification (Gillespie, 2020).

From loosely organized actions to intrusive and complex systems, this broad shift in how platforms control speech indicates the contradictory and interrelated needs shaping the development of content moderation. Whilst platforms retain unilateral control over the act of moderation, these companies are best described as occupying the center of a sprawling governance system populated by a constellation of agents (van Dijck et al., 2021). Expectations and formal provisions on content moderation by users, states, politicians, media, civil society, and markets (advertisers, commercial partners, competitors) shape and constrain each other, and in so doing also affect how platforms behave. The ambiguities of Twitter content moderation policy changes may also result from the highly-paid, highly-educated Trust and Safety staffers that have traversed Twitter at different points in time. That these actors have an unequal capacity to affect platforms have fueled fears that moderation can deepen structural injustices, punishing racial, gender, and economic minorities while unfairly protecting powerful actors – including platforms themselves (DeCook et al., 2022). This is a problem worsened by the centrality of US actors in these networks, who, perhaps unwillingly, end up imposing US speech ideologies and  “dislocating” content moderation from the historical and cultural dynamics that form foreign speech norms.

These political difficulties are compounded by platforms’ for-profit nature. Content moderation provides contradictory economic incentives. Speech control systems are costly and sometimes antithetical to the production of data these organizations depend on to target users with advertisements, their main source of revenue. The initially minimalist approach of platforms thrived on this logic. In allowing user expression to be “as free as possible” (Douek, 2021, p. 759), companies incentivised more user-generated content and interactions, thus fostering the datafied sociality that underpins their business model. Yet, as it became clear over time, a poorly moderated environment might put off users and drive away advertisers. Whilst hampering engagement and data creation, the recent intensification of moderation aims to address this point by decreasing harm, taming controversy, and potentially placating legislators.

Finally, the use of AI introduces new biases and logics to decisions that have traditionally been made by direct human judgment (Ananny, 2019). Platforms may admit that mistakes will happen, and then consider which of those are more viable (Douek, 2021). Meanwhile, a common worry is that AI can make these mistakes harder (and perhaps impossible) to understand, justify, and dispute (Gorwa et al., 2020).

Platforms were not designed to adjudicate these contrasting political, economic, and technological demands. The for-profit nature of these companies may partially explain this predicament. But there is also the chasm between platforms' enormous control capacities and their frail legitimacy of these capacities. Power can be said to be wielded legitimately when it is “acquired and exercised according to established rules”, which must be justified by “socially accepted beliefs about (i) the rightful source of authority and (ii) the proper ends and standards of government (normative justifiability)”, and when “positions of authority are confirmed by express consent or affirmation of appropriate subordinates” (Beetham, 2012, p. 123). The initial legitimacy of platforms arguably emerged from how they did not appear to be an “authority”, and thus their actions did not need to be accounted as “rightful” or “proper”. The tenuous justifiability of their power became evident when, over time, this position became untenable. These organizations might be materially able to govern and coordinate people and things with an unprecedented efficacy, but they are not designed to represent users. Their consent to company  rules is widely seen as formal and imposed. And once platforms are understood as illegitimately wielding power, they put themselves in legal and political jeopardy. As we will argue, Twitter’s existential need to regain political legitimacy seems crucial to understand how they transformed their ideas and practices towards objectionable speech.

Beyond what this section summarized, the history of content moderation remains considerably under-researched. Empirical works concern single platforms or topics and typically do not theorize about their findings; Douek’s normative suggestion of a turn to proportionality seems to be one of the only substantive attempts to conceptualize this trajectory. Tracing this history in more detail seems crucial to understand how and when, exactly, the boundaries of online speech have changed. This chapter explores one entry point to this very broad endeavor: how the very conceptualization and remedies against objectionability changed on Twitter. The next section explains the methods we used to answer this problem.

# Digital methods for empirical research on content moderation

Our objective is to trace a history of objectionable speech in Twitter policies intended to moderate violent language or behavior. Going beyond an analysis of public rules, we then want to examine how a subset of Tweets moderated under these policies have been flagged, suspended, demoted and otherwise moderated in practice. These objectives entail three methodological steps: (1) collecting relevant policies and Tweets; (2) analyzing changes in content moderation policies and traces of moderation techniques in a dataset of problematic Tweets; and (3) visualizing policy changes and moderation practices.

From its creation in 2006, Twitter became a platform of choice for both political movements involved in changing language for both progressive and “transgressive” [(Nagle, 2017)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?me1Zrf&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682232632&usg=AOvVaw0P0k4w8P-Msr-Z9x1ILeYe) purposes. While the emergence of extreme political cultures pushed Twitter, policy makers, legislators and other stakeholders to reconsider where to place the limits of online expression, social justice movements have sometimes pressured platforms to limit the circulation of words, phrases, personalities and references to historical events deemed to no longer hold a legitimate place in public debate. Adding in the mix its own dynamics as a private corporation, Twitter underwent several changes in its philosophy of content moderation — the latest of which is Elon Musk’s claim to transparency and pluralism as a response to allegations of partisanship by conservative politicians across the Western hemisphere [(Elliott, 2022)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Qqt8Eb&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682233356&usg=AOvVaw3VagA2mgPEkEvzxS-Ov5sv).

In this context, Twitter has unveiled a myriad of different policies that, over time, exchange clauses, moderation techniques, and conceptions of what counts as objectionable. “Hateful conduct” and “Abusive behavior” have morphed from relatively passive conceptions of “technical harm” (such as spam or “technical abuse”) into various types of social abuse (see Katzenbach et al., 2022, for a list of all kinds of policies). From the Terms of Service and Twitter Rules (Twitter, 2010b, 2022f), three basic types of content branch into a variety of policies specialized in violent content and behavior (violent threats (Twitter, 2022g), sensitive media (Twitter, 2022d), glorification of violence (Twitter, 2022b), spam (“platform manipulation” in Roth & Pickles, 2022; Twitter, 2022c, 2022e) and institutional processes like elections, COVID-19 and conflict misinformation (Twitter, 2022). Some content becomes less tolerable over the years, while others that were deemed unacceptable may now be tolerated under conditions of “reduced visibility”, once called “safety ranking” [(Twitter, 2017](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZEOyal&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682234232&usg=AOvVaw1exWvZme1A9myFkvIpwAAA)[b](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZEOyal&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682234607&usg=AOvVaw3h1AExIiVjwQnjlJr2DyCC)[)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZEOyal&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682234924&usg=AOvVaw0pdz8293A_xqyEOqYpLkdT).

These dynamics make it impossible to disentangle specific policies, such as “hateful conduct”, from tangential ones, such as “spam” and “platform manipulation”. One can select several content moderation policies as possible points of origin of contemporary Twitter conceptions of hateful speech or abusive behavior, and they will find that techniques applied to one type of content will be transferred to another seemingly unrelated one. For this reason, we decided to cast a wide net on our selection of policies, starting from the (old) Terms of Service and Twitter Rules and then following all of their ramifications. Only after obtaining a complete list of policy URLs and manually recording the types of content and moderation techniques mentioned in each page, were we able to filter out the policies we knew bore no influence on hate and abuse-related ones.

As we found that Twitter’s content moderation policies changed their online policy organization around 2018, we began collecting policy pages beginning with the oldest URL available (twitter.com) and used a crawler to find any other internal policy links. In practice, this meant using the Digital Methods Initiative’s Internet Archive Wayback Machine Link Ripper to first obtain daily impressions of twitter.com from 2006 to 2022. Then, we used the Digital Methods Initiative Link Ripper to crawl the internal links of policy-related pages we found in all impressions of twitter.com, including the Terms of Use, the Twitter Rules, and more specific policies such as Hateful Conduct and Abusive Behaviour. By the end of this process, we had a total 20,731 archived links for 31 unique policy pages (see Table 1 and 2 in the Appendix). These pages include specific policies, as well as blog posts announcing new policy updates.

To get an overview of how policies changed, we first counted the amount of times that each of our 20,731 archived pages were edited per day. Edits amount to difflib counts of parts of a page that aren't the same.[[1]] They can be seen in **Figure 1**, which helps us identify and contextualize periods of significant overhauls in Twitter’s content moderation system. Content moderation policies were analyzed by looking at all monthly versions of each of the 34 policy pages we stored. This implied a systematic manual annotation of a policy’s title, subsection(s), definitions of each objectionable content, any listed examples, and each of the moderation techniques assigned to said content. The result is a spreadsheet with records of every listed content and its corresponding reinforcement techniques (see Table 6 in the Appendix). This table allows us to interpret the conceptual evolution of a given type of content, displaying for example how a definition attributed to one kind of behavior may eventually change and be used to describe other content.

Moderation techniques allow us to interpret the severity of each type of content, with some deserving “immediate and permanent suspension” and others being up to users to report. We also see how techniques are progressively applied from one to multiple policies; “visibility reduction” is for example a relatively marginal way to hide hateful content in 2017, but becomes predominant across nearly all policies by 2022 (see **Figure 2**). To ensure that we captured all the reinforcement techniques mentioned in content moderation policies, we automatically counted the amount of times each technique was mentioned (see Table 4 in the Appendix). This implied that we manually read monthly versions of all policies, record every possible expression of moderation techniques, and only then count the times they were mentioned. We abbreviated specific expressions into categories of enforcement techniques; for example, the expression “we may temporarily lock you out of your account” will be abbreviated as “Temporary suspension”. Results can be seen in Figure 2 or Table 5 in the Appendix).

Much of the empirical work on content moderation already relies on community guidelines (see, for example, [Jiang et al., 2020; Maddox & Malson, 2020; Zolides, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?AUs34l&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682239454&usg=AOvVaw2Ll6gvDmDHV7D62ZInuQkt). We contribute to this research by utilizing additional digital methods to explore the practice of moderating such speech — that is, how content moderation techniques were exerted on a given corpus of Tweets. Detecting how Tweets are moderated in practice requires a different methodological recipe, though it also relies on Internet history research methods (Brügger, 2010). To look at how a set of Tweets were moderated implies detecting traces of suspension, labels, demotion or other platform techniques listed in policy documents. Each of these consist of different metadata or traces stored in user interfaces that one could, at the time of writing, collect through Twitter’s APIs or HTML. Tweet labels, for example, are not provided by any of the APIs, but may be scraped from a Tweet’s URL, specified by an ID. This also applies to Tweet penalties, like suspensions. Though one could use Twitter APIs to confirm if a user or Tweet are still active, information returned from scraping is richer because it captures specific disclaimers assigned to moderated content (for example, “This Tweet is from a suspended account”). Finally, Tweet demotions may be examined by scraping the ranking of search results for Tweets, or in Tweet reply threads. These are the two locations where Twitter claims to “reduce the visibility” of problematic content (Twitter, 2017b).

We collected moderation metadata from two datasets of moderated Tweets. The first is a collection of Tweets mentioning #StopTheSteal and #ElectionFraud, dating from late 2020 to early 2021. We first used T-CAT, the Twitter Capture and Analysis Toolkit (Borra & Rieder, 2014), to collect a total of 3,267,201 Tweets dating from November 4 2021 to late January 2021, using the above-mentioned hashtags as queries. We then complemented this dataset with traces of labeling, suspension and demotion scraped in January 2021, January 2022, and December 2022. Prior to Twitter’s acquisition, StopTheSteal Tweets had been moderated under at least three policies: platform manipulation, electoral integrity and glorification of violence. Moderation techniques included a “strike system”, which entailed conditional suspension based on users’ behavior, and labels, which consisted in debunking or “contextualising” “misleading” Tweets with authoritative sources (Twitter, DATE). Because moderation metadata capture is computationally heavy, we limited our dataset to a random 1/1000 sample of Tweets.

Combining original and current-day metadata allows us to effectively “reconstruct” three types of moderation. To retrace Tweet suspensions, we scraped the statuses and labels of all #StopTheSteal Tweets using Selenium, a browser automation tool, during the three above-mentioned intervals. By comparing Tweet statuses over time, we were able to find which Tweets and users had been suspended and which had been allowed back into the platform.

Tweet demotions, or the practice of down-ranking Tweets in search results or newsfeed rankings, can be partly reconstructed by collecting the search ranking of results for a problematic keyword. One can also keep track of engagement numbers such as replies, likes and impressions, since the goal of demotion is to reduce the traction of problematic content. As rankings were not provided by our original T-CAT dataset, we collected the engagement and ranking metadata of an additional 272 Tweets mentioning “StopTheSteal”, every two hours of five consecutive days (December 9 and December 15, 2022), using Selenium. Because this task was computationally intensive, we restricted our results to the top 100 Tweets (see Figure 5 in the Appendix). For a comparison with hateful language, we repeated this task for results containing a slur, “jewish puppet”. We used a hate speech classifier [(Mathew et al., 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?GkbLQ9&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682241489&usg=AOvVaw1dLBdjQ27S54jiZLB1MwPm) to detect levels of offensiveness in each of the Tweets displayed in daily search results. We ensured that classifications were accurate by manually consulting Tweets in the top 30 search results.

## Limitations

Our method bears the limitations of any Web history research [(Arora et al., 2016)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?qEA1oO&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682242808&usg=AOvVaw30KN-5lM3Bc0T9tykeDNYY). For what concerns our study of policy documents, one of these limitations is that the data we obtain is what the Internet Archive has stored. This likely excludes some document versions. Other limitations are due to Twitter’s policies: earlier ones are written in an ambiguous language that do not specify when or how enforcement techniques are applied. And, whilst these policies are crucial normative performances by the platform, their provisions do not necessarily exhaust all the moderation rules and practices used by Twitter. While relying on secondary literature to compensate for gaps of information caused by these three limitations, we also used findings from our empirical study of content moderation practices. The application of demotion, flagging and suspension techniques – including inconsistencies that may not be explained in policy documents – are illustrated in some of our findings (for example, Figures 5 and 6). It is important to reiterate that studies of content moderation practices (in Figures 3a, 3b, 4, 5 and 6 in the Appendix) are only snapshots in time, and might not be representative of how the platform moderates hateful, misleading and other Tweets at all times.

# Constant, variable and emergent objectionability: modulating moderation across three types of objectionable information

We begin our analysis by identifying three, broad types of objectionable content found in our selection of Twitter content moderation policies. The first refers to what we call constant objectionability, or “ugly content”: illegal and otherwise out-of-the-line activities moderated under a zero-tolerance policy. The second refers to content that has undergone significant conceptual and moderation changes. Because it is moderated less severely as the former while still enjoying some legal and public consensus on their objectionability, we call it variable objectionability, or “bad content”. The third type of objectionable content refers to emergent objectionability, i.e., policies that Twitter has devised ex post facto as an attempt to respond to emerging crises. Each type will undergo variable amounts of change but they remain basic conceptions of objectionable content whose limitations have pushed Twitter’s larger philosophy of content moderation to evolve toward what we will later argue is a form of modulated moderation.

## The ugly: violent threats, child exploitation, terrorism and other constantly objectionable content

Over the years, what we refer to as “ugly content” will change very little conceptually. One of the first references to “ugly” content will be made in the Terms of Service, in 2006 as “unlawful, offensive, threatening, libelous, defamatory, obscene or otherwise objectionable or violates any party's intellectual property or these Terms of Use”. In 2010, this vocabulary was used to refer more specifically to sexually obscene content (pornography) and grave crimes, such as child exploitation and the potential to harm someone physically (“violent threats”). The definition given to each of these three types of content will change little, though examples of content not previously listed appear in the “Sensitive media” policy. This includes specific statements — “direct, specific threats of violence against others” — as well as systematically abusive behaviors — “sending messages to a user from multiple accounts”, or “having the sole purpose of your account be to send abusive messages to others” (Twitter, 2013). Another example is extreme violence, including graphic content, or the promotion and recruitment to commit acts of violence, with for example terrorist or “violent organizations”.

Because the infringement of these types of content is against the law in several countries (including the US), it is usually not up to Twitter to punish it on its own terms. It must instead relegate an infringement to local law enforcement, to whom Twitter will typically act as an intermediary. Where no obligation exists for Twitter to collaborate with local authorities, however, this content is dealt with “zero tolerance” measures devised in early policy years. These are “deplatforming” measures (Rogers 2020), which we refer here as a group of techniques that consist in suppressing content or permanently banning users from a platform and facilitating the permanent reprobation of intolerable conduct. The most severe forms of deplatfoming are reserved for extremely vile content, such as child exploitation. All versions include “immediate and permanent suspension” and removing culpable users any possibility of returning to the platform (see **Figure 2**). Other measures devised for violating Tweets include immediate deletion, irrespective of what reasons users may give for posting it (Appendix, Figure 2).

**Figure 2**. *Types of content moderation enforcement applied by each Twitter policy (2006-2022).*

One of the likely reasons why “ugly content” undergoes little conceptual and technical change is because its objectionability is not just for Twitter’s to define. The objectionability of illegal content is stipulated in local or widely shared laws and reinforced by strong social consensus regarding their objectionability. In the case of terrorism, more specifically, Twitter borrows largely from the legal vocabulary of a group of powerful state actors who historically share a strong commitment against jihadism. Along with Facebook, Microsoft, and YouTube, Twitter founded the GIFCT (Global Internet Forum to Counter Terrorism) on July 26, 2017, in the face of the potential harm produced by ISIS content on social media platforms (Borelli, 2021, p. 15). Back-door conversations between Twitter and various governments were consolidated by GIFCT, which provided Twitter with a conceptual blueprint for its “Twitter media policy” and later its “Violent organizations” policy. The organization has transparency reports dating to 2019, at which point it has a taxonomy of terrorist and violent extremist content that included “Imminent Credible Threat”, “Graphic Violence Against Defenseless People”, “Glorification of Terrorist Acts”, “Radicalization, Recruitment, Instruction”, and “New Zealand Perpetrator Content” (GIFCT, 2019). Twitter’s policy of July 2017 includes six similar rules on violent extremism, referring to “identified groups subscribing to the use of violence as a means to advance their cause, whether political, religious, or social” (Twitter, 2017). A later update from 2017 aligns with additional clauses, including “recruiting” or “engaging in or promoting acts for the violent extremist group”.

There are relatively few changes to the rules on terrorist content until 2019, but enforcement mechanisms remain the strictest: immediate and permanent suspension, as well as sanctioning accounts and groups that are affiliated with identified violent extremists. For these policies, deplatforming techniques will rely on a database of cryptographic “fingerprints” of digital content developed by GIFCT and used to automatically detect and prevent users from uploading this kind of objectionable content. To define what content belongs in the database, GIFCT uses the UN’s consolidated sanctions list as grounds for the inclusion of content in its taxonomy (Diaz and Hecht-Fellela, 2021, p. 5).

Engagement in a regulatory institution like GIFCT may allow Twitter to legitimize its content moderation and address representational issues; in this case, it responded to states’ mounting pressure on ISIS content with rules and expertise. But it also comes with political and technical vulnerabilities, as it shows biases in its conception of who is likely to be involved in terrorist attacks. Twitter’s incorporation of counter-terrorist and counter-extremist expertise, for example, comes with a focus on violent jihadism and Islamism to the detriment of far-right extremism in the US and Europe (Meier 2020; Kundnani & Hayes, 2018; Abbas, 2020, 2021). This is partly because of the weight of definitions provided by powerful state actors. As an interviewee from Google quoted by Borelli (2021, p. 12) put it, “If you’re a company, you can look at these lists and say: this group has (…) gone through a process that’s been vetted by a democratic government…when it comes to far-right, governments have been more reluctant to name groups to such a list”. With European and American extremist groups operating more as a complex cultural scene (Conway 2020), moderation involves applying these rules to a relatively large segment of users, including elected representatives. Using this counter-extremism logic to bind categories of content to “terrorism” and “violent extremism”, Twitter opts to create another policy stream (“hateful conduct”) to manage enforcement of its rules in the face of the greater representational power of the far-right.

## The bad: hate speech, abusive behavior and the rise of variable objectionability

The conceptual evolution of “bad content” goes from two extremes: a period of relative abstentionism when Twitter neither intervened nor clearly defined what counts as hateful content, to one where it would progressively abide to social pressures to limit the potential harm of such content on the ground (such as incitement to violence) and in the lived experiences of its users (such as discrimination).

In a period when Twitter described itself as an “aggressively open company” (Twitter, 2010a), this initially abstentionist approach consisted in framing user-generated content as users’ property (users “own” what they “share” –Twitter, 2010a), which implied that moderation was in most cases outsourced to the user itself, based on their own definition (and experience) of hateful or abusive conduct. The abusive behavior policy, for example, would state in 2014 that whatever the “voices, ideas and perspectives” involved in “user disputes and false statements”, a platform policy cannot “media content or intervene in disputes between users” [(Twitter, 2014)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?MXtFcQ&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682250811&usg=AOvVaw1PcZuUVlveBWZWBKNeSyj3). Users are in fact “allowed to post […] potentially inflammatory content, provided they do not violate the Twitter Rules and Terms of Service.” ([Twitter, 2014](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?MXtFcQ&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682251262&usg=AOvVaw2ntAaqhcgX9gM7oRfPk69Y)).

Still, the level of tolerance for specific kinds of hateful statements lowers in 2018, when, despite maintaining reporting as its primary method of capture of hateful conduct Tweets, the hateful conduct also mentions Tweet deletion — a measure that was until then only applied to sensitive media (gore, pornography), violent threats and terrorism. That year, Twitter removed a single clause to “ask users” to remove violating Tweets, adding a few more enforcement techniques preceding a possible suspension. Hateful imagery, references to mass murders, violent threats, inciting fear about a protected category and repeated non-consensual slurs are all first asked to be removed; then, accounts caught employing them may only use Twitter in read-only mode; and finally, these accounts may be permanently suspended ([Twitter, 2018](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682252233&usg=AOvVaw2uD3C2_R2aNAxThGxbC7u_)[; see also](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682252648&usg=AOvVaw2kIRCP0YPjjZiCD12HC9IQ) [Figure 2](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682253009&usg=AOvVaw1cde9bvfzq8MiBS8VGaVeq) [in the](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682253394&usg=AOvVaw1ClpO5xjUlTWaD7KfNI0FL) [Appendix](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682253784&usg=AOvVaw1OoEQPbk6_pEnSOoZHQG5z)[)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ZviEQB&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682254140&usg=AOvVaw1yGKUK8WVAeexUcyRlCXdp). Adding to previous developments, this change of moderation measures signals how, eventually, some hateful and abusive Tweets and users may reach a level of intolerance similar to constant extremes, like sensitive media and terrorism, which are typical of “ugly content”.

The loss of tolerance for hateful and abusive content seems to respond to a number of events on the ground. A few months after the 2017 “Unite the Right” rally in Charlottesville, VA, Twitter (like other social media platforms) began to take such content more seriously in no small part due to calls from journalists, activists and politicians on platforms to address the extreme speech from far-right actors rife on the platform. The effect of these events begin to suggest a certain causal connection between online content and offline events. There is also pressure from social justice movements and activists to include provisions against particular experiences of verbal abuse [(Levin, 2017)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?KT3cKX&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682255369&usg=AOvVaw27ZeDAtwMZUHe5TnBVGb5B). In 2018, for example, Twitter decided to sanction the “targeted misgendering or deadnaming of transgender individuals” (Twitter, 2018). This prompted further specification from a provision of 2016, when Twitter included “gender” and “gender identity” to its list of potential victims of hate conduct (Twitter, 2016a).

The seeming ad-hoc fashion with which Twitter would edit its hateful conduct policies has proven to be one of the most politically controversial aspects of their moderation rules. The suspension of certain high-profile users and creation of clauses that protect marginalized identities attracted the criticism of politicians, users, and relevant stakeholders, who began to perceive Twitter and other Big Tech companies as partial, if not partisan [(Ghaffary, 2022)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?V9FILu&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682256567&usg=AOvVaw2mZjyLQ8KinSNq3QNDrWDN). While these voices could be ignored as marginal or extreme, other analyses may also identify larger, structural biases in Twitter’s hateful conduct policy. Consider how definitions of hateful conduct compare to those of terrorism or “violent organizations''. Aside from an update on hateful “logos, symbols, or images'' in February 2017, the first modification of Twitter’s Rules in December 2017 characterizes hate as a discrete category, different from violent extremism (see **Figure 1**). More specifically, it splits violence — which includes “threatening or promoting terrorism” and “affiliating oneself with organizations that use or promote violence against civilians — from “hateful imagery and display names” and “[h]ateful conduct, which refers to “promoting violence against, [threatening], or harassing other people” on the basis of protected characteristics. While both classifications mention violence, the “Violence” category refers to specific terms (“terrorism”, “organized groups”) that indicate the primacy of the listing regimes and logics of the “countering violent extremism” expertise commented above. In this sense, Twitter relies on the external logic of GIFCT to define “terrorism and violent extremism” as constantly objectionable (ugly content), while using “hateful conduct” as a variably objectionable category (bad content).

**Figure 1**. *Number of edits of Twitter content moderation policies (2006-2022).*

In addition to the problem of partiality, a number of institutional threats loom: the U.S. elections of 2020; the threat of platform manipulation (including coordinated inauthentic behavior); and misleading claims about the transmission, prevention and treatment of COVID-19, which challenged the medical authority of local health organizations. On the one hand, these institutional threats push the platform to broaden its repertoire of prohibited content (see Figure 1 in the Appendix). The Civic Integrity Policy from 2019, designed to address US electoral false information, is one such case: misleading claims, threats or inaccurate statements about elected officials, voting booths or any other circumstances linked to voting are immediately removed, pending the permanent suspension of users. On the other hand, it also encourages the platform to consider moderation techniques more attuned to the contingent ways in which content may become objectionable in the eyes of users and stakeholders, as explained in the next section.

## Bad today, good tomorrow? Elections, pandemics and other emergent forms of objectionability

Three proposed solutions, already in circulation in 2017 [(Twitter, 2017)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?dDCkkr&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682260008&usg=AOvVaw0aP7uxbThR0sW-WZ7bfk6r), are “a strike system” in place of unspecified or permanent suspension; demotion and reduce the visibility (or reach) of problematic Tweets; and various “context-inducing” labels. Labeling provides a possibility to offer nuance or context to Tweets that make sweeping statements about partial information, and was widely used in policies against “misleading” information, such as COVID-19 misleading information [(Twitter, 2020)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?qPkRi6&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682260904&usg=AOvVaw2bnF9WHXPRQfASC8dDaVWb) and Electoral Integrity [(Twitter, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?3d1yXg&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682261745&usg=AOvVaw3p_1Flz5ll8FbKxLRxnPee). The strike system allows the platform to place users under a conditional form of suspension regime, where, after one, two, or three infringements, they may be subject to temporary or permanent suspension. If the infringement is not too severe, temporary suspensions open a small pathway to redeem users back into the platform. Demotion techniques allow platforms to disincentive potentially problematic content not just before they become influential, but based on public opinion on their objectionability. The rankings of search results and replies may be determined by how acceptable a given content is in a time and place: should it become problematic, it will go down the rankings; should it become normal or even commendable tomorrow, it will go up.

An analysis of Twitter’s moderation of Tweets linked to the #StopTheSteal campaign of 2020 illustrates this new dynamic. Looking, for example, at the statuses of Tweets published between November 2020 and January 2021, we find that a majority of Tweets were either labeled or initially suspended (**Figure 3a and 3b**). As per the election or civil integrity policy outlined in April and September of 2019, such Tweets were likely subjected to a range of “soft” correctives, particularly labels intending to give nuance or “context” to inflated, hyper-partisan and other misleading claims about the US elections. Part of this strategy was to give “authoritative sources” enough visibility to counter misleading claims, either with labels automatically assigned to any Tweet mentioning a problematic keyword (StopTheSteal being one of them) [(Michael Shellenberger [@ShellenbergerMD], 2022)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?sYurlE&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682263211&usg=AOvVaw0JxSZeLDU9SOrq_8mk-7q5), or through demotion techniques in search results and user feeds [(Joanna Geary, 2021)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?IWTW0g&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682263852&usg=AOvVaw20MwrNWhm2MdWz46GKTf2_).

By January 2022, however, we see that a minority of suspended Tweets were allowed back into the platform. Some of these then chose to make their profiles private – perhaps for fear of another retribution – while others were suspended again; but most had at this point lost about half of their original engagement numbers (**Figure 4**). These results may suggest that Twitter’s more nuanced content moderation regime was not concerned with acting on time to delete violating content, but capturing a form of potential or latent objectionability: for example, the probability that physical altercation may ensue from complex and oftentimes undistinguished indicators of social animosity (as it did). In the absence of clear indicators of objectionability, Twitter’s moderation apparatus did not judge it reasonable to suppress all contents tied to election disputes and interference, but opted instead to give nuance to misleading Tweets, and allow some infringing users to return to the platform through its strike system’s pathways to redemption.

Another evidence of modulation is Twitter’s increasing reliance on “visibility reduction” mechanisms, commonly known as demotion or reduction techniques [(Gillespie, 2022)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?gOYhN4&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682265001&usg=AOvVaw2bQbcEhkkswAwJ-pEh8St8). Above, we have described how demotion typically functions to downrank problematic sources and uprank authoritative ones, though other examples have been looked at in more detail by Gillespie (2022). Given its application on the hateful conduct policy (amongst others), we have taken as an example Twitter search results for one specific hateful keyword, obtained between December 12 and 15, 2022. In **Figure 5**, we see that Tweets classified as offensive were not amongst the top rankings. Instead, top rows were given to Tweets that consisted in debunking a popular conspiratorial rhetoric around that given keyword. If or when offensive results are on top, they drop positions in a number of hours. These dynamics are less indicative of an attempt to permanently keep problematic content away from users’ reach (a metric formalized as “impressions” [(Elon Musk [@elonmusk], 2022)](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?qzeQhJ&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1707915682265956&usg=AOvVaw3HJdz6EFIGl50dqXwiSR7l), then letting them fluctuate downwards until they may begin to become acceptable.

# Modulated moderation

Underpinning the transformations we described above is a process whereby Twitter progressively became both more active and more subtle in its approach to objectionable speech. The resulting content moderation regime is marked by a dual modulation, we argue, which expands what we mean by complexification of content moderation but challenges assumptions on the alleged proportional approach to speech control. On one level, a modulated moderation means that Twitter’s approach to speech control undergoes a process of differentiation. It is not that there has been a proliferation of categories of what clearly counts as objectionable or non-objectionable, and that such proliferation turned a First Amendment’s approach impossible. Changes are more nuanced. There is a differentiation of how speech is perceived in terms of levels of harm. This is of course suggested by the emergence of forms of variably objectionable content, whose definition (and gravity) changes in time, as do the techniques used to reprimand them. The inconsistent ways in which such content is defined by both Twitter and the stakeholders it involves in its internal policy-making processes (e.g.,, the GIFCT) made Twitter’s problem of partiality more explicit. Setting aside “ugly” content – whose objectionability already enjoys stable legal and public consensus – Twitter was constrained to define “bad” from “good” or acceptable content that will fail to satisfy all of the various, contradictory norms of its stakeholders and users. This will threaten the nature of platforms as consolidators of very large amounts of information – a function that implies at its basis the consolidation of noise, difference and contradiction.

A seeming solution to the problem of partiality involved factoring in the contingent ways in which content is defined as objectionable by its stakeholder and users – the second way in which moderation was modulated. The moral quality of user-generated content emerges from its relationship with a shifting reality: how other individuals make sense of this content, how they estimate its harm, and on the basis of which legal and political traditions. Objectionable speech becomes from the platform perspective a potential category: techniques like demotion, labeling or forms of suspension moderate content based on the degree to which it is objectionable in a given time and place, “not only what any one user is likely to see but also what society is likely to attend to, take seriously, struggle with, and value” (Gillespie 2022). Under strike systems, some users involved in disseminating the StopTheSteal hashtag in 2020 were initially suspended and then “forgiven” after a period of suspension. Under its demotion systems, this very same hashtag has now been whitelisted and ranks on top of search results. Twitter content moderation techniques cease to adjudicate objectionability through a clear-cut “allow” or “do not allow” dichotomy, and function instead to moderate content based on their relative, public objectionability. Since the very notion of objectionability is defined as always in flux, the platform’s regime becomes more adaptable to sudden changes in public speech norms.

Importantly, these two forms of modulation are contradictory. The first suggests that there is, indeed, something unique about content that can lead the platform to moderate it, even if using a much more varied number of tools. The second suggests that objectionability and content should be understood as inherently disconnected, and the evaluation of their association is always contextual. This contradiction indicates how unlikely is the idea that Twitter truly changed how it understands the normative nature of objectionability. There is no evidence that Twitter as a company was suddenly convinced that some forms of speech should not be tolerated. Neither is there evidence that the platform unexpectedly decided to adopt the sort of utilitarian view of the relationship between language and ethics that the second modulation would be based on. In fact, what the contradiction between those different forms of modulation suggests is more likely that platforms commodify even moderation, in the sense that objectionability becomes a customer and stakeholder variable – not a criteria – to calculate how and when a given user or Tweet should be moderated.

While a modulated moderation does entail much more attention to speech control, it does not necessarily entail a harsher form of moderation. It seems more like a compromise between the enormous social demands for moderation and the fear from the platform of being perceived not as a “platform” but as a partial and even partisan gatekeeper. The adoption of techniques like demotion, labeling, and different forms of suspension consists of dynamic techniques that do not establish strict lines between acceptable and intolerable content. A Tweet, if perceived by users or stakeholders as problematic today, will be downranked and invisible in search and reply rankings; if acceptable tomorrow, will be raised up and visible. Whether these contents are really objectionable does not necessarily matter. In this sense, the modulation of moderation cannot be explained by proportionality, whose end goal can only be normative. 

One can presume how such “normative plasticity” – a rather flexible approach to define and police harmful content – can be useful for Twitter. Politically, it creates a sort of middle-ground arrangement, whereby the company tries to define itself as neither a neutral platform nor as a censor. Since the documents we read are explicitly about objectionable speech, any assumption about the economic consequences of this transformation can only be indirectly assessed. It is evident, though, that nuanced enforcement mechanisms also render moderation more amenable to Twitter’s business model. Not only does content continue to circulate – generating potential interactions and data – but users might have fewer incentives to migrate to other platforms with more suited content moderation philosophies.

This may invite a reconsideration of content moderation as not just a form of adjudication between multiple views of what counts as objectionable but a form of modulating the very meaning of objectionability. Such sociomaterial and strongly institutionalized practice is shaped by a built-in representational failure created by platforms’ problem of partiality, i.e. its lack of  legitimacy to arbitrate complex and contradictory speech norms. In this sense, modulated moderation is more a  political than a moral art, aimed at building a pragmatic, crisis-resistant speech architecture that is able to withstand external shocks resulting from the unpredictable ways in which speech becomes unacceptable.
