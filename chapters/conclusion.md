# Conclusion

One of the recurring observations throughout this thesis has been that controversies over moderation rarely remain controversies about moderation alone. Whether the object of dispute is extremist speech, immigration, election integrity, COVID-19 treatments, political violence, or misinformation, conflicts initially framed as disagreements over content frequently develop into disagreements over the means and norms of moderation itself [@chongCompetingNormsFree2018b]. What begins as a disagreement over speech becomes a disagreement over the norms through which speech is governed. This observation provides a useful point from which to return to the central question posed at the beginning of the thesis: what is moderation, actually? 

While it has increasingly become conflated with censorship in both public discourse and scholarly debate, [Chapter 1](Manuscript/Chapter%201.%20Censorship%20and%20moderation.md) argued that the two concepts stem from different intellectual traditions and emerged as responses to different political and communicative problems. Through a distant reading of scholarship on moderation and censorship across five languages, I showed that censorship is most commonly theorised as an ensemble of practices exercised by actors possessing asymmetrical means of information management [cit]. Whether these actors are states, churches, editors, bureaucracies, publishers, or communication infrastructures, censorship generally presupposes an environment in which information can be intercepted, reviewed, modified, or prohibited before circulation [@postCensorshipSilencingPractices1998]. Historically, it belongs to worlds in which publication remains relatively centralised and where access to public communication is unevenly distributed [@darntonCensorsWorkHow2015].

Moderation emerges from a different problem. Historically associated with restraint, temperance, mediation, and the management of debate [@lrabinowitzFindingModerationPlatos2023; @cthuderozModerationTout], moderation is less concerned with preventing communication than with maintaining communicative order under conditions in which exclusion alone becomes insufficient. If censorship addresses the problem of controlling publication, moderation addresses the problem of governing communication once publication becomes widely accessible. This distinction becomes particularly relevant in nominally open and pluralistic environments where speech cannot simply be prevented from circulating and where a central normative question becomes: how does one censor without censoring [@bourdieuLangagePouvoirSymbolique2001; @jansensuecurryCensorshipKnotThat1988]? 

Understanding moderation in this way requires moving beyond its most visible manifestations. The empirical material examined throughout this thesis suggests that removal constitutes only one element within a considerably broader repertoire of speech governance techniques. Initially, *content* moderation emerges not only as a means of limiting communication but also as a means of maintaining the conditions under which communication is possible in plurivocal environments, to name online forums [@edwardsMODERATOREMERGINGDEMOCRATIC; @wrightDemocracyDeliberationDesign2007b]. The task of the moderator, as highlighted later by Grimmelmann [@grimmelmannVirtuesModeration2015], depends on ensuring what I call "socio-information coherence": the epistemic conditions that enable participants to understand and exchange with one another. A fundamental feature to maintaining the legitimacy of the moderator as such is *metamoderation*, i.e., the moderation of debates around the norms on which are based moderation rules; the norms of engagement. As platforms centralised many of these affordances into the hands of their trust and safety teams, the legitimacy of moderation as such suffered from a series of crises about how and why platforms moderate they way they do.

On the hand, moderation is also a *restraint* mechanism. Owing to its philosophical origins, it suggests a measure to restrict or reduce "excesses" — however defined in time and place — in a given threshold of tolerance. Moderation is known as such through marginalisation, obfuscation, suspension or embargoing techniques, all of which seek to minimise the removal of information as a first resort. These techniques are "censorial" to the extent that they also engage in correcting, editing, modifying and eventual removing information based on fundamentally normative criteria. But in its history online, we see that they are often combined with the practice of moderation as a debate management mechanism, far from adjudication alone. 

This insight provides a useful bridge to [the methodological contribution of the thesis](Chapter%202.%20After%20deplatforming.md). If moderation operates not only through visible interventions but also through the broader organisation of communicative environments, studying it becomes considerably more difficult than simply documenting removed content or platform policies. One of the central challenges confronting moderation research is that moderation frequently leaves behind traces rather than direct evidence: posts disappear; visibility changes; recommendations shift; accounts become more difficult to discover; search results are reordered. The intervention itself often remains inaccessible, while its effects become visible only retrospectively.

[Chapter 2. After deplatforming](Chapter%202.%20After%20deplatforming.md) approached this problem by drawing inspiration from traditions that confronted similar challenges long before the emergence of digital platforms. Scholars of censorship [@darntonCensorshipComparativePerspective1994; @mooreCensorship2016a; @postCensorshipSilencingPractices1998], publishing history and literary editing [@holquistIntroductionCorruptOriginals1994] have frequently reconstructed intervention through its *traces*. This involved documenting processes modifications, omissions, substitutions, or absences altogether. Such approaches begin from the recognition that interventions into communication frequently reveal themselves retrospectively through their consequences rather than through direct observation. Building on this tradition, Rogers and I proposed a framework for reconstructing what we termed the "scene of disappearance" of moderated content. The chapter treats moderation as a "platform effect" distributed across policies, infrastructures, enforcement mechanisms, user adaptations, visibility systems, and patterns of circulation. Understood as such, a broader range of empirical material becomes relevant:  policy archives, visibility changes, recommendation systems, user responses, evasive vocabularies, migration patterns, alternative platforms, and emerging speech cultures all become traces through which moderation can be studied.

This perspective proved particularly productive when examining the relationship between moderation and speech affordances in [Part II](Part%20II.%20Speech%20affordances.md). The concept of speech affordances was introduced to capture the ways in which platforms shape possibilities for expression through the interaction of policies, technical systems, and user cultures. While affordance theory has often focused on what technologies may or may not allow users to do [@bucherAffordancesSocialMedia2018], the chapters in Part II approached affordances as *constitutive* of platforms themselves [@gillespietarletonCustodiansInternet2018]. The question is not simply what platforms enable, but what forms of communication become sayable under different moderation regimes and what speech cultures develop from (or in spite) of it.

One consequence of this approach is that moderation is no longer understood solely in relation to *individual* platforms, but networks of platforms that become interdependent within a market of more or less allowed speech. This observation emerges in [Chapter 3](Chapter%203.%20The%20affordances%20of%20extreme%20speech.md) and [Chapter 4](Chapter%204.%20The%20affordances%20of%20replacement%20narratives.md), which examined how the moderation of ambiguous online content formed various interdependencies between platforms with more or less permissible moderation policies. In this context, moderate speech undergoes processes of displacement and transformation. Messages acquire euphemistic forms, are translated into humour, irony, symbolism, or ambiguity. They migrate between platforms possessing different moderation regimes and different thresholds of acceptability. As a measure for the containment of undesired culture, this phenomenon of "displacement" shows how moderation as removal seldom erases (and thus reforms) a problematic idea, but scaffolds it across various "subterfuges" [@holquistIntroductionCorruptOriginals1994] that afford its different expressions.  

The same observation emerged from the analysis of large language models in [Chapter 5](Chapter%205.%20LLMs%20and%20the%20generation%20of%20moderate%20speech.md). Although, thus far, AI alignment tends to be treated as a separate field of inquiry, many of its underlying questions concern speech governance. Here, the question of speech affordances asks that we study how speech moderation is operationalised through the relatively different architecture that is alignment. AI companies operationalise different notions of "harm" as risk, controversiality, lack of neutrality, autonomy, biological and nuclear catastrophes, and so on. These risks are mitigated through different ways of cleaning training datasets; different reinforcement learning procedures; red-teaming exercises; benchmarking; and so on [@gabrielArtificialIntelligenceValues2020]. Here, I set out to examine what specific discursive techniques emerge from different models when they are prompted by a moderation trigger from a space with different normative precepts: controversial questions from Reddit. Examining model responses to controversial Reddit prompts revealed significant variation in how different systems respond to contested speech, and such different reflect age-old tensions found in social media platform governance. 

The moderation crises examined throughout [Part III](Part%20III.%20Moderation%20crises.md) reveal that the challenge facing platforms is not simply one of enforcement, but the the management of disagreement over speech norms. Categories such as hate speech, misinformation, incitement, harassment, or discrimination are operationalised through policies, enforcement procedures, and technical systems tend to presume a degree of stability in how those categories are understood. Yet,  the empirical material examined here reflects how the meanings attached to these categories remain contingent and politically contested.

[Chapter 6](Chapter%206.%20Moderation%20in%20crisis.md) approached this problem through the concept of moderation crisis. The chapter argued that moderation crises emerge when the assumptions underpinning moderation cease to command sufficient legitimacy amongst the populations subjected to them. This is not simply because users object to moderation decisions, but because moderation has always, in a sense, generated disagreement. What constitutes acceptable content evolve over time and remain subject to continuous renegotiation in user debates. Moderation therefore becomes implicated in disputes over language itself:  what one actor understands as the enforcement of anti-discriminatory norms may be interpreted by another as political partiality. These disagreements cannot be reduced to factual disputes, but concern competing understandings of the normative frameworks through which public communication ought to be organised. Viewed from this perspective, moderation performs a role that extends beyond content governance alone. It participates in broader societal processes through which communities negotiate what can be said, how and under what conditions particular forms of expression become unacceptable historically. 

[Chapter 7](Chapter%207.%20Of%20migrants%20and%20strangers.md) demonstrated how these tensions emerge across different media environments. The refugee crisis provided a particularly useful case because it brought into confrontation several competing understandings of public communication. Across both social media and broadcast media, debates surrounding migration became increasingly difficult to conduct because participants no longer agreed on the normative vocabulary through which those debates should occur. Disagreements over immigration frequently became disagreements over what xenophobia meant;  disagreements over integration became disagreements over racism; and discussions concerning cultural conflict became entangled with competing understandings of discrimination, tolerance, and inclusion. One consequence of this process was that accusations of media bias became increasingly common. Yet, the analysis suggested that they reflected disagreement over more fundamental assumptions embedded within journalistic editorial norms. Editorial guidelines designed to minimise discriminatory representations of migrants were interpreted by some audiences as evidence of ideological commitment, while, from the perspective of journalists, such decisions appeared as professional obligations. The resulting conflict was therefore not simply epistemics, but concerned competing understandings of what responsible communication should look like.

This observation connects closely to the broader argument developed throughout [Part IV](Part%20IV.%20Ruptures.md). Fragmentation frequently emerges not because actors disagree about facts, but because they disagree about the normative frameworks through which facts acquire meaning. The COVID-19 pandemic explored in Chapter 8 reveals a related but distinct dynamic. Unlike the refugee debates, where disagreement concerned the interpretation of social norms, the pandemic generated situations in which authoritative institutions themselves struggled to maintain consensus. Public-health agencies, governments, scientific organisations, and medical experts frequently disagreed regarding treatments, transmission mechanisms, preventative measures, and public-health interventions. These disagreements evolved rapidly as new information emerged, while platforms were expected to moderate communication according to authoritative guidance. This expectation placed platforms in the unusual position to adjudicate between competing claims to authority. This inadvertently transformed them into "accidental authorities", inheriting responsibilities that traditionally belonged to institutions operating outside the platform ecosystem, and exercising a judgement for which neither their infrastructures nor their legitimacy claims were originally designed.

The implications of this process become even clearer in [Chapter 9](Chapter%209.%20Normative%20dislocation.md). The concept of normative dislocation was introduced to describe situations in which moderation systems developed within one political and historical context are applied to another. Around the 2022 election, moderation was frequently framed through the language of misinformation, electoral integrity, and democratic resilience. Yet these categories entered a political environment shaped by a different historical trajectory marked by unresolved tensions surrounding the historical memory of military dictatorship, redemocratisation and transitional (or retributive) justice processes. Here, moderation became inseparable from questions of historical memory: decisions concerning what information should circulate became decisions concerning which interpretations of the past remain publicly legitimate, and how to mediate them as they erupted into profound political polarisation. 

As mentioned earlier, the chapters gathered under [Part III](Part%20III.%20Moderation%20crises.md) suggest that moderation crises are ultimately crises of consensus concerning the institutions, procedures, and authorities through which disagreement can be managed. The consequences of this instability become visible in [Part IV](Part%20IV.%20Ruptures.md). "Platform ruptures" emerge as a reorganisation of communication spaces after profound disagreements over moderation. Actors cease to recognise the legitimacy of the communicative environments from which they were excluded and begin constructing alternative ones. 

[Chapter 10](Chapter%2010.%20Platform%20ruptures%20and%20the%20formation%20of%20online%20counter%20spheres.md) approached this process through the formation of online "counter-spheres". Drawing on traditions of counterpublic theory [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990], the chapter argued that alternative communicative environments frequently emerge where actors perceive existing publics as incapable of accommodating their concerns, experiences, or interpretations of events. While such environments are often described as part of a process of fragmentation or polarisation, one could also seem them as spaces of production that create new, complementary interdependencies with the platforms that once rejected them. What emerges are speech markets that may well be characterised by different affordances, moderation regimes and thresholds of acceptability, but which nevertheless remain interconnected by their respective limitations. This becomes particularly apparent in [Chapter 11](Chapter%2011.%20Epistemic%20ruptures%20in%20the%20encyclopedias%20of%20alternative%20facts.md)’s examination of alternative encyclopaedias. Projects such as Metapedia and Infogalactic sought to replatform alternative epistemic principles under the exact same platformed logic as Wikipedia's. This observation is reinforced by [Chapter 12](Chapter%2012.%20Deplatforming,%20demotion%20and%20folk%20theories%20of%20Big%20Tech%20persecution.md), where the empirical evidence suggests that deplatforming rarely severs connections but it reorganises them into new platform uses.

If many of the crises and ruptures described throughout the preceding chapters emerge because platforms have become increasingly involved in adjudicating disputes over public norms, the question therefore becomes how moderation can operate under conditions where consensus can no longer be assumed. Chapters 13 and 14 suggest that part of the answer lies in a gradual transformation of the logic of moderation itself. Throughout much of the past decade, moderation has frequently been organised around platforms identifying objectionable content, determine whether it violates platform rules, and intervene accordingly. While the sophistication of the technical systems involved has increased considerably, one of the limitations of this model is that it presumes a relatively stable understanding of objectionability. 

[Chapter 13](Chapter%2013.%20%20Modulating%20moderation.md) explored how Twitter responded to this challenge through what my co-authors and I described as "modulated moderation" that, rather than treating objectionability as a fixed property of content, attunes its interventions based on how objectionable a type of content may be in a time and place. Demotion occupies a particularly important place within this framework because it allows platforms to intervene relative agnostically, i.e., relative to dynamic and contradictory public perceptions of objectionability. In turn, [Chapter 14](Chapter%2014.%20From%20Twitter%20to%20X.md) maps this shift as part of a broader transformation in platform moderation philosophies. Community Notes arguably emerges from a recognition that moderation disputes concern the consensus and legitimacy of those responsible for evaluating it. Community Notes attempts to identify agreement across groups that would ordinarily disagree with one another, attempting to moderate based on whatever consensus is generated amongst users.

In this respect, Community Notes can be understood as one example of a broader family of moderation techniques that seek to distribute the work of judgement across publics rather than concentrating it within platforms. Such systems remain experimental and imperfect, produce exclusions of their own and remain vulnerable to strategic behaviour and manipulation. Nevertheless, they return to parts of the history of content moderation itself. One of the arguments developed in Chapter 1 was that the purpose of moderation was to ensure that disagreement remained possible without collapsing the conditions of exchange altogether. While contemporary platforms differ enormously from the communicative environments in which these earlier understandings emerged, its underlying problem returns: how can communication continue amongst actors who disagree not only about political questions but also about the terms through which those questions should be discussed?

The developments examined in Chapters 13 and 14 do not provide a definitive answer to this question, but indicate that moderation may be gradually returning to a debate management system. This shift is particularly visible in the emergence of bridging systems [cit], distributed evaluation mechanisms [cit], and other experiments designed to facilitate contact between actors occupying different normative environments. They invite a reconsideration of moderation as a public-interest infrastructure to maintain communicative relations between groups that may otherwise possess few opportunities for intelligible dialogue. Throughout the empirical material examined here, ruptured publics rarely ceased orienting themselves towards one another. The persistence of these relations suggests that platform ruptures do not eliminate them, but if anything, reveal how difficult it is to escape them. This observation raises the possibility that some of the developments often described as fragmentation may simultaneously be understood as a reorganisation of contact between political cultures that previously interacted under very different conditions.

Indeed, one alternative reading of the period examined throughout this thesis is that it represents not only a crisis of public communication, but indeed an expansion of it. Debates concerning immigration, national identity, political violence, historical memory, discrimination, race, religion, and democratic legitimacy long predate social media — what changed was not the existence of disagreement, but its visibility. Positions that had previously remained marginal, geographically dispersed, institutionally excluded, or culturally stigmatised acquire new communicative environments. Of course, this does not imply that all forms of speech deserve equal legitimacy; some communicative conflicts do indeed produce harms. Yet, it would be equally mistaken to interpret every rupture solely as a pathology. In some cases, it may reveal disagreements that had never been resolved in the first place.

Such confrontations are indeed uncomfortable, but create opportunities for forms of exchange that invite for the continuousing transform of moderation as the practice of making dialogue possible. This does not mean it is easy, that  all forms of dialogue are desirable, or that one should forget about the important objections made to ideas of deliberative democracy [cit]. The history of political thought contains many reasons to be sceptical of naïve assumptions concerning rational consensus. Yet, they do not convince that dialogue is impossible — simply that it ought to be innovated under every new circumstance in which communication media are transformed. Indeed, the challenge facing contemporary platform governance is that the communicative environments under consideration differ substantially from those for which earlier theories of dialogue were developed. Scale, automation, algorithmic curation, recommendation systems, generative AI, and transnational communication create conditions that historical traditions of moderation rarely had to confront. 

Part of this transformation is already visible through research on so-called "prosocial media" [cit], bridging systems, deliberative technologies and collective sensemaking — topics still relatively separate from platform governance, at least academically. But new initiatives do explore how principles drawn from dialogue studies and conflict resolution might inform technical design, and organisations working on social cohesion begin to engage directly with questions of platform architecture. This involves scholars of deliberative democracy, peace studies, communication, and computer science that accept common responsibilities, and push for regulators to reconsider the assumptions underpinning contemporary approaches to platform governance. Whether such efforts will succeed, or what "success" means here, remains uncertain. Yet, the question they pose is likely to remain one of the defining questions of contemporary communication in massive plural environments: not how disagreement may be "resolved", but under what conditions and norms they may engage in sustainable dialogue. Moderation, on this account, does not adjudicate the quarrel; it keeps the quarrel from collapsing the space in which it occurs.
