# Conclusion

One of the recurring observations throughout this thesis has been that controversies over moderation rarely remain controversies about moderation alone. Whether the object of dispute is extremist speech, immigration, election integrity, COVID-19 treatments, political violence, or misinformation, conflicts initially framed as disagreements over content frequently develop into disagreements over authority. Actors cease arguing only about whether a particular claim is true, harmful, discriminatory, misleading, or objectionable and begin arguing instead about who possesses the legitimacy to make such determinations in the first place. What begins as a disagreement over speech becomes a disagreement over the norms through which speech is governed.

This observation provides a useful point from which to return to the central question posed at the beginning of the thesis: what is moderation? While moderation has increasingly become conflated with censorship in both public discourse and scholarly debate, Chapter 1 argued that the two concepts stem from different intellectual traditions and emerged as responses to different political and communicative problems. Through a distant reading of scholarship on moderation and censorship across five languages, I showed that censorship is most commonly theorised as an ensemble of practices exercised by actors possessing asymmetrical means of information management. Whether these actors are states, churches, editors, bureaucracies, publishers, or communication infrastructures, censorship generally presupposes an environment in which information can be intercepted, reviewed, modified, or prohibited before circulation. Historically, it belongs to worlds in which publication remains relatively centralised and where access to public communication is unevenly distributed.

Moderation emerges from a different problem. Historically associated with restraint, temperance, mediation, and the management of debate, moderation is less concerned with preventing communication than with maintaining communicative order under conditions in which exclusion alone becomes insufficient. If censorship addresses the problem of controlling publication, moderation addresses the problem of governing communication once publication becomes widely accessible. This distinction becomes particularly relevant in nominally open and pluralistic environments where speech cannot simply be prevented from circulating and where different actors maintain fundamentally different understandings of what constitutes acceptable expression. In such contexts, moderation appears as a response to a problem that censorship struggles to solve: how can speech be governed when there is no longer stable agreement about what should be excluded from public communication?

Understanding moderation in this way requires moving beyond its most visible manifestations. Contemporary debates often reduce moderation to content removal. Accounts are suspended, videos are removed, search results are hidden, recommendations are adjusted, and users experience these interventions as moments of exclusion. Yet the empirical material examined throughout this thesis suggests that removal constitutes only one element within a considerably broader repertoire of speech governance techniques. Demotion, contextualisation, labelling, warning systems, visibility adjustments, recommendation filtering, temporary suspensions, and algorithmic downranking all operate according to a different logic. Rather than preventing speech from existing altogether, they seek to regulate the conditions under which speech circulates. Their objective is often not elimination but attenuation. They intervene in visibility, reach, legitimacy, and discoverability rather than existence itself.

Reducing moderation to censorship therefore obscures one of its most important characteristics. Moderation emerged historically not only as a means of limiting communication but also as a means of maintaining the conditions under which communication remains possible. Throughout this thesis, I have described this function through the concept of socio-informational coherence. Every communicative environment depends upon certain shared assumptions that enable participants to exchange with one another. These assumptions concern language, norms, authority, evidence, civility, expertise, and conduct. They need not be complete, nor do they require unanimous agreement. They do, however, provide enough common ground for communication to occur. Moderation contributes to maintaining these conditions. It is therefore not merely a mechanism through which speech is restricted. It is also a mechanism through which communicative environments reproduce themselves.

This dual character of moderation helps explain why conflicts over moderation have become increasingly prominent over the past decade. If moderation were merely a matter of removing objectionable content, disputes would concern the content itself. Yet moderation also participates in defining the conditions under which communication occurs. As a result, moderation controversies often become controversies over the legitimacy of those conditions. Participants disagree not only about whether particular forms of speech are harmful, discriminatory, or misleading, but also about the standards through which such determinations are made. Put differently, disagreements about moderation increasingly become disagreements about metamoderation: the norms, procedures, institutions, and authorities through which moderation itself is organised.

This insight provides a useful bridge to the methodological contribution of the thesis. If moderation operates not only through visible interventions but also through the broader organisation of communicative environments, studying moderation becomes considerably more difficult than simply documenting removed content or platform policies. One of the central challenges confronting moderation research is that moderation frequently leaves behind traces rather than direct evidence. Posts disappear. Visibility changes. Recommendations shift. Accounts become more difficult to discover. Search results are reordered. The intervention itself often remains inaccessible, while its effects become visible only indirectly.

Chapter 2 approached this problem by drawing inspiration from traditions that confronted similar challenges long before the emergence of digital platforms. Scholars of censorship, publishing history, and literary editing have frequently reconstructed intervention through its traces. Rather than observing censorship directly, they have examined modifications, omissions, substitutions, silences, and absences. Such approaches begin from the recognition that interventions into communication frequently reveal themselves retrospectively through their consequences rather than through direct observation.

Building on this tradition, Richard Rogers and I proposed a framework for reconstructing what we termed the scene of moderation. Rather than approaching moderation as a discrete event, the chapter treated moderation as a platform effect distributed across policies, infrastructures, enforcement mechanisms, user adaptations, visibility systems, and patterns of circulation. This shift has important implications. Once moderation is understood as a platform effect, a much broader range of empirical material becomes relevant. Policy archives, visibility changes, recommendation systems, user responses, evasive vocabularies, migration patterns, alternative platforms, and emerging speech cultures all become traces through which moderation can be studied.

This perspective proved particularly productive when examining the relationship between moderation and speech affordances in Part II. The concept of speech affordances was introduced to capture the ways in which platforms shape possibilities for expression through the interaction of policies, technical systems, and user cultures. While affordance theory has often focused on what technologies allow users to do, the chapters in Part II approached affordances from the perspective of speech governance. The question was not simply what platforms enable, but what forms of communication become easier, more difficult, more visible, or less visible under different moderation regimes.

One consequence of this approach is that moderation can no longer be understood solely in relation to individual platforms. Speech affordances shape how information moves between platforms as well. This observation emerged clearly in Chapters 3 and 4, which examined the circulation of extreme speech and replacement narratives across different communicative environments. A recurring finding across both chapters was that speech rarely disappears when it becomes difficult to express. Instead, it undergoes processes of displacement and transformation. Messages become coded. They acquire euphemistic forms. They are translated into humour, irony, symbolism, or ambiguity. They migrate between platforms possessing different moderation regimes and different thresholds of acceptability.

This process is neither new nor unique to digital communication. Scholars of censorship have long observed that prohibited ideas frequently survive through indirection. What digital platforms add to this dynamic is a more complex geography of displacement. Speech no longer moves only through language. It moves through infrastructures. Narratives travel from fringe platforms into mainstream ones, from imageboards into social media feeds, from alternative media systems into broadcast news, and back again. Their meaning often changes as they travel because each environment affords different forms of expression. What appears as an overt political statement in one environment may appear as irony in another, humour in a third, and coded language in a fourth.

The study of replacement narratives illustrates this particularly clearly. Narratives concerning demographic replacement did not circulate uniformly across platforms. Instead, they were continually adapted to local affordances. They acquired different vocabularies, different rhetorical forms, and different degrees of explicitness depending on the communicative environment in which they appeared. The significance of this finding extends beyond the specific case. It suggests that moderation does not simply determine whether speech exists. It also shapes the routes through which speech travels, the forms through which it survives, and the publics through which it continues to circulate.

The same observation emerged from the analysis of large language models in Chapter 5. Although AI alignment is often treated as a separate field of inquiry, many of the underlying questions concern speech governance. Different organisations operationalise notions of risk, harm, controversiality, and objectionability in different ways. Through training datasets, reinforcement learning procedures, red-teaming exercises, and safety evaluations, these organisations effectively construct models of acceptable and unacceptable communication. Examining model responses to controversial Reddit prompts revealed significant variation in how different systems respond to contested speech. More importantly, it revealed that moderation increasingly occurs before communication enters circulation at all. Rather than governing speech retrospectively, alignment systems attempt to shape the production of speech itself.

Taken together, these chapters suggest that moderation cannot be understood simply as a practice of removal. It is equally a practice of distribution, transformation, anticipation, and displacement. It governs not only what becomes difficult to say, but also where speech travels when it encounters resistance. This observation becomes particularly important once we turn to the moderation crises examined in the second half of the thesis. For if speech continues to circulate despite intervention, the central problem increasingly becomes not the elimination of disagreement but the management of disagreement under conditions in which consensus can no longer be assumed.

The moderation crises examined throughout Part III reveal that the challenge facing platforms is not simply one of enforcement. It concerns the management of disagreement under conditions in which agreement about the norms of communication can no longer be taken for granted. This distinction matters because moderation systems are frequently designed as if objectionability were a relatively stable property of content. Categories such as hate speech, misinformation, incitement, harassment, or discrimination are operationalised through policies, enforcement procedures, and technical systems that presume a degree of stability in how those categories are understood. Yet the empirical material examined throughout this thesis suggests that the meanings attached to these categories remain contingent, historically situated, and politically contested.

Chapter 6 approached this problem through the concept of moderation crisis. The chapter argued that moderation crises emerge when the assumptions underpinning moderation cease to command sufficient legitimacy amongst the populations subjected to them. This is not simply because users object to moderation decisions. Moderation has always generated disagreement. Rather, crises emerge when disagreement extends to the categories, institutions, and authorities responsible for defining what moderation seeks to regulate. At that point, moderation becomes entangled in broader struggles over meaning.

The examples examined throughout the chapter illustrate this dynamic particularly clearly. Categories such as racism, antisemitism, hate speech, extremism, or misinformation are often treated as though their meanings are self-evident. In practice, however, their meanings evolve over time and remain subject to continuous renegotiation. Concepts that appear stable from the perspective of institutional actors often appear contested from the perspective of users. Moderation therefore becomes implicated in disputes over language itself. What one actor understands as the enforcement of anti-discriminatory norms may be interpreted by another as political partiality. What one actor regards as misinformation may be understood by another as legitimate scepticism. These disagreements cannot be reduced to factual disputes. They concern competing understandings of the normative frameworks through which public communication ought to be organised.

Viewed from this perspective, moderation performs a role that extends beyond content governance. It participates in broader societal processes through which communities negotiate what can be said, how it can be said, and under what conditions particular forms of expression become unacceptable. Moderation decisions therefore contribute to the production of social meaning. They participate in defining the boundaries of legitimacy, shaping collective memory, and establishing the categories through which future communication is interpreted. This helps explain why moderation controversies often appear disproportionate to the content that triggered them. What is at stake is rarely the content alone. What is at stake is the authority to define the terms through which communication itself is evaluated.

Chapter 7 demonstrated how these tensions emerge across different media environments. The refugee crisis provided a particularly useful case because it brought into confrontation several competing understandings of public communication. Across both social media and broadcast media, debates surrounding migration became increasingly difficult to conduct because participants no longer agreed on the normative vocabulary through which those debates should occur. Disagreements over immigration frequently became disagreements over xenophobia. Disagreements over integration became disagreements over racism. Discussions concerning cultural conflict became entangled with competing understandings of discrimination, tolerance, and inclusion.

One consequence of this process was that accusations of media bias became increasingly common. Yet the analysis suggested that such accusations often referred to something more fundamental than concerns about factual accuracy. They reflected disagreement over the normative assumptions embedded within journalistic practice itself. Editorial guidelines designed to minimise discriminatory representations of migrants were interpreted by some audiences as evidence of ideological commitment. From the perspective of journalists, such decisions frequently appeared as professional obligations. From the perspective of critics, they appeared as political interventions. The resulting conflict was therefore not simply epistemic. It concerned competing understandings of what responsible communication should look like.

This observation connects closely to the broader argument developed throughout Part IV. Fragmentation frequently emerges not because actors disagree about facts, but because they disagree about the normative frameworks through which facts acquire meaning. What appears as a neutral standard from one perspective may appear as an exclusionary norm from another. In this respect, the emergence of alternative media systems cannot be understood solely through the lens of misinformation or ideological radicalisation. It also reflects dissatisfaction with the institutions responsible for producing public knowledge.

The COVID-19 pandemic explored in Chapter 8 reveals a related but distinct dynamic. Unlike the refugee debates, where disagreement concerned the interpretation of social norms, the pandemic generated situations in which authoritative institutions themselves struggled to maintain consensus. Public-health agencies, governments, scientific organisations, and medical experts frequently disagreed regarding treatments, transmission mechanisms, preventative measures, and public-health interventions. These disagreements evolved rapidly as new information emerged. Yet platforms were nevertheless expected to moderate communication according to authoritative guidance.

This expectation placed platforms in an unusual position. In the absence of stable institutional consensus, moderation increasingly required platforms to adjudicate between competing claims to authority. Decisions about which information should remain visible, which information should be labelled, and which information should be removed effectively positioned platforms as participants in disputes they were originally expected merely to host. As Chapter 8 argued, platforms became accidental authorities. They inherited responsibilities that traditionally belonged to institutions operating outside the platform ecosystem and were forced to exercise forms of judgement for which neither their infrastructures nor their legitimacy claims were originally designed.

The implications of this process become even clearer in Chapter 9. The concept of normative dislocation was introduced to describe situations in which moderation systems developed within one political and historical context are applied to another. The Brazilian case demonstrates that moderation cannot be separated from the historical experiences through which speech norms acquire meaning. Around the 2022 election, moderation was frequently framed through the language of misinformation, electoral integrity, and democratic resilience. Yet these categories entered a political environment shaped by a different historical trajectory. The problem confronting Brazilian institutions was not simply misinformation. It was the continued presence of unresolved tensions surrounding military dictatorship, democratisation, transitional justice, and competing memories of political legitimacy.

From this perspective, moderation becomes inseparable from questions of historical memory. Decisions concerning what information should circulate also become decisions concerning which interpretations of the past remain publicly legitimate. Platforms therefore enter fields of conflict that extend well beyond communication itself. They become implicated in struggles over memory, identity, legitimacy, and democratic continuity. Moderation begins to resemble a form of governance over collective memory as much as a governance of information.

Taken together, the chapters gathered under Part III suggest that moderation crises are ultimately crises of consensus. Not consensus in the simplistic sense of universal agreement, but consensus concerning the institutions, procedures, and authorities through which disagreement can be managed. Once those foundations become unstable, moderation acquires a different political significance. It becomes one of the principal sites through which societies negotiate competing understandings of legitimacy.

The consequences of this instability become visible in Part IV. If moderation crises concern disagreements over the norms through which communication is governed, platform ruptures concern the reorganisation of communication once those disagreements become sufficiently entrenched. Throughout the thesis, I have used the concept of platform rupture to describe situations in which actors cease recognising the legitimacy of the communicative environments in which they previously participated and begin constructing alternative ones. Importantly, these ruptures do not necessarily emerge from technological innovation. They emerge from political, normative, and epistemic disagreements that become difficult to reconcile within existing infrastructures.

Chapter 10 approached this process through the formation of online counter-spheres. Drawing on traditions of counterpublic theory, the chapter argued that alternative communicative environments frequently emerge where actors perceive existing publics as incapable of accommodating their concerns, experiences, or interpretations of events. Such environments are often described as fragmented or polarised. Yet fragmentation alone does not adequately capture what is occurring. Counter-spheres are not merely spaces of withdrawal. They are also spaces of production. They generate alternative narratives, alternative forms of expertise, alternative authorities, and alternative understandings of legitimacy.

This becomes particularly apparent in Chapter 11’s examination of alternative encyclopaedias. Projects such as Metapedia and Infogalactic emerged not because their contributors rejected the production of knowledge. On the contrary, they sought to produce knowledge according to alternative epistemic principles. Their existence therefore reveals that epistemic conflict frequently concerns not the rejection of truth but disagreement over the institutions authorised to produce it. Epistemic ruptures occur when actors cease recognising common authorities as legitimate arbiters of knowledge and begin constructing alternative infrastructures through which competing understandings can be maintained.

Yet the empirical material examined throughout these chapters also suggests that rupture rarely produces complete separation. Users continue moving between platforms. Information continues circulating across boundaries. Alternative media systems remain oriented towards mainstream ones, just as mainstream systems remain oriented towards alternative spaces. The result is not the emergence of isolated publics but the formation of new relations of interdependence. What emerges are speech markets characterised by different affordances, different moderation regimes, and different thresholds of acceptability, but which nevertheless remain connected to one another.

This observation is reinforced by Chapter 12. Folk theories of persecution frequently portray deplatforming as a moment of exclusion from public life. Yet the empirical evidence suggests something more complicated. Deplatforming rarely severs connections entirely. Instead, it reorganises them. Users migrate. Audiences fragment. Content travels through alternative routes. New dependencies emerge. The consequence is less a clean separation of publics than a reconfiguration of how communication circulates between them.

These findings have important implications for how moderation is understood. If speech repeatedly survives through displacement and if ruptured publics remain connected through complex forms of interdependence, then moderation cannot be evaluated solely according to whether content is successfully removed. The more important question concerns what kinds of communicative environments moderation ultimately produces.

The final part of the thesis examined a development that initially appears paradoxical. If many of the crises and ruptures described throughout the preceding chapters emerge because platforms have become increasingly involved in adjudicating disputes over public norms, then one possible response would be for platforms to withdraw from that role altogether. Yet this is rarely possible. Platforms remain subject to legal obligations, commercial pressures, public scrutiny, and growing demands to address everything from hate speech and political violence to misinformation and coordinated manipulation. They cannot simply abandon moderation. The question therefore becomes how moderation can operate under conditions where consensus can no longer be assumed.

Chapters 13 and 14 suggest that part of the answer lies in a gradual transformation of moderation itself. Throughout much of the past decade, moderation has frequently been organised around a relatively straightforward model. Platforms identify objectionable content, determine whether it violates platform rules, and intervene accordingly. While the sophistication of the technical systems involved has increased considerably, the underlying logic remains broadly adjudicative. Moderation operates by determining whether particular forms of speech should circulate and by imposing interventions when they should not.

One of the limitations of this model is that it presumes a relatively stable understanding of objectionability. Content is treated as though its harmfulness, misleadingness, or acceptability can be established independently of the social environments within which those judgements occur. Yet the moderation crises examined throughout this thesis repeatedly demonstrate that such stability is often absent. Users disagree about what constitutes misinformation. They disagree about what constitutes racism, discrimination, manipulation, harassment, or political extremism. They disagree about which authorities deserve trust and which institutions possess legitimacy. Under such conditions, moderation increasingly encounters the limits of adjudication.

Chapter 13 explored how Twitter responded to this challenge through what I described as modulated moderation. Rather than treating objectionability as a fixed property of content, modulated moderation approaches intervention as a question of degree. Content does not necessarily move between the binary categories of acceptable and unacceptable. Instead, it becomes subject to varying levels of visibility, amplification, discoverability, and circulation. Demotion occupies a particularly important place within this framework because it allows platforms to intervene without relying exclusively on removal. Information may remain available while becoming less prominent. Its visibility may be reduced without necessarily being eliminated.

The significance of this development extends beyond the specific techniques involved. Modulated moderation reflects a broader recognition that communicative environments characterised by deep normative disagreement may require different forms of governance than those premised upon stronger consensus. Instead of seeking to resolve disagreement through categorical decisions, platforms increasingly seek to manage disagreement by regulating the conditions under which communication circulates. This does not eliminate normative judgement. Decisions concerning visibility remain political decisions. Yet modulation represents a shift away from moderation understood exclusively as adjudication.

Chapter 14 suggests that this shift may be part of a broader transformation. Community Notes emerged from a recognition that many moderation disputes concern not only information itself but the legitimacy of those responsible for evaluating it. Traditional fact-checking systems frequently struggled because large segments of the public no longer accepted common authorities as neutral arbiters of truth. Community Notes responded to this problem by attempting to identify agreement across groups that would ordinarily disagree with one another. The significance of the system lies less in whether it always succeeds than in the problem it seeks to solve. Rather than imposing consensus from above, it attempts to identify consensus where it already exists.

In this respect, Community Notes can be understood as one example of a broader family of moderation techniques that seek to distribute the work of judgement across publics rather than concentrating it within institutions. Such systems remain experimental and imperfect. They produce exclusions of their own. They may fail under particular political conditions. They remain vulnerable to strategic behaviour and manipulation. Nevertheless, they point towards a different understanding of moderation. Instead of assuming that legitimacy emerges primarily from authoritative adjudication, they assume that legitimacy may depend increasingly upon visible processes through which disagreement is negotiated.

Seen historically, there is something familiar about this development. One of the arguments developed in Chapter 1 was that moderation emerged not simply as a practice of restraint but also as a practice of debate management. The moderator of a discussion was not expected to resolve disagreement. The purpose of moderation was to ensure that disagreement remained possible without collapsing the conditions of exchange altogether. While contemporary platforms differ enormously from the communicative environments in which these earlier understandings emerged, the underlying problem is recognisably similar. How can communication continue amongst actors who disagree not only about political questions but also about the terms through which those questions should be discussed?

The developments examined in Chapters 13 and 14 do not provide a definitive answer to this question. They do, however, indicate that moderation may be gradually returning to concerns that were present in some of its earlier conceptualisations. Rather than functioning solely as a mechanism for limiting communication, moderation increasingly becomes concerned with organising the conditions under which communication occurs. This shift is particularly visible in the emergence of bridging systems, distributed evaluation mechanisms, and other experiments designed to facilitate contact between actors occupying different normative environments.

These developments invite a reconsideration of moderation as a public-interest infrastructure. Throughout much of the platform governance literature, moderation is approached primarily as a regulatory challenge. The central questions concern compliance, enforcement, transparency, accountability, and risk mitigation. These questions remain important. Yet the cases examined throughout this thesis suggest that moderation also performs another function. It participates in maintaining communicative relations between groups that may otherwise possess few opportunities for meaningful contact.

This observation becomes particularly important when viewed in relation to the ruptures described in Part IV. Alternative platforms, alternative media systems, and alternative knowledge projects are frequently interpreted as evidence of fragmentation. In many respects, they are. Yet fragmentation is only part of the story. Throughout the empirical material examined here, ruptured publics rarely ceased orienting themselves towards one another. Mainstream media continued responding to alternative media. Alternative platforms continued reacting to mainstream platforms. Counter-spheres continued defining themselves against the publics from which they had separated. Even where disagreement appeared profound, relations of observation, response, and exchange persisted.

The persistence of these relations suggests that platform ruptures do not eliminate the need for communication. If anything, they reveal how difficult it is to escape it. The various publics examined throughout the thesis remain entangled within the same informational environments even when they reject one another’s authorities, norms, and institutions. This observation raises the possibility that some of the developments often described as fragmentation may simultaneously be understood as a reorganisation of contact between political cultures that previously interacted under very different conditions.

Indeed, one alternative reading of the period examined throughout this thesis is that it represents not only a crisis of public communication but also an expansion of it. Many of the disagreements explored here did not emerge because digital platforms created them. Debates concerning immigration, national identity, political violence, historical memory, discrimination, race, religion, and democratic legitimacy long predate social media. In many cases, what changed was not the existence of disagreement but its visibility. Positions that had previously remained marginal, geographically dispersed, institutionally excluded, or culturally stigmatised acquired new opportunities for articulation. Actors who rarely encountered one another suddenly found themselves sharing communicative environments.

This does not imply that all forms of speech deserve equal legitimacy, nor that every rupture should be celebrated as democratic progress. Some communicative conflicts produce genuine harms. Some forms of political mobilisation remain explicitly exclusionary. Some narratives rely upon conspiracy, scapegoating, or historical distortion. Yet it would be equally mistaken to interpret every rupture solely as a pathology. The visibility of disagreement is not necessarily evidence of democratic decline. In some cases, it may reveal disagreements that had never been resolved in the first place.

The refugee debates discussed in Chapter 7 offer one illustration. The tensions surrounding immigration did not originate on social media. Nor did concerns regarding cultural change, integration, or social cohesion emerge with digital platforms. What changed was the ability of different actors to articulate competing interpretations publicly and to challenge the institutions that previously mediated those discussions. Similar observations can be made about the historical grievances examined in Chapter 9, the alternative epistemologies explored in Chapter 11, and the counter-spheres described in Chapter 10. Across these cases, platformisation did not create disagreement so much as expose it.

From this perspective, some of the crises examined throughout the thesis may also be understood as moments of confrontation between political and normative cultures that had previously coexisted without engaging one another directly. Such confrontations are often uncomfortable. They frequently produce instability. They may generate fragmentation, distrust, and conflict. Yet they also create opportunities for forms of exchange that would otherwise remain impossible. The problem is therefore not whether disagreement should exist. The problem is how communicative environments can continue functioning when disagreement becomes both visible and persistent.

This returns us to the question of dialogue.

One of the more striking conclusions emerging from the thesis is that marginalisation alone appears increasingly difficult to sustain in highly pluralistic communicative environments. Speech can be displaced, transformed, translated, redirected, and redistributed. It can become more difficult to encounter. It can lose legitimacy within particular spaces. Yet it rarely disappears altogether. The result is that disagreement remains present even when institutions attempt to suppress it. Under such conditions, dialogue ceases to be merely a normative aspiration. It becomes a practical problem of governance.

This does not mean that dialogue is easy, nor that all forms of dialogue are desirable. The history of political thought contains many reasons to be sceptical of naïve assumptions concerning rational consensus. The disagreements examined throughout this thesis often concern questions that are unlikely to be resolved through discussion alone. Yet traditions stretching from Erasmus to contemporary work on political moderation have long recognised that communication across difference serves purposes beyond agreement. Dialogue creates opportunities for understanding, clarification, coexistence, and mutual intelligibility even where consensus remains elusive.

The Erasmus discussed throughout the theoretical literature reviewed in this thesis is instructive in this regard. Moderation, in the Erasmian sense, was never simply a matter of occupying the political centre. It concerned the cultivation of conditions under which opposing positions could encounter one another without requiring total victory or total defeat. Similarly, the traditions of political moderation explored by thinkers such as Craiutu treat moderation less as an ideology than as a practice of coexistence under conditions of uncertainty. What these traditions share is an interest in preserving the possibility of communication even where deep disagreement persists.

The challenge facing contemporary platform governance is that the communicative environments under consideration differ substantially from those for which earlier theories of dialogue were developed. Scale, automation, algorithmic curation, recommendation systems, generative AI, and transnational communication create conditions that historical traditions of moderation never had to confront. This suggests that future research must move beyond the question of how harmful content should be removed and towards the question of how communicative environments can be designed for disagreement.

Part of this work is already visible. Research on prosocial media, bridging systems, deliberative technologies, conflict transformation, peacebuilding, and collective sensemaking increasingly addresses problems that were once considered separate from platform governance. New initiatives are exploring how principles drawn from dialogue studies and conflict resolution might inform technical design. Organisations working on social cohesion are beginning to engage directly with questions of platform architecture. Scholars of deliberative democracy, peace studies, communication, and computer science are increasingly confronting overlapping concerns.

The significance of these developments extends beyond academic research. Regulators, too, may need to reconsider the assumptions underpinning contemporary approaches to platform governance. Much existing regulation remains oriented towards the identification and removal of harmful content. While such interventions remain necessary, the findings presented throughout this thesis suggest that content governance alone is unlikely to address the deeper problems generated by communicative fragmentation. Questions concerning contact, understanding, legitimacy, and dialogue are becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

The central question emerging from this thesis is therefore not whether moderation should exist. The cases examined throughout these chapters make clear that some form of speech governance is unavoidable. The question is what moderation becomes once stable consensus can no longer be assumed. The history traced throughout this dissertation suggests one possible answer. Moderation began as a practice concerned with restraint and the management of disagreement. Over time, digital platforms increasingly transformed it into a system of large-scale adjudication. The crises, conflicts, and ruptures examined throughout the thesis reveal some of the limitations of that transformation. The developments explored in the final chapters suggest that moderation may once again be moving towards forms concerned less with resolving disagreement than with organising the conditions under which disagreement can continue.

Whether such efforts will succeed remains uncertain. Yet the question they pose is likely to remain one of the defining questions of contemporary communication. Not how disagreement can be eliminated, but how societies characterised by deep normative plurality continue speaking to one another despite it.
