# **Part III. Moderation crises**

==In a separate page, in front of every chapter, make clear what my contribution for each co-authored piece was.==

Part III turns to what happens when the consensus that a public sphere needs in order to hold together begins to fail. [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1) argued that moderation keeps its legitimacy only where there is some relative agreement about the norms of debate — about what may be said, and about how, or whether, to moderate it. The four case studies gathered here describe what follows when that agreement breaks down. Each is a different kind of dissensus, marked by the actor whose norms come apart from the platform's: its own publics (Chapter 6), the editorial conventions of legacy news media (Chapter 7), public health authorities and heads of state (Chapter 8), and institutional memories (Chapter 9). Read in sequence, they describe crises of rising stakes — from a public losing faith in a platform, to a platform displacing the very institutions whose authority it was meant to relay.

[Chapter 6. Moderation in crisis](#/chapter-6) examines the dissensus between a platform and its own publics. Through a genealogy of how "problematic information" was contested on Twitter and YouTube across hate-speech and COVID controversies, it shows how publics progressively disagree with, and eventually divorce from, a platform over its moderation decisions. What triggers the divorce is not only what is moderated but how: as content moderation becomes vertical — decided from above, with little visible deliberation — users shift from arguing about what is unacceptable to say toward arguing about the unacceptable ways in which speech is moderated in the first place. The consensus that [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1) held to be necessary for a public sphere's cohesion is, here, eroded by the manner of its own enforcement.

[Chapter 7. Of migrants and strangers](#/chapter-7) turns to the dissensus between social media publics and the editorial norms of legacy news media. It compares how the "refugee crisis" — sparked by the Syrian civil war and other conflicts — was articulated across Dutch television news and on Facebook and Twitter between 2013 and 2018, a period in which mass media was losing ground to social media as an authoritative source of truth and of norms. Using digital methods and NLP to set broadcast transcripts beside audience responses, it traces how the two came to read the same events through different normative lenses. A rejection of mainstream editorial guidelines by social media users eventually issued in a turn toward "alternative", populist right-wing news media, and in the active transgression of anti-discriminatory speech norms — to the point where broadcasters and their publics were, in effect, working from mutually alternative facts.

[Chapter 8. Twitter as an accidental authority](#/chapter-8) describes a platform stepping into the breach left by faltering institutions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Twitter moderated the claims of authoritative sources and their audiences on the treatment, transmission and prevention of the virus. As public health organisations and heads of state struggled to hold consensus — among themselves as much as with their audiences — Twitter exceptionally assumed an adjudicative role, an "accidental authority" in its own right: it flagged, suspended and deleted content, including that of the very authoritative sources whose word it elsewhere sought to amplify.

[Chapter 9. Normative dislocation](#/chapter-9) ==raises the stakes furthest.== Around Brazil's 2022 presidential elections, platform moderation rooted in US electoral experience prioritised electoral "misinformation" while leaving open calls for a military coup largely untouched. The chapter calls this *normative dislocation*: US-based moderation standards operating outside the local frameworks — built through reconciliation, dialogue and democratic reconstruction — within which Brazilian speech about political violence had been contained. In dislodging those frameworks, platforms risked reigniting historical conflicts a country had laboured to settle, and with no adequate provision for public dialogue. It closes by proposing moderation models that build in consensus-building and locally embedded understandings of historical violence.

Across the four, dissensus over particular content gives way to a harder dissensus over the norms of debate themselves, and over who is entitled to set and enforce them. When that conflict finds no place in which it can be worked through, it tends to resolve itself by what I call "platform ruptures". That is the subject of [Part IV. Ruptures](#/part-4).
