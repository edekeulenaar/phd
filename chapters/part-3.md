# **Part III. Moderation crises**

Part III turns to what happens when moderation norms become subject to unresolved dissensus. [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](Manuscript/Chapter%201.%20Censorship%20and%20moderation.md) made the case that moderation keeps part of its legitimacy from metamoderation affordances — that is, possibilities for publics to agree on the terms of their engagement. The four case studies gathered here describe what follows when that agreement breaks into deep disagreements [@fogelinLogicDeepDisagreements1985]. Each is a different kind of dissensus, marked by the actor whose norms come apart from the platform's: its own publics ([Chapter 6](Chapter%206.%20Moderation%20in%20crisis.md)), the editorial conventions of legacy news media ([Chapter 7](Chapter%207.%20Of%20migrants%20and%20strangers.md)), public health authorities and heads of state ([Chapter 8](Chapter%208.%20Twitter%20as%20an%20accidental%20authority.md)), and institutional memories ([Chapter 9](Chapter%209.%20Normative%20dislocation.md)). Read in sequence, they describe crises of rising stakes — from a public losing faith in a platform, to a platform displacing the very institutions whose authority it was meant to relay.

[Chapter 6. Moderation in crisis](Chapter%206.%20Moderation%20in%20crisis.md) examines the dissensus between a platform and its own publics. Through a genealogy of how "problematic information" was contested on YouTube comment spaces discussing hate-speech and misinformation, it shows how users progressively disagreed with, and eventually divorced from, a platform over its moderation decisions. What triggered this rupture were not only moderation norms but also techniques: users shifted from arguing about what is unacceptable to say toward the unacceptable ways in which speech is moderated in the first place. The consensus that [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](Manuscript/Chapter%201.%20Censorship%20and%20moderation.md) held to be necessary for a public sphere's cohesion is, here, eroded by the manner of its own enforcement.

Chapter 6 began as a study presented at the Association of Internet Researcher's conference of 2022. It is a collaboration with Ivan Kisjes. Ivan executed the method (data processing, several internal methodological decisions), while I designed and wrote the method and study overall. The study is not yet published and, written, remains in draft form.

[Chapter 7. Of migrants and strangers](Chapter%207.%20Of%20migrants%20and%20strangers.md) turns to the dissensus between social media publics and the editorial norms of legacy news media. It compares how the "refugee crisis" — sparked by the Syrian civil war and other conflicts — was articulated across Dutch television news and on Facebook and Twitter between 2013 and 2018, a period in which mass media was losing ground to social media as an authoritative source of truth and of norms. Using digital methods and NLP to set broadcast transcripts beside audience responses, it traces how the two came to read the same events through different normative lenses. A rejection of mainstream editorial guidelines by social media users eventually issued in a turn toward "alternative", populist right-wing news media, and in the active transgression of anti-discriminatory speech norms — to the point where broadcasters and their publics were, in effect, working from mutually alternative facts.

Chapter 7 is a collaboration with Thomas Poell, Anne Helmond, Bernhard Rieder and Jasmijn van Gorp. Thomas Poell initiated the article with Bernhard Rieder during at DMI winter school data sprint in 2018. Bernhard and Mari Wigham collected the data from the CLARIAH Media Suite, Twitter and Facebook APIs. Anne wrote the literature review, and Thomas the introduction and conclusion. I designed the empirical study and wrote the main body of text, while Ivan Kisjes executed the data analysis. Jasmijn provided feedback and edited the article. The article also benefitted from public feedback in several workshops organised by CLARIAH, including by Liliana Melgar Estrada, Roeland Ordelman,  Susan Aasman and Julia Noordegraaf.

[Chapter 8. Twitter as an accidental authority](Chapter%208.%20Twitter%20as%20an%20accidental%20authority.md) describes a platform stepping into the breach left by faltering institutions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Twitter moderated the claims of authoritative sources and their audiences on the treatment, transmission and prevention of the virus. As public health organisations and heads of state struggled to hold consensus — among themselves as much as with their audiences — Twitter exceptionally assumed an adjudicative role, an "accidental authority" in its own right: it flagged, suspended and deleted content, including that of the very authoritative sources whose word it elsewhere sought to amplify.

The chapter is part of collection of studies edited by Richard Rogers. It is a collaboration with Ivan Kisjes, Rory Smith, Carina Albrecht, Eleonora Cappuccio and Jack Wilson. Jack edited the chapter and contributed to its conceptual focus. Ivan executed and co-designed the method with me. Rory and Carina contributed to the literature review and the data collection, while Eleonora produced the chapter's visualisations. With their contributions, I designed the study and wrote the chapter.

[Chapter 9. Normative dislocation](Chapter%209.%20Normative%20dislocation.md) raises the stakes somewhat further. Around Brazil's 2022 presidential elections, platform moderation rooted in US electoral experience prioritised electoral "misinformation" while leaving open calls for a military coup largely untouched. The chapter calls this *normative dislocation*: US-based moderation standards operating outside the local frameworks — built through reconciliation, dialogue and democratic reconstruction — within which Brazilian speech about political violence had been contained. In dislodging those frameworks, platforms risked reigniting historical conflicts a country had laboured to settle, and with no adequate provision for public dialogue. It closes by proposing moderation models that build in consensus-building and locally embedded understandings of historical violence.

The chapter is a journal article co-authored with Marcelo Alves Junior. Ivan Kisjes contributed to the data collection and method design. Betsy Grossman and Sara Vrondran have contributed to data analysis during the DMI Winter School data sprint of early 2023. Angeles Briones did most visualisations of this project. Richard Rogers and Sabine Niederer encouraged the focus on the notion of normative dislocation. I designed the study and wrote most parts of the introduction, literature review, method, findings and discussion.

Across the four, dissensus over particular content gives way to a harder dissensus over the norms of debate themselves, and over who is entitled to set and enforce them. When that conflict finds no place in which it can be worked through, it tends to resolve itself by what I call "platform ruptures". That is the subject of [Part IV. Ruptures](Part%20IV.%20Ruptures.md).
