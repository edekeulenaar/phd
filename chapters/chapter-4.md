# **Chapter 4** 
# Tracing the convergence of replacement narratives in poorly moderated online milieux


In the last few years, journalists have decried the role of social media platforms such as YouTube as “radicalization machines” whose algorithms funnel users towards more extreme political content [(Tufekci, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?MNLGe9). Recent quantitative research has however questioned this prevailing hypothesis, arguing that the problem of political radicalization online is not caused by the supply of media “'radicalizing’ an otherwise moderate audience” but rather, and more disturbingly, that it is also a question of “audience demand, previously constrained by a more limited ideological scope” [@mungerRightWingYouTubeSupply2020]. Similarly, political scientists Matthew Goodwin and Roger Eatwell argue that the authoritarian turn in Europe and internationally should be understood as a symptom of a “new era of political fragmentation, volatility, and disruption” as opposed to the “"last howl of rage’ from old white men soon to be replaced by tolerant Millennials” (2019, epub).

In this case study, we take a media studies approach to answering the question of how reactionary political opinion appears to emerge from the poorly moderated milieux of the anonymous message board 4chan and the YouTube comment section. As media studies scholars, our most fundamental axiom is McLuhan’s statement that “the medium is the message” [@mcluhanUnderstandingMediaExtensions1994]. McLuhan advocated for an ecological type of understanding of the systemic dynamics that each new technology creates for itself. In claiming that "the effects of technology do not occur at the level of opinions or concepts, but alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without any resistance", McLuhan may be understood as merely pointing to the profound (and often largely unnoticed) epistemological effects of new media environments [@mcluhanUnderstandingMediaExtensions1994, 40]. Whereas McLuhan’s metaphor of the “global village” was much celebrated by the techno-utopians of Silicon Valley, what comes to the fore in our analysis is the dark side of McLuhan’s metaphor: the global village as a parochial environment, which retrieves atavistic political dynamics (wrongly) considered by many to have been relegated to the dustbins of history. We thus offer this research in an effort to render visible the misunderstood effects of new media on much broader socio-political dynamics, specifically the growth and spread of racial conspiracy theories online.

In the midst of a prolonged and economically-driven crisis legitimation, the future of the European project was by 2019 uncertain. Nationalist Eurosceptic parties, many on the far-right, sought to dismantle whilst, paradoxically, promoting the notion of a pan-European cultural (and in some cases race-based) identity. Since the so-called Syrian “refugee crisis” began in 2013, the issue of migration has served to galvanize a resurgent far-right in many European countries, including Germany [(Wigger et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?hzRCee) and France [(Frigoli & Ivaldi, 2017)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?GEMxL4). Beyond Germany’s “Welcome Culture” policies, the European Union’s response to the crisis has fed into the narratives of a number of autocratic parties and politicians, who portray the EU’s agenda on this and other issues as being fundamentally at odds with their national interests [(Wigger et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?l4m7kA). As captured in the successful Brexit "Leave" campaign, these populist narratives indeed portray the relationship between the EU and the refugee crisis in distinctly conspiratorial terms as part of a plan to undermine the sovereign integrity of the nation state. Nationalist-populists politicians frame this and other issues in the antagonistic narrative of “the people versus the elite”, or “us versus them”. This rhetorical style seeks to appeal to the “common sense” of “ordinary people”. Its core contention is that representative democracy is a carefully cultivated "simulacrum" intended to "delude ordinary voters", which only radical populists dare to challenge by saying "uncomfortable truths" — as voiced by the Vlaams Blok slogan "zeggen wat u denkt", Flemish for "say what you think" (Betz and Johnson, 2017).

In her analysis of the “alt-right”, the American scholar of extremism Alexandra Minna Stern has written of how “interactive social media facilitates a genre of affective and open-ended storytelling” which “moves through nodes and platforms in embedded and hyperlinked forms [...] becoming interwoven into metanarratives with the potential to reshape discourse and what is knowable and expressible.” [(Stern, 2019, p. 95)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?DrPuQm). Following this observation, our argument is that politicians from the radical populist right do not so much _invent_ as they _amplify_ this style of political rhetoric, which finds its home in the relatively unmoderated milieux of the “alternative” political discussion sections of 4chan/pol or the YouTube comment section [(R. Lewis, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?k5Ia9g), besides Telegram, VK, BitChute and other emerging “alt-tech” [(Donovan et al., 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?hiObP9). As such, our empirical analysis looks at the role of relatively unmoderated online discussion forums in fostering extreme antagonism online, and how this kind of discourse corresponds to the radical right wing variant the “populist” rhetorical style that “seeks to mobilize ordinary people against both the established structure of power and the dominant ideas and values of the society” [(Betz & Johnson, 2017, p. 69)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?yd24tl). Building on past research about the technical and social affordances of the anonymous message board 4chan [(Tuters & Hagen, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?gogm8V) and the YouTube comment section [(Alshamrani et al., 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?HHdCe1) in giving rise to viral conspiracy theories and racist vernacular slang, this chapter makes use of a heuristic that posits 4chan and the YouTube comment section subcultural milieux. It posits these subcultural milieux as a site of convergence for extremist ideas of “codes” — in this case as the French “great replacement” and the American “white genocide” conspiracy theories — which are then disseminated or “transcoded” into more mainstream political culture. 

## Mainstreaming Replacement Narratives

In March of 2019, a man by the name of Brendon Tarrant killed 51 people and injured 49 during two consecutive attacks on mosques in Christchurch, New Zealand. Before the killing, Tarrant posted a manifesto on 8chan, the sister website of 4chan, entitled _The Great Replacement: We March Ever Forwards_, in which he advocated the use of "edgy humor and memes in the vanguard stage" for a global war to defend Western culture. Presenting himself as motivated by a narrative of Europe under threat, Tarrant also inscribed the weapons with which he carried out his attack with the names and dates of significant battles in European history.

Since World War II, French and European far-right discourse has sought to distance itself from the racial language of Nazi and American white supremacism by embracing a strategy of “differential racism” (Balibar & Wallerstein, 1991, p. 22). In the past decade, and with greater momentum since the European refugee crisis, these nuances are arguably becoming lost, as American “alt-right” nativism, particularly the belief that “whites” are victims of a demographic replacement, has made greater inroads into the French and European political mainstream. In March of 2011, for example,  Jean Raspail’s _Camp des Saints_ (1973) became a top five best-seller in France [(Dupuis, 2011)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?oKFi0R). The book tells the story of how millions of Indian migrants “slithered” into France [(Raspail, 1975, p. 15)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?5s9ZLX), demanding First World standards of living while showing no interest in integrating with local culture. It was later cited by Steve Bannon as an analogy of the European refugee crisis, which he described as “a Camp of the Saints-type invasion into Central and then Western and Northern Europe” [(Blumenthal & Rieger, 2017)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?2f6g1C). Most recently the presidential campaign of Éric Zemmour in France has demonstrated the inherent speciousness of “differentialist” rhetoric when he was heard approving of Ukrainian refugees for being “white” and “Christian” in contrast to “Arab” and “Muslim” [(Valeurs Actuelles, 2022)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?LzisT8).

Though originally from the United States, the concept of a “white genocide” is frequently associated with the Great Replacement [(Davey & Ebner, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?wbS4PV). Described by one expert as "a master plan dedicated to the total destruction of the white race [...] driven by forces engaged in an active conspiracy specifically targeting the white race" [(Wendling, 2018, epub)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?IvrA3n), the term "white genocide" was originally coined in 2006 by Bob Whitaker [(Hawley, 2017)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?A80jpH), an American Radical Right figure also known for formulating the slogan "anti-racist is a code word for anti-white" (which encapsulates the “alt-right’s” conjoining of anti-political correctness with white nationalism [(Greene, 2019](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?UU4SA6)). As such, the term “white genocide” can be seen as something of an American variant on Camus’ great replacement theory, which while bearing a family resemblance to the latter, shifts the focus from “European culture” to “whiteness”.

## The affordances and political culture of 4chan and the YouTube comment section

How and to what extent have the racialist framing of migration by the American “alt-right” come to resonate with the ideas of “cultural” or “civilizational” replacement from the French far-right in specific milieux of the Web? To explore this question, we begin discussing how the design of environments like 4chan or the YouTube comment section enable or afford the expression of extreme ideas or vernaculars, which would otherwise be moderated on YouTube videos as hateful language or “abusive behavior”  [(YouTube, 2019, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?G7knkR). Our hypothesis is that the unmoderated milieux of social media platforms, such as the YouTube comment section, tend to concentrate extreme contents that, by virtue of meeting in the same place, may begin to converge ideologically. Methodologically, we examine how the great replacement and white genocide theories have spread across 4chan and the YouTube comment section, and then use a host of natural language processing techniques to assess the extent to which they have converged in each milieu.

### Speech affordances

Since roughly 2018, a host of social media platforms have taken more resolute measures to hamper the circulation of various kinds of speech that actively “[push] the boundaries of acceptable norms of public culture” (Pohjonen and Udupa 2018: 1174), as with for example active content moderation measures against hate speech (YouTube, 2019) or “abusive behavior” (Twitter, 2022). One of the most frequently reported by-products of platform moderation is the distribution of content across platforms with varying levels of tolerance for objectionable speech. Hateful language tied to pseudo-scientific racist “debates”, conspiratorial narratives linked to COVID-19 or the U.S. elections of 2020 have been reported to migrate to platforms that can best afford it, be these “free speech” alternatives like 4chan, Telegram (Rogers, 2020), or specific areas of otherwise actively moderated platforms, such as the YouTube comment section. Through their governance policies and content moderation features, every platform may be understood as effectively affording and licensing certain forms of speech, which in turn provide more or less favorable conditions for the growth of different political cultures.

Indebted to Gibson (J. J. Gibson, 2013), the term “affordances” is broadly understood in STS, social media and platform studies as “what material artefacts such as media technologies allow people to do” (Bucher & Helmond, 2017, p. 3). This broad definition can refer to a plethora of platform features that regulate users’ speech: “abstract high-level” affordances that “enable or constrain [...] communicative habits” (Bucher & Helmond, 2017, p. 12), such as content moderation policies (A. Gibson, 2019); feature-oriented “low-level” affordances, such as content moderation techniques (demotion, deplatforming) and moderation features (flagging, reporting); and “platform vernaculars”, such as anonymity and free speech features designed in accordance with users’ own speech norms. Each of these are implemented by different platforms in ways we describe below.

### The affordances of the YouTube comment section

For the past five years, YouTube has often been the target of public criticism for allegedly “radicalizing” its users through the personalization features of its recommender system [(P. Lewis, 2018; Munn, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?YJknUT), as well as hosting a sizable community of far-right or “alt-right” influencers [(R. Lewis, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?r31bdB). In response, the platform has ceased to rely solely on users to report or “flag” content, enacting instead a series of new measures to remove hate speech, “misleading statements” and other types of objectionable information [(YouTube, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?w08cKe). These measures are enacted through its content moderation policies, which have since 2019 been repeatedly updated to capture new kinds of hate speech (such as the more conspicuous discriminatory vernaculars of biological or “scientific” racist beliefs [(E. de Keulenaar et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?CBsdCo)), and outline techniques to sanction and “deplatform” such content outside of the platform [(Rogers, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?kBPeVT). It is under the enactment of its hate speech or “abusive behavior” policies that YouTube has removed thousands of contents tied to far-right extremism in June of 2019 [(Waterson, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?EtOBxT), or introduced more sophisticated techniques to downrank or “demote” objectionable content in search and recommendation results [(E. de Keulenaar et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?TXiB0L).

The YouTube comment section bears a notorious reputation for toxicity: time and again, research has highlighted the concentration of racist slurs and generally “uncivil” behavior in this section of the platform [(Alshamrani et al., 2020; Goode et al., 2011)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?8EaqZk). Over the years, the platform has tested various techniques to address this problem [(Alexander, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?8zGybi). In 2015, YouTube launched a “ranking system” akin to Reddit’s, where comments can be up- or down voted by users or be automatically ranked on top of a comment list if its poster is someone the video uploader follows [(Alexander, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?6Vb6aA). With modest results, it tried in 2016 to give more moderation affordances to video uploaders, who were granted the ability to appoint specific moderators, blacklist undesirable slurs, or automatically filter inappropriate content with the assistance of hate speech detection algorithms [(Shao et al., 2018, p. 279)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?QxwfwU). Finally, in 2017, the company allowed creators to turn off their comment sections in specific videos [(Alexander, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?JC8mDe). The problem of toxicity has however persisted, partly because the comment section relies for the most part on users to report inappropriate language, based on the assumption that (human) moderation is best equipped to judge what is and is not permissible in context [(Munn, 2020, p. 2)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?HtNaqK).

### The vernacular affordances of 4chan/pol

These user-based or “vernacular” methods of speech moderation are at the foundation of image boards like 4chan, which is historically known as incorporating in its design a “free speech absolutist” philosophy of public debate [(Phillips, 2015)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?N6Ulkm). The image broad has since 2003 been regimented by a relatively lax moderation policy, with founder Christopher Poole delegating 4chan’s moderation practices to its own “community”; 4chan’s existing “Rules” and small group of about 20 senior and junior moderators only “provide a general framework” [(Schwartz, 2008)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?OY1XQF). Junior moderators, or “janitors”, are tasked with deleting posts, images or recommending seniors to ban users that violate any of 4chan’s 17 rules [(4chan, 2022)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Z7fT7l), though what gets deleted and why is reportedly up to the volition of a few senior moderators [(Arthur, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?pSiAo7). Since 2016, for example, 4chan’s moderation has reputedly favored content on the (extreme) right – despite the site officially prohibiting racist content [(Arthur, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?jfwei8).

4chan’s exertion of free speech-friendly moderation policies is especially visible in its communication affordances. The key affordance to facilitate the propagation of racist and otherwise openly transgressive (or “extreme”) speech is its anonymity: users cannot (and should not, as per 4chan’s policies) reveal their identities. A notable effect of this affordance is the isolation of speech from the moral oversight of one’s public identity, which facilitates the usage of ambiguous and ironic language that characterized the communication strategy of emerging “alt-right” groups [(Tuters, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?GiqyzM). Similarly, the ephemerality of 4chan board feeds — i.e., the relatively fast order in which user posts appear and disappear in a feed — is described as a factor in the board’s constant concoction of conspiratorial narratives, by virtue of users weaving arbitrary connections from a stream of unrelated posts [(Tuters et al., 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?dUeeCW).

### The cross-platform dissemination of replacement conspiracies via poorly moderated spaces

Though existing studies have described the progressive dissemination of these vernaculars across Facebook, YouTube, Twitter, Instagram and other platforms, they have seldom specified the kinds of affordances that facilitate it. We develop the hypothesis that the relatively lax speech regulations of 4chan and the YouTube comment section can be thought of as access points for objectionable language to cross through and concentrate in. Under this analytical framework, 4chan is typically considered the baseline from which objectionable language arises. Due to its unique affordances, the site has been the home of many memes, vernaculars and tropes that have been characterized (erroneously or not) the nascent “alt-right” during the online culture wars of 2015-2018: Pepe the Frog, Kekistan [(Tuters, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?RAa75O), conspiracy theories like “Qanon” [(Zeeuw et al., 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?nqC21W) and Pizzagate [(Tuters et al., 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?3GLuFZ), as well as various hateful slurs [(E. de Keulenaar et al., 2021; Peeters et al., 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?CDPNtY)). In previous research, we have also described how YouTube could not prevent comments from expressing conspiratorial narratives linked to COVID-19, despite its “deplatforming” of numerous related videos [(E. de Keulenaar et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?JxF0PY). The same could be hypothesized for conspiracies linked to demographic replacement, which, due to its xenophobic, islamophobic, racist and antisemitic components, could arguably be classified by YouTube’s hate speech policy as bordering on “Conspiracy theories saying individuals or groups are evil, corrupt, or malicious” [(YouTube, 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?NFQl4K). While a considerable number of channels spreading such conspiracies have been terminated since 2019 [(E. de Keulenaar et al., 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?geDsV2), comments claiming the veracity of replacement conspiracies in otherwise “authoritative” reports, such as a Deutsche Welle feature on migration towards Europe, are indeed still online [(DW News, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?P9UMQ0).

## Method

With such considerations in mind, our method is designed to explore (1) the dissemination of French and American replacement conspiracies across the less moderated spaces of 4chan and the YouTube comment section; (2) the extent to which these relatively distinct narratives of demographic replacement (civilization and racial respectively) coincide or “meet” in the same YouTube comment sections; and (3) whether, by virtue of “meeting” in the same spaces, they eventually begin to converge ideologically. These three points of inquiry were approached with the methodological pipeline we describe below.

### Data collection

Our 4chan data was collected using 4CAT, a data collection and analysis tool (Peeters et al., 2021). 4CAT relies on 4plebs, an online archive of nearly all of 4chan, to extract posts. Using 4CAT, we collected all posts that mentioned “white genocide”, “great replacement” or “grand remplacement” – the equivalent in French – in any period of time. We queried these comments and videos using the keywords “great replacement” and “white genocide”. This resulted in 4,427 posts. Our collection of YouTube comments originates from a dataset compiled by Dimitri Tokmetzis, a Dutch journalist specializing in online political extremism for _De Correspondent_  [(Tokmetzis, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?PMtThA). Tokmetsiz used the YouTube API v.3 to capture the videos and comments of channels by official left- and right-wing European and American political parties, media organizations, NGOs, and think tanks identified on Wikipedia, right-wing extremist forums (4chan/pol, 8chan), or by academic literature and reports by NGOs such as Hope not Hate and Kafka [(Kafka, 2021)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?gHISfg). The categorization of channels as left- or right-wing followed a broad spectrum of channels that moved from the political center to extremes at different points in time (Tokmetzis, 2019). The dataset contains 36,065,106 comments from 175,553 videos dating from 2006 and the end of 2018.

### Query design

Our obvious choice of keywords for filtering the two above-mentioned datasets were “white genocide”, “great replacement”, and the equivalent term in French, “grand remplacement”. However, our objective was to explore the possible convergence of these two conspiracy theories. For this reason, we aimed to capture the widest possible amount of alternative expressions of these two concepts so as to eventually test whether one or another formulation is mentioned interchangeably by users on 4chan or YouTube. While one could refer to “white genocide” as such, others may also use vernaculars like “white erasure” (or in French, “genocide des blancs”). The concept of “great replacement” is used interchangeably with “muslim invasion”, “population replacement”, or in French “invasion musulmane”. There are terms that cross the boundaries between these two distinct concepts: “kalergi”, “mass migration”, “demographic replacement” or “dieversity” (a pun on “diversity”) may refer to both white or European replacement. Finally, slurs like “race mixer”, “rapefugee” or t “amerimutt” may allude to the underlying beliefs of the white genocide and great replacement concepts in a much more colloquial manner. Colloquial expressions were selected from a list of 4chan slurs  collected by Peeters et al [(Peeters et al., 2020)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?FCtzVF). More conventional expressions (“mass migration”) were found using word2vec [(Goldberg & Levy, 2014)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Wyl6qB), a natural language processing technique designed in this case to find combinations of words that are semantically similar to a given query. The queries were “great replacement”, “white genocide” and “grand remplacement”.

Our results comprised 3 terms related to the “white genocide” conspiracy theory, 7 terms related to “great replacement” (2 of which were in French), 5 terms related to either conspiracy (“hybrid” terms), and 7 terms from Peeters et al.’s list of hate speech slurs (Figure 2). With these queries, we found a maximum of 3,127 4chan posts and 58,387 comments. For a baseline comparison, we also found a total of 2,881 video transcripts.

### Finding ideological convergences between the “great replacement” and “white genocide” conspiracies on 4chan and YouTube comments

Our analysis consisted in first (1) tracing the spread of the great replacement and white genocide conspiracies on 4chan and YouTube comment sections; (2) finding any mention of the two conspiracies in the same YouTube comment sections; (3) measuring the extent to which YouTube commenters have began to mention the great replacement or white genocide conspiracies interchangeably – i.e., whether these two concepts have begun to converge _semantically_. To trace the cross-platform dissemination of the two concepts, we simply counted the amount of times that 4chan posts, YouTube comments and YouTube video transcripts mentioned any of our 23 queries over time (**Figure 4**). We then looked at whether any of these queries were mentioned in the comment section of the same channels (**Figure 5**). Finally, we measured _semantic_ converges between the two concepts based on whether commenters mentioned the same words next to any of our 23 queries . Technically, this meant comparing frequently collocated words, or bigrams, for each of the 23 queries related to the “great replacement” and “white genocide” theories (**Figure 6**).

## The cross-platform dissemination of the great replacement and white genocide conspiracy theories

### 4chan/pol

If we take 4chan/pol/ as a baseline for contemporary extreme-right political discussion, as we do below, we note that while “great replacement” experienced a significant spike in discussion around the time of Tarrant’s shooting, in general there has been a sustained growth on the extreme right around the term “white genocide”. While discussions on 4chan/pol/ cover a wide range of topics, they are virulently antisemitic, racist, and preoccupied with an dea of European identity that is entwined with fascist ideas.

**Figure 4**. *Number of YouTube comments, videos and 4chan posts that mention “great replacement”, “white genocide” and related terms, 2008-2022.*

While /pol/ is not primarily a neo-Nazi website, there does for example exists a distinct sub-community in /pol/ specifically devoted to the discussion of National Socialist ideology [(OILab, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?d9G9q3). As seen in the graphic below, the general tendency of growth in discussion on /pol/ has grown along with the Trump insurgency and reached a nadir in the summer of 2017, coinciding with the much-publicized attempts to “Unite the Right” at a march in Charlottesville Virginia, at which point National Socialist discussions also reached their peak and then markedly diminished. The same spike can also be seen above, especially in discussions including the term “white genocide”. In a cry heard around the world, marchers were heard chanting “You will not replace us” and “Jews will not replace us.” While this twist of Camus’ conspiracy theory may seem puzzling when one considers that Jews make up a small percent of the population and have a low birth rate, it resonates with the extreme antisemitism to be found on 4chan/pol/.

To get a sense of how 4chan’s (current) speech affordances facilitate the expression of theories of demographic “replacement” conspiracy theories, below we describe a selection of two of the top most posted images from posts using the term “white genocide”. One image includes the antisemitic “Happy Merchant”, who eliminates cultural and racial difference by mass producing the mixed-race “Amerimutt” [(see Hagen, 2018)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?LcVA2g). The anti-semitic twists of the replacement narrative may be attributable to the influence of the work of Kevin MacDonald, whose book _Culture of Critique: An Evolutionary Analysis of Jewish Involvement in Twentieth-Century Political Movements_ (2002) is one of the more book popular intellectual resources on 4chan/pol/ [(E. V. de Keulenaar & Kisjes, 2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?OuU1ny). MacDonald’s antisemitic argument is that Jews use their disproportionate influence as cultural gatekeepers to dilute to undermine “white culture” because they fear that a racially cohesive nations would expel them.

Another oft-shared image in posts featuring “white genocide” that refers to a "Kalergi plan’, which alleges that the European Union is a secret Bolshevik and Jewish “plan for the genocide of the white people of Europe” (**Figure 5b**). Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi (1894–1972), an early advocate of European unification and founding president of the Paneuropean Union. The Paneuropean Union was an organization opposed by the Nazis on the basis that it was allegedly under the control of Freemasonry. In the early 2010s, a conspiracy theory developed in the far-right around the "Kalergi Plan’ accused the EU of conspiring to intentionally encourage a mass immigration into Europe in order to replace its original population. This theory was even aired within the European Parliament, in a speech in which the British National Party MEP Nick Griffin referred to Kalergi as the "godfather of the European Union’ and spoke of Kalergi’s plan as the "biggest genocide in human history" and a "breeding-out" of "indigenous Europeans" via “the encouragement of mass non-white immigration" [(Thorpe, 2018, p. 227)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?kOuO3m).

### YouTube videos and comments

While the “white genocide” conspiracy bore more popularity on the largely American-centric 4chan/pol board, we see on that it becomes fare more intertwined with the “great replacement” conspiracy on YouTube videos and comment sections (**Figure 6**). The most frequently mentioned queries on YouTube videos include “mass migration”, “white genocide” and “demographic replacement”, compared to “white genocide”, “kalergi” and “mass migration” in YouTube comments. These four terms evoke both the question of a systematic invasion of European lands via mass migration, and that of a replacement of a specific _demography_ rather than culture. The mention of the “Kalergi plan” is arguably a bridge between the ideas of racial and cultural replacement, as it holds that a civilisational project like the European Union is essentially a _racial_ project designed to combat racism through miscegenation. It should also be said that, by virtue of being more actively moderated, YouTube videos tend to mention far milder terms than comments. At first sight, this may suggest that commenters tend to express the more extreme variants of concepts otherwise concealed by the more polished language of YouTubers.

### Where on YouTube do the white genocide and great replacement conspiracies converge?

We find that the terms “great replacement” and “white genocide” are mentioned in the same comment sections, namely those of European and North-American far-right YouTubers Tommy Robinson, Stefan Molyneux, Millennial Woes, Lauren Southern and Paul Joseph Watson and  (highlighted in red) (**Figure 6**). These “bridges”, particularly Lauren Southern, act as locales where users also mention other variations of the two conspiracy theories such as “dieversity”, “demographic” and “population replacement”. Though we find evidence that users mention the racial and civilisational aspects of the two conspiracies interchangeably, we also see that “great replacement” is mentioned in channels where “white genocide” does not appear and vice-versa. These include channels of European political parties or movements, like the Irish branch of the (originally) French _Génération Identitaire_ (in yellow). The same can be said about the term “white genocide”, which is mentioned in comment sections of a majority of North-American or British channels (in green).

### To what extent do “white genocide” and “great replacement” converge semantically?

Looking closely at terms mentioned by commenters that refer to “great replacement” and “white genocide”, we find that the two conspiracies converge around equivalences of “europeans” as “whites”. Words mentioned in combination with both concepts include allusions to a given culture (“Western”, “european”), race (“whites”, “ethnic”), reproduction (“women”, “children”) and government conspiracies (“left”, “plan”, “real”). The same can be said about comments in French, which allude to race and ethnicity (“épuration ethnique”, “asiatiques”, “congolais”, “blanches”), culture (“paganisme”, “occidentale”) and antisemitism (“juif”). It is nevertheless clear that content in French bears little connection to that in English, and that words similar to “white genocide” and “great replacement” only converge around English-speaking conversations. In this sense, we deduce here that convergences between “white genocide” and “great replacement” mostly occur in English-speaking comment spaces, where we see equivalences between the concept of “white” and “European”.

## Conclusion: Countering Mainstreaming 

As the French philosopher and commentator on the French New Right Pierre-André Taguieff argues, the primary “ideological achievement” of the French New Right has been the “reformulation of ‘racism’ in the vocabulary of difference” [(Taguieff, 2001, p. 5)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Xia2Pm). On the right, this strategy has had the effect of seeming to undermine the arguments of their anti-racists, who are mocked for inaccurately mistaking newer “alternative” forms of right-wing Western supremacy from old-fashioned biological racism. In fact, opposition to migration need not even be a far-right issue at all. For example, the Oxford philosopher David Miller (2016) argues that, while states have an obligation to do their fair share to protest refugees, their primary responsibility is to maximize the welfare of their own citizens, and as such they may have valid reasons for excluding immigrants when costs of integration are too high. While we may not personally agree with such a perspective, demonizing such a position may paradoxically serve the rehabilitation of far-right ideas in the form of narratives oriented towards cultural preservation and hamper efforts to moderate the extremes of European public debate.

This becomes all the more urgent with the convergence of far-right and other politically extreme content in the poorly moderated “fringe” spaces of public debates, such as 4chan, the YouTube comment section and various alternative (“alt-tech”) platforms that seek to operate as “counter” public spheres. We note how this convergence works based on specific speech affordances, where vernaculars, ideas and tropes associated to extreme ideologies effectively meet and converge ideologically and as part of the information diets of the users that inhabit the unmoderated milieux of public debate. In a more practical sense, we also offer a methodology for anti-racist efforts to locate and contextualize far-right ideologies across the Web, by tracing the ways in which extremist ideas conflate in common spaces and produce new concepts that oppose equality, with for example the language of cultural diversity as a form of “differential racism” (Balibar & Wallerstein, 1991, p. 22). In studying the “speech affordances” that regiment these spaces, we observe the delicate politics of (online) speech moderation, which must constantly balance efforts to, on the one hand, suppress or “forget” historically extreme ideas, and on the other, provide the space to actively counter these through the constructive means of public debate.
