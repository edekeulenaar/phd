# **Part II. Speech affordances**

The studies gathered in this part show how platforms are constituted, materially, by moderation — by what I define as *speech affordances*: the content moderation policies, techniques and user speech cultures that signal what may and may not be said in a given space, and under what conditions. Moderation, in this sense, is less something applied to a platform from outside than something a platform partly consists of. If Part I asked what moderation is and how one might study it, the chapters here describe how speech norms come to shape individual platforms and information flows amongst them. 

[Chapter 3. The affordances of extreme speech](#/chapter-3) lays down the theoretical groundwork. It departs from a long-standing observation in linguistics and censorship studies — that speech moderation "materializes everywhere" as a practice that constitutes and defines the boundaries of publicly permissible language [@postCensorshipSilencingPractices1998]. Where societies do not rely solely on regulatory means such as state censorship, they modulate the circulation of "extreme speech" by other means; on platforms, that modulation takes the form of speech affordances. The chapter distinguishes three kinds: "high-level" affordances such as content moderation policies and the legislation and zeitgeist they are modelled after; "low-level" affordances such as the techniques of enforcement — demotion, deplatforming, flagging, user reporting — and the platform features that carry them; and the user speech cultures that enforce norms of their own, through community guidelines, anonymity, or the ephemerality of a feed. One of the immediate implications of speech affordances, traced here through the short life of the "Kekistan" meme, is that sanctioned language does not disappear so much as it reconstitutes itself across a wider, fringe-to-mainstream geography of the Web. The same meme reads as a white-nationalist symbol on 4chan/pol, as an overt dog whistle on Twitter, and as something more equivocal in the YouTube comment section — its meaning fragmented into whatever each platform could afford to express.

Removed speech requires multiple linguistic subterfuges and displacements — a "necessary dissimulation" [@holquistIntroductionCorruptOriginals1994] that does not *say* what made it be silenced but signifies it by means of opacity. What these chapters add is that the displacement is at once linguistic and material: extreme speech is dissimulated in its wording and displaced across public spheres, transformed each time it passes from one affordance regime to another. [Chapter 4. The affordances of replacement narratives](#/chapter-4) follows one such displacement in detail. It shows how "replacement" narratives — the "great replacement" and "white genocide" codes — converge in the poorly moderated milieux of 4chan/pol and the YouTube comment section, and how they are "transcoded" into more mainstream political culture as they travel. The point is not that these milieux invent such speech, but that their affordances let it be amplified, recombined and disseminated in forms that stay sayable elsewhere.

[Chapter 5. LLMs and the generation of moderate speech](#/chapter-5) carries the same question into large language models. Where the earlier chapters concern the speech platforms afford their users, this one looks at how moderation, or "moderate speech", is imbued into the speech the models themselves generate. Comparing eight LLMs across 526 of the most controversial questions drawn from various subreddits, it examines the discursive techniques — refusals, defusal, and other rhetorical strategies — through which they answer prompts that sit along the boundaries of acceptability. It finds that models tend to meet high-"risk" prompts with refusals or normative language, but answer the most controversial prompts in markedly more inconsistent tones — a distinction that reflects a deeper tension, in value alignment, between anticipation (mitigating risk in advance) and deliberation (negotiating a live controversy). Read this way, LLMs belong to a much longer history of speech moderation, and become a new kind of participant in the ongoing negotiation of public speech norms.

Across the three chapters, speech affordances are the material through which platforms — conversational models now among them — give shape to speech norms, and through which sanctioned speech is displaced, disguised and circulated rather than simply removed. What the part leaves open is what happens when these affordances do not agree with one another: when the norms held by one platform, public or institution diverge from those of another. That is the problem of Part III.
