# **Chapter 6** 
# Moderation in crisis: YouTube and Twitter debates on the boundaries of acceptable speech 

+ "Penalizing someone for violating the rules is in fact one of the ways we as communities and societies discover, test, and reassert our shared values." [@gillespieExpandingDebateContent2020, 3]


A dictionary definition of moderation tends to present it as the management of *excess* that disrupts and disregulates set norms. It may be an "avoidance of excess or extremes, especially in one's behavior or political opinions" and the overall "action of making something less extreme, intense, or violent" [@oxfordenglishdictionaryModerationMeaningsEtymology2025]. Platforms have borrowed this definition to speak of moderation as something reducing "excesses" in speech, "misinformation", violent collective behaviour, and institutional crises ranging from elections to political violence. That ties speech moderation — be it in the form of institutional censorship [@postCensorshipSilencing1998] or the implicit norms of everyday language [@allanForbiddenWordsTaboo2006] — to a process that has always existed as an ensemble of information management techniques that, as alternatives to vertical censorship, are designed to reduce the spread and thus influence of "excessive" ideas in a public sphere [see, for example, @loewensteinLegislativeControlPolitical1938]. These techniques may counter speech, but also more disciplinary actions to marginalize, obfuscate, embargo or mediate objectionable information (see [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation v2](Chapter%201.%20Censorship%20and%20moderation%20v2.md)). 

At the same time, moderation is itself subject to a crisis. Since it is ultimately a normative practice, moderation systems have been criticised by myriad actors — from users [@panComparingPerceivedLegitimacy2022] to political representatives and regulators [for example, @committeeonoversightandgovernmentreformCoverBigTech2025] — for exerting more or less arbitrary definitions of what constitutes acceptable speech. For one, different platforms, users and institutions define and locate excesses differently. EU regulation and UN conventions have framed moderation as a key guardrail against "misinformation", "disinformation" or hate speech as the embodiment of historical excesses that, if normalised, may upend canonical post-war norms designed to maintain democratic integrity and human dignity. In an oppositely polarised view, others regard these efforts as a coordinated attempt to suppress marginalised political beliefs — whether historical or not — whose very censorship, they argue, confirms their validity. More than a crisis of platform legitimacy, this has contributed to a more fundamental crisis of consensus around what constitutes acceptable and unacceptable speech in the first place [@chongCompetingNormsFree2018b]. 

There have been a few destabilising factors. For one, platforms have tended to give space to actors and ideas once relegated to the more or less invisible fringes of legacy media [@mungerRightWingYouTubeSupply2020]; see also [Chapter 8. Twitter as an accidental authority](#/chapter-8). Though there have been efforts to deplatform them again [@zuckermanDeplatformingOurWay2021], the sheer plurality and massive connectivity of opinions, equipped by generous affordances for expression, have tended to make this strategy technically and politically unsustainable. Part of this difficulty is that more speech affordances also means a democratisation of censorship, not in the sense that anyone can censor anything, but that platforms, communities and individual users have technical leverage to determine what one may or may not say in the spaces they carve online, including in smaller corners of centrally moderated platforms.

One implication is that, rather than focusing on content moderation *enforcement*, policy and media research concerned with the state or "health" [@twitterTwitterHealthMetrics2018] of online debate could first assess public consensus on speech norms, i.e., how they deliberate on the very terms in which to hold and moderate public debate. Fundamentally, speech norms — the more or less implicit norms that delimit what may or may not be sayable — are "essentially contested" [@delaatCoercionEmpowermentModeration2012]. If they are not held by authority, they must be held by consensus [@allanForbiddenWordsTaboo2006], which of course is never total nor stable but subject to "constant negotiation" in public, legal and political deliberation [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990]. These deliberations end up modulating the boundaries of what is and is not sayable in public spheres, where a serious lack of consensus, in the form of deeply entrenched and polarised debates, can be taken as an indicator of a balkanisation of platforms into a growing number of alt-tech and decentralised alternatives. 

So how do platforms and users negotiate the boundaries of acceptable language? Thus far, a great deal of studies in related fields have been interested in user perceptions of moderation. Some of these inquire how users experience [@myerswestCensoredSuspendedShadowbanned2018; @maHowUsersExperience2023] and interpret moderation [@maywormContentModerationFolk2024], while others ask users about the legitimacy and fairness of moderation decisions [@panComparingPerceivedLegitimacy2022; @maImNotSure2022; @caiContentModerationJustice2024]. Informed by these studies, I would like to delve deeper into the *process of deliberation* that surrounds platform and user moderation. Tackling this question has meant a great emphasis on the conditions of online dialogue *about* and *within* moderation, be that providing context to disputed topics [@caplanContentContextModeration2018; @leeAddingMoreContext2020; @wilsonHateSpeechSocial2020]; reducing political content in recommendations [@weatherbedInstagramThreadsWill2024]; or embedding bridge-based algorithms [@dekeulenaarAlgorithmicDiplomacyIntegrating2018; @ovadyaBridgingSystemsOpen2023] in systems like Community Notes [@twitterhelpCommunityNotesTwitter2023; @xcommunitynotesDiversityPerspectives2025], which have now become the norm across platforms, including Tiktok. 

Using digital methods [@rogersDigitalMethods2013], this case study offers a longitudinal and cross-platform analysis of what YouTube and its commenters have deliberated on what is unacceptable speech and what moderation techniques (cancelling, deplatforming, shadowbanning, etc) to apply. I use a variety of natural language processing (NLP) techniques to perform a "distant reading" [@morettiDistantReading2013] of what YouTube comments and platform policies consider to be unacceptable speech, and then close-read and contextualise instances in which significant divergence arose. I rely on an archive of 102 million YouTube comments posted between 2006 and 2022 [@jurgYouTubeRadicalization20082019]. Comments are filtered by keywords that refer to unacceptable speech ("racist", "mysogynystic", "blasphemy", etc.) and content moderation practices ("deplatforming", "cancelling", "shadowbanning", etc.). YouTube policies are collected via the Wayback Machine and Platform Governance Archive [@katzenbachPlatformGovernanceArchive2023a]. Finally, I look at evidence of migration towards alternative platforms, such as Telegram, BitChute or Truth Social following those moments.

## **The moderation of crises**

One of the core functions of moderation, as discussed in [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1), is to manage crises as a form of _excess_. There are, on the one hand, traditions of liberal political philosophy stemming as far back as Hobbes where the term “moderation” refers to a fundamental need for societies to maintain a relatively “circumscribed and peaceful sphere” [@rabinowitzHarmonyCitySoul2014, 2-3] by restraining or minimising political, religious or other “extremisms” as forms of “immoderation”. This first use of moderation is a form of restraint: one “curbs” “moral indignation and fervor in favor of complaisance and a willingness to get along for the sake of a shared interest in security […] and prosperity of the individual and of the nation” [@rabinowitzHarmonyCitySoul2014, 9]. Likewise, one sees many examples of platform moderation as a method to demote and, to an extent, remove the worst excesses of user-generated content to maintain a certain norm, and thus security, in the rest of the platform, where crisis is addressed by lowering the amplitude of expression that threatens baseline coordination.

In another sense, moderation is also synonymous with sustainable relations as a way to prevent conflict as crisis. The underlying method, here, is to facilitate concord or “social cohesion” through “agreement and shared opinion” in such a way that excesses resulting from poor social relations are unlikely to arise [@rabinowitzHarmonyCitySoul2014, 217-218]. Rabinowitz draws from Montesquieu’s “spirit of pluralism” as a guarantee of moderation in “citizens themselves” via a “spirit of commerce” [@rabinowitzHarmonyCitySoul2014, 8; @craiutuVirtuesPoliticalModeration2001]. This implies a pluralistic environment in which moderation is practiced in the very relations among publics and the possibilities that they create for an environment “respectful of difference” [@smithSociologiesModeration2013]. Such an environment, in principle, allows for a cathartic effect that maintains concord while reserving specific spaces in society for “unleashing certain passions while redirecting and restraining others” [@rabinowitzHarmonyCitySoul2014, 219], moderating not only content outputs but relational dynamics over time.

## **The crisis of moderation**

At the same time, moderation — particularly as the restraint of political or social excesses — is itself in crisis. First, there is a difference of moderation agendas, i.e., disagreements over _what_ needs moderating. The finger is often pointed to actors on the left of the political spectrum, particularly CSO actors pressuring platforms to sanction speech offensive to minorities [cit]. On the other side of the spectrum, the agenda for moderation is not restricted to free speech but “woke” language in its most caricatural form [cit], or, as of late, the incitement or glorification of violence related to the murder of Charlie Kirk [cit]. These conflicting agendas rest on different historical premises around what language is of legitimate use [@mcintoshLanguageTrumpEra2020], and around what influence an expression may have on a certain historical status quo, such that “excess” is refracted through incompatible normative backgrounds.

Second, there is considerable disagreement about the _method_ of content moderation. Different estimations over how problematic a certain expression is lead to disagreements over the punishment or redress of that expression. One high-profile example is the deplatforming of Donald Trump, which was judged both as proportional or necessary given the violence unfolding during the Capitol Hill riots [cit] and inappropriate given his functions as representative of the United States [cit]. Naturally, this has also led to significant discord over _who_ should carry moderation decisions [cit], i.e., who has legitimacy to turn moderation into “levers” of public expression, and thereby of public debate and culture, raising questions of authority, accountability and the locus of decision-making.

Third, perhaps the thorniest of disagreements is over how objectionable speech is defined. That is, what racist, misinformative or inciting speech consists of is itself the subject of significant discord [cit]. The impact of this disagreement is fundamental in the sense that it precedes other applications of moderation, particularly _the reason_ for moderation, since justificatory thresholds and proportionality cannot be coherently specified without prior agreement on what the underlying category denotes.

## **Essentially contested norms**

Disagreement over moderation is normal to the extent that speech norms are essentially contested in the sense posited by Gallie [@gallieIXEssentiallyContestedConcepts1956] and later explored by [@delaatCoercionEmpowermentModeration2012]. Consensus over what constitutes racist, misinformative and otherwise objectionable language may benefit from more or less consensus over time [cit], particularly as disagreements over their definition are “not resolvable by argument of any kind” while being “sustained by perfectly respectively arguments and evidence” [@gallieIXEssentiallyContestedConcepts1956, 169]. This gives rise to “endless disputes about their proper uses on the part of their users”, implicit or explicit [@gallieIXEssentiallyContestedConcepts1956, 169], where contestation is structurally embedded in the concept’s application rather than a mere failure of rational discourse.

Consider xenophobia, racism or antisemitism as essentially contested concepts: they are appraisive (they accredit some value or judgment); internally complex and open (the value has multiple aspects and can refer to multiple things); variously describable (different aspects may be emphasised differently); open to evolution under changing circumstances; used defensively and aggressively (each user asserts their version against others); linked to an exemplar (derived from an acknowledged tradition or prototype); and sustained by competition (rivals can develop the concept differently or further). These characteristics do not dissolve the concept but, following Gallie, constitute its living use across disputes and time.

Speech norms are also contested in their relations to a taboo. The taboo is in itself profoundly contradictory, because it signifies both “the law and its opposite” (Faugere, Mugnier and Sylvos, 2023, p. 16); that is, it indicates the rule and that which it prohibits. For example, the expression “n-word” may indicate the rule not to say the word while, in the same breath, indicating the very thing it is trying to hide. There is a productive tension that reveals the contestedness of not just the rule — to not be racist — but the very use of the term. This tension is “productive”, in the sense that it invites myriad different ways to address, express or contest the taboo, multiplying boundary tests and metalinguistic negotiations.

Contestedness also appears in uses or applications of speech norms. De Laat, in his study of Wikipedia editing systems, highlights the “contestedness” of moderation in the sense that it is constituted by two consistent but conflicting logics: a logic of _control_ (see [Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1)), where editors complain about moderation as a coercive and bureaucratic procedure that runs counter to Wikipedia’s egalitarian ethos of the “encyclopedia that anyone can edit”; and an _enabling_ logic, in the sense that proponents view the same rules as empowering affordances against vandalism, increasing reliability, and preserving Wikipedia. The same institutional devices thus appear both as constraint and as capacity, depending on vantage point and normative baseline.

In all cases, the contestedness is essential to use, to the extent that it is endless but not pointless. The contestedness of speech norms creates an incessant “discursive contestation” [@fraserRethinkingPublicSphere1990]. This makes public spheres fundamentally “discursive spaces where society deliberates about normative standards and even develops new frameworks for expressing and evaluating social reality” [@hauserFeaturesPublicSphere1987, 439], keeping borders dynamic and malleable to shifting public opinion and norms rather than fixed by decree.

These debates include recurring questions about what kind of language risks creating long-term violence or communal harm (e.g., hate speech); what kind of language destabilises social or political order; ensures the dignity of individuals or groups; destabilises a normative or historical order (e.g., Holocaust revisionism); conjures and legitimises an unforgivable past (e.g., Nazi insignia); and, more recently, risks the deliberative manipulation of public opinion to destroy public consensus or deliberation (e.g., state-led disinformation). The breadth of these issues underscores why definitional disputes have downstream effects on enforcement, design and legitimacy.

## **Consensus features**

The tension deriving from contestedness _requires constant kinds of consensus-building_, especially in the absence of an authority (Allan and Burridge, 2006, pp. 8–9). Part of this is essential to the formation of the _public_ aspect of public spheres. Public spheres are constituted by “discursive features from which individuals who are personally strangers derive the code of common meanings that enable them to inhabit the same world” [@hauserFeaturesPublicSphere1987 citing Taylor 1971]. Language thus forms a “commonality” of norms and meanings that constitutes, in the terms of online territories, the “boundary” of a public sphere — the binding element of public spaces as a shared understanding — where the boundary is discursive because it is made by common meaning or, at least, by efforts to arrive at it.

It is, in this sense, a space of personal sacrifice, i.e., a “discursive realm in which individuals and groups transcend their private concerns […]” [@hauserFeaturesPublicSphere1987, 438]. “Public sphere” here means that, as Hauser reiterates, it becomes the space where particularities (in the form of individual, exclusive, or sector-specific interest) _must_ become universal. What ensues is the discursive work to find commonality — to “articulate the general interest of society” (Habermas 1992). Part of this exercise is to “reach understanding” (Habermas 1981; 1984: 230), i.e., to arrive at a universalising function that can support coordination and mutual accountability beyond parochial frames.

On an individual or group level, actors attempt to be understood on the basis of validity claims in “communicative action”. Agents, through their utterances, make validity claims (to truth, rightness, truthfulness) on which the meaning of their utterances depends, which are then taken up by their interlocutors and form the basis of understanding and any subsequent interactions. We only understand a speech act or what someone is saying if we have access to the utterer’s reason for saying that. I.e., “We understand a speech-act when we know what makes it acceptable” (Habermas 1984), i.e., justifiable, reasonable. On a broader level, the “lifeworld” is a “background stock of cultural knowledge that is ‘always already’ familiar to agents” and thus makes mutual understanding possible, i.e., historical experiences and the meaning and context behind the normative orientation of language. This may constitute historical narratives: for example, one understands that something is antisemitic based partly on shared or told history of the Holocaust, even as normative words retain multiplicity of meaning.

In the Western hemisphere, particularly in Europe, certain historical periods have marked the creation of speech norms. These include post-war events, such as denazification in Germany; social movements, such as feminist and sexual liberation movements; and nationalisation or decolonisation, be it external (e.g., the colonial legacy of France, England and other countries over ideas of race and identity) or internal (e.g., “derussifying” the Baltics and Eastern Europe). For example, the process of denazification in Germany, though formally delimited from 1945 to 1954, is considered an ongoing and ever-evolving process [@vincentPunirReeduquerProcess2008]. That process begins with establishing new ground rules, reforming the constitution accordingly, and establishing memorials that remind and explain why these rules were put in place. Historical pretext gives meaning to speech norm: “[…] that is, the concept implies that members of the society in question know what the norm is and agree on the strength of the obligation accompanying the rule involved.” (Allan, 2016, p. 28). Other times, a moral imperative is established but without full historical consensus, as in decolonisation and emancipatory measures in the US.

Speech norms also have a functional impact in the form of speech affordances. One can see speech norms as forming the “pragmatic preconditions” of communication: _the rules of discourse_. These rules indicate what can and cannot be said, which in turn delimit the larger boundaries of a (linguistic) field of action, in this case a platform. Speech norms function as “rhetorical lenses that filter and alter the utterances they frame; they can accommodate some expressive modes and not others” (Fraser 1991). As indicated in the previous chapter, such rules also transform into material affordances. I.e., norms around what can and cannot be said correspond to concrete affordances that may co-determine what users can and cannot do (e.g., post or repost offensive content, etc.), shaping both expression and distribution.

## **New challenges brought by platforms**

First, there is a liberalisation of speech without adequate measures to mediate the norms and values different groups carry. Second, a “democratisation of censorship” emerges in the sense that platforms and users are bestowed the possibility to decide what to censor in spite of platform regulations, enabled both by _personalised_ speech affordances and by the _personalisation_ of speech norms (identity politics, etc.). Free speech has become a “contested norm” that no longer enjoys an elite consensus [@chongCompetingNormsFree2018, 202], to the extent that it becomes an actively and reciprocally contested ground of different speech norm communities, where each community performs its own exercise of “free speech” (for example, one emphasises limitless expressions of identity, while another emphasises limitless expressions of ideas).

Third, we observe an acceleration of historical projects of cultural reform without adequate consensus-building (mechanisms for understanding outside of authority, re: Habermas; Allan and Burridge). Norms always go through different stages: emergence, cascade and internalisation [@haddenWhatsNormMapping2016, 250]. It becomes increasingly difficult to establish a norm without internalisation — the point at which there is implicit and at least “general” consensus [@rossiVarietiesNormativeConsensus1985, 334] — in online environments where there is no centralised point of deliberation and consensus-building [@sunsteinRepublicCom2002]. Likewise, _previous_ norms may also lose consistency, with increased debates about their historical justifications and their portability into new media contexts.

Fourth, “context collapse” impairs mutual understanding. We only understand a speech act if we have access to the utterer’s reason for saying that. I.e., “We understand a speech-act when we know what makes it acceptable” (Habermas 1984), i.e., justifiable, reasonable. With collapsed contexts, it is difficult to see the background, context and reason behind someone else’s utterance. The challenge is, thus, to _not_ understand the very norms of language that renders communication impossible, because it removes necessary context for communication and eventual understanding — we no longer “know what makes it acceptable” normatively.

Fifth, the application of moderation decisions is partial. Studies that look into the relationship between users and platforms have focused on how users experience [@myerswestCensoredSuspendedShadowbanned2018; @maHowUsersExperience2023] and interpret moderation [@maywormContentModerationFolk2024]. Others ask users about the legitimacy and fairness of moderation decisions [@panComparingPerceivedLegitimacy2022; @maImNotSure2022; @caiContentModerationJustice2024], examining experiences and perspectives as misunderstandings, miscalculations, or flawed “imaginaries”. In the absence of clear (and trusted) justifications for moderation, users effectively draw “connections between related phenomena, developing non-authoritative conceptions of why and how their content was removed” (Myers West, 2018, 8). This raises the question of whether moderation “works” if users do not consent to it in the long term, and how it transforms the content that was originally sanctioned (see e.g., [Chapter 11. Deplatforming, demotion and folk theories of Big Tech persecution](#/chapter-11)).

These studies, however, cannot highlight the overall _deliberation_ that undergirds experiences or perceptions of moderation — namely, _what_ users themselves may consider as objectionable information and how they think such information should be moderated in relation to their environment, i.e., how the platform does moderation. This is the reason why we ought to look at the _process of deliberation_ of these standards, especially when it is centralised by platform moderation. Accordingly, we follow the _flow_ of speech norms in acknowledgment of discursive spaces as “open-ended” [@hauserFeaturesPublicSphere1987, 439] and variable, dynamic, and in constant negotiation. This larger process of public deliberation about speech norms is of historical weight: they are, as Fraser would put it, processes that come to delineate public spheres and their “counter” parts.

# **Research questions**

So, what can we do? First, analysis: what is the state of public norm deliberation on main platforms, and to what extent do users and platforms agree on _what_ ought to be unacceptable and what that means; what are the effects of disagreements. Second, recommendations: what can be done on the level of policy and platform design. I propose that both of these ought to focus on enforcing public consensus rather than focusing on moderation enforcement. How have YouTube, Twitter and Facebook publics defined acceptable speech in relation to truth and discrimination between 2006 and 2022; to what extent have these platforms’ content moderation policies (and techniques) converged with users’ definitions; what is the impact of any eventual divergence amidst users and platforms; and what are the role of speech norms in the formation of public spheres.

# Method

To illustrate this, I would like to see how speech norms have evolved within platform policies and their user base.

## Datasets

### YouTube comments

The YouTube dataset used for this study was collected by Dutch journalist Dimitri Tokmetzis in the fall of 2018 and later expanded in 2023. The dataset contains 600,000 videos by around 940 US and European channels of varying political orientations, in addition to 120 million video comments. We chose to only examine comments, as we deemed them 

Tokmetsis used the YouTube API v.3 to capture the YouTube channels of left- and right-wing political parties, media organizations, NGOs, and think tanks identified in Wikipedia, academic literature, imageboards (4chan/pol, 8chan), and reports by NGOs such as Hope not Hate and Kafka (Kafka, 2021; Tokmetzis, 2019). Ranging from 2006 to late 2018, Tokmetzis’ dataset contains 1,491,099 videos, 496,858 video transcripts, and 39,755,848 comments.

It was collected in the fall of 2018 in the context of contentious debates about speech norms, including free and hate speech, or the standards by which one ought to judge something or someone as racist, misoynystic, or otherwise discriminatory [cit]. 

### YouTube policies

For comparison, all of YouTube's policies were collected using the Wayback Machine and the Platform Governance Archive (PGA for short) [@katzenbachPlatformGovernanceArchives2023]. While the latter offers complete access to policy documents, it merges them into one category, "Community Guidelines". Thus, the Wayback Machine was used to collect each policy — from Hate Speech, to Spam or various blog posts — individually. This was done by downloading the text of all YouTube policy URLs in the PGA [@platformgovernancearchivePGAWorkbenchURL2023] using a command line. 

## Filtering process

The YouTube dataset was filtered to only contain references to debates about what constitutes acceptable language ("you can't say *"; "* is unacceptable", "* should be suspended OR removed OR banned OR cancelled", etc.); moderation ("suspended", "demoted", "shadowbanned"...); discrimination ("hate", "harassment", "discrimination", "antisemitism"...); and fakeness ("fake news", "misinformation", "disinformation", and so on) (see [CH4 CS1 Table 1](CH4%20CS1%20Table%201.png)).

## Analysis

### YouTube policies 

The analysis consisted of two parts. The first was to look at what which kinds of content YouTube policies tended to be invested in. This was done by using the relative number of edits that each policy received [see Figure x], in addition to which enforcement techniques were most mentioned over time [see Figure x]. Though it is not a robust indicator of what YouTube may or may not deem problematic, it does reveal a process of *problematisation* of user-generated content, as seen in its identification, definition and the severity of its punishments. This process may respond to internal concerns [@yorkSiliconValuesFuture2022] or be steeped in strategic PR exercises in response to user or regulatory concerns about content moderation [@hillDefinitelyNotBusiness2023]. 

### YouTube comments 

The second was to "distant read" [@morettiDistantReading2013] the ways in which YouTube commenters discuss notions of acceptability and truth, and of how platforms should or should not to moderate. This was done by using bigrams, i.e., words that are often positioned next to the term "unacceptable" in a context window of at least five other words. To make sense of results, Rieder's Rank Flow visualisation application [@riederRankFlow2019] was used to count which entities, issues and topics were most mentioned every year between 2006 and 2022 [see Figure x]. 

It is important to highlight what is meant by "distant reading", here. In one sense, we are not looking for explicit expressions of belief — i.e., what users *think* is or is not unacceptable — but what topics and issues arise when users discuss unacceptability in general. As seen in the [Findings], these include crime; violent or offensive language; misinformation in the form of distortions, manupulations of context, conspiracy theories or lies; or speech moderation itself. In another sense, we are more interested in *fluctuations* in these associations than representativity. The total number of comments discussing unacceptability is small [n=?], but they do illustrate a rich amount of topical variations, particularly in relation to different events. 

Next, we looked at how commenters discussed YouTube's content moderation, or moderation in general. This implied filtering commenters with sentences containing the expression "YouTube should"; "YouTube should remove" or "YouTube should allow", and then finding prominent themes or topics in the ending of each sentence [see Figures x and y]. We used word trees [@wattenbergWordTreeInteractive2008] for this task. This technique finds sentence completions for common prefixes (i.e., those just specified). As we found diverging ideas of what and how YouTube ought to moderate, we also examined how users conceptualised YouTube as a space, comparing sentences that mentioned "YouTube is" over time [Figure x and y].

# Findings

## Policies: conceptual and technical complexification

Looking at YouTube, we see that its policies, like Twitter's (see [Part V. Modulation and consensus](#/part-5)), have evolved from board but stable definitions of objectionable information into more detailed clausures. Elsewhere I have called the former a "constant extreme": content that is consistently sanctioned no matter the context in which policies are enforced. This includes sex and nudity, "shocking and disgusting content", dangerous illegal acts, paedophilia, threats, and in compliance with basic law, copyright, privacy and impersonation (see [Figure 2](Figure%202%205.png)). 

Then, from 2014 onwards, policies undergo a period of conceptual refinement, or complexification [@passmannHealthyCommunitiesToxic2023]. Technically, the Community Guidelines page fragments into pages dedicated to each type of sanctioned object: hate speech, spam, violent or graphic content, harmful or dangerous content, and content deriving from salient events, such as the COVID-19 crisis and elections. In this context, enforcement techniques begin to move from a plethora of deletion methods to dynamic demotion. This technique is explicitly applied to what the platfrom calls "borderline content" [@youtubeFourRsResponsibility2021], which denotes conspiratorial, hateful or other content with varying levels of objectionability, depending on context, public or regulatory opinion.

## Comments: from unacceptable speech to unacceptable speech moderation

How do these policies compare to users’ discussions about problematic speech? In [Figure 2](Figure%202%205.png), we see that different issues are given slightly different levels of attention. The most discussed type of objectionable content is racism (or racists, depending on the term ending), followed by abuse / abusive, bigot / bigoted, discrimination / discriminate, sexist / sexism and offensive / offensiveness. The persistence of racism as the most mentioned term among filter keywords reveals, at least partly, its importance in wider (English-speaking) debates on speech norms. One could see it as a sort of "anchoring issue" that uphold both *debate* about speech norms and the normative imperatives that derive from it. 

![Figure 3](Figure%203.1.png)

Indeed, looking closer at discussions about *racism* over time, we see that the definitions commenters have attributed to the term have shifted considerably, to the point of obtaining an essentially contested meaning. [Description]  

One can see similar fluctuations in meaning in what commenters associated to "unacceptability" over time. At first, commenters point to *unacceptable issues*, beginning with the same "consistent extremes" identified in early YouTube policies: "murder", killing "innocent lives", "barbaric" and "cruel" crimes, "violence", its "viciousness", violence committed by "islamic" movements, "terrorist groups" or "sponsored terrorists" [Figure x]. Comments then move from discussing *unacceptable issues* to *unacceptable speech*, beginning with "racism", "blasphemy", "abusevely insulting" someone, "anti-gay bigotry", "discrimination" or "discriminating", speech referring to "gay" people or "gender", "racial profiling" [Figure x]. From 2014, refer to speaking about one's "skin color", "divison and hate", "locker room talk" (likely in reference to Trump Access tapes), "anti semitism" and "slurs" [Figure x].

From 2011, we see evidence of users discussing not how others ought or ought not to speak, but unacceptable ways of moderation speech itself. Some refer to "free speech" and "Internet chaos" [Figure x]; free speech becomes a more discussed topic in 2017, and from 2019 some critique the fact of "interrupting" or "shouting at opponents"; the "refusal" and "concealed censorship" of "mainstream" media with regards to opposite points of views; the tendency for debates to be managed by it; the circumstances or plausibility of "safe" or "risk spaces"; "censorship" at "university classroooms"; the "usage of language" and its links with "behaviour" [Figure x].  

Progressively, we see a shift in discussions about *unacceptable issues*, to *unacceptable speech*, to *unacceptable ways of moderating speech*; or, at which what is unacceptable is each other’s speech moderation. This can be seen in discussions about how to deal with hate speech [Figure x]. Naturally, the age-old US debate on where to delineate the boundaries of hate and free speech is evident, here, too. While some consider hate speech to be a "free" or "protected" speech, others express concern about its presence in the platform ("hate speech is a real thing") or illegality ("hate speech is illegal for services in the us"). But perhaps most important is the fact that *the very definition* of hate speech, particularly racism — to an extent, the very grounds on which speech norms are justified — is contested. [describe]

As speech moderation becomes more explicit — from both other users or by the platform — the vision of YouTube that commenters expression also begins to change. In 2011 [Figure x], YouTube was described as a "great place" or "forum" to "meet awesome people", "share different views", "blurt out any shit that comes to your mind", "share different views", "sharing ideas", "spreading a message"; or a "great source for intellectual debate" and "learning"; and in other ways, still, a place "to be reminded that there are still a lot of dumbfucks" or "ignorants to gather in groups and support one another's ignorance".

# Discussion

## 1. The rhythm of speech norms

The speed and relative ephemerality (though with accumulated results / memory) with which issues and speech are evaluated as acceptable or unacceptable is punctuated by a few rhythms. 

- Processes of reiteration: reiterate that which is and has always been unacceptable, like murder, crime, rape and certain unacceptable crimes. This is in a sense something that has already been solidified in public memory.
- Processes of deliberation. Certain norms are actively debated and pushed for, illustrating how they are not (yet) entirely internalised. 
- What users and platforms consider unacceptable speech is dynamic, and therefore often contradictory.

## 2. Platforms, from frontiers to borders

- By taking unilateral content moderation decisions, users portray platforms from open spheres to private actors with arbitrary decision-making.
- As speech by users or platforms moderation becomes a subject to debate, the contours of YouTube as a platform become more explicit and thus contested.
- We move from "frontiers" (something at the frontier) to "borders" (something politically recognised, officialized).
- "Frontiers" as a state in which the contours of platforms as a space for debate and not yet entirely demarcated and remain malleable. 
- "Borders" as a state in which the contours of platforms are declared and marked by either groups of users or the platform itself (in this case, YouTube). 

## 3. From unacceptable speech to unacceptable ways of moderating speech 

- As a results, users move from discussing *unacceptable speech* to _unacceptable ways of moderating speech_.

## 4. Ruptures

- When there is no consensus on *how to moderate speech*, there is either entrenchment or migration towards other platforms.
- This is a worthwhile moment in this process because it marks a situation in which both the border of YouTube or user clusters are actively questioned, and a dynamic of in and outgroup demarcation (or entrenchment) arises around groups of users that identify with one or another way of moderating language (or speech norm values, i.e., values by which to decide how to moderate language). 
- This is what Ahn et al. identify this as a form of partisan group norms, in the sense that "sanctioning someone allows them to signal their partisanship by adhering to that norm" [@ahnSanctioningPoliticalSpeech2024]. 

## 5. A democratisation of censorship and multiplication of public spheres

## 6. The need for common spaces of speech norm deliberation

- In this sense, there is a fundamental need to open intermediary spaces where users and platform content moderators can deliberate and form a consensus around _what is objectionable language_ and _how (or whether) it ought to be_ _moderated_. A need for content moderation as a “social contract”: consensus as the basis for speech moderation.
