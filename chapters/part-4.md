# Part IV. Ruptures

When consensus over speech norms breaks down beyond repair — and finds no place in which it might be worked through — users migrate and build elsewhere. The three chapters here describe the several forms that movement takes: the alternative spaces that ruptures bring into being ([Chapter 10](#/chapter-10)); the alternative knowledge they go on to produce there ([Chapter 11](#/chapter-11)); and the way users adapt, rather than abandon, their use of the platforms that removed them ([Chapter 12](#/chapter-12)).

[Chapter 10. Platform ruptures and the formation of online counter spheres](#/chapter-10) conceptualises "alt-tech" platforms as counter-spheres. Much of the existing literature describes these spaces by what makes them peculiar — their subcultures, their permissive moderation, their bespoke infrastructures. The chapter argues instead that they are a recurring structural response to normative rupture: where speech norms are essentially contested and subject to constant discursive contestation, deep disagreement eventually leads some users to constitute alternatives, building complementary affordances to bypass the limitations of their former hosts. Taking a distant-reading approach, it sketches a brief history of such ruptures across the Web — cloud hosts, the blogging spheres, wikis, social media, messaging applications — before comparing the normative and technical affordances of competing spaces, from Twitter, X to Truth Social. What it finds is less a story of opposition than of coexistence: counter-spheres and the mainstream platforms they break from tend to complement one another's normative and technical limits, each affording what the other cannot.

The chapter is a summary of a presentation given to a symposium organised by the University of Copenhagen's Microsociology of Online Deviance lab by Jakob Demant and Alessandro Moretti. Its current written format is in draft form and yet to be published.

[Chapter 11. Epistemic ruptures in the encyclopedias of alternative facts](#/chapter-11) carries the same logic from the spaces of speech to the substance of knowledge. Reading Wikipedia through Lyotard's account of postmodern knowledge, it argues that the platform's ideal of consensus and neutrality meets its limit wherever disagreements prove irreconcilable, and that altpedias form at those fault lines. They are not, the chapter contends, a symptom of "post-truth" indifference to facts, but partisan projects that contest who decides what counts as knowledge, reassembling entire "universes of alternative facts" in the same platformised style of an authoritative, encyclopaedic form. Following the migration and continued "edit wars" of contested entries — the page on race and intelligence chief among them — it shows counter-spheres consolidating around epistemic norms just as they do around speech norms, each rupture marking the boundary by which an altpedia defines itself against the mainstream it broke from.

The chapter is an early article co-authored with Marc Tuters, Ivan Kisjes and Kaspar Beelen. Marc and I wrote the article together. Ivan and I designed the method, while Kaspar contributed to word2vec analyses of wiki text. Together, we organised several DMI data sprints to explore the general topic of alternative wikis.

[Chapter 12. Deplatforming, demotion and folk theories of Big Tech persecution](#/chapter-12) examines how YouTube moderated COVID-19 conspiracy narratives as "borderline content" between April and October 2020. It describes how users sanctioned for posting borderline content do not so much abandon YouTube as change how they use it: they mirror their removed videos onto permissive hosts such as Bitchute while continuing to treat YouTube as the primary stage on which that content is posted. Enforcement without deliberation, here, does not settle the dispute; it manufactures the distrust that reorganises, rather than ends, a public's relationship to the platform that rejected it.

The chapter is a journal article co-authored with Anthony Burton and Ivan Kisjes. Ivan designed the method, while Anthony and I designed the study and shared the writing.

| De Keulenaar, E., Burton, A.G. and Kisjes, I. (2021) “Deplatforming, demotion and folk theories of Big Tech persecution,” Fronteiras - estudos midiáticos, 23(2), pp. 118–139. Available at: https://doi.org/10.4013/fem.2021.232.09. |

Between them, the three chapters show how a platform that draws and polices its lines with no shared venue for negotiating them does not resolve disagreement so much as displace it into counter-spheres that constitute themselves precisely against the norms they were excluded by, whether those are norms of speech, of knowledge, or of platform use itself. Having traced this far the costs of moderation reduced to its censorial function, the thesis turns, in [Part V. Modulation and consensus](#/part-5), to what platforms have begun to do about it.
