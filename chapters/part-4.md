# Part IV. Ruptures

==In a separate page, in front of every chapter, make clear what my contribution for each co-authored was.==

If Part III ended on the observation that unresolved disagreement tends to resolve itself by exit, Part IV follows the exit. When consensus over speech norms breaks down beyond repair — and finds no place in which it might be worked through — publics do not simply fall silent; they leave, and they build elsewhere. The two chapters here describe both sides of that movement: the alternative spaces that ruptures bring into being (Chapter 10), and the mechanism by which moderation itself drives users toward them (Chapter 11).

Chapter 10 conceptualises "alt-tech" platforms as counter-spheres. Much of the existing literature describes these spaces by what makes them peculiar — their subcultures, their permissive moderation, their bespoke infrastructures. The chapter argues instead that they are a recurring structural response to normative rupture: where speech norms are essentially contested and subject to constant discursive contestation, deep disagreement eventually leads some users to constitute alternatives, building complementary affordances to bypass the limitations of their former hosts. Taking a distant-reading approach, it sketches a brief history of such ruptures across the Web — cloud hosts, the blogging spheres, wikis, social media, messaging applications and, latterly, large language models — before comparing the normative and technical affordances of competing spaces, from Twitter, X to Truth Social. What it finds is less a story of opposition than of coexistence: counter-spheres and the mainstream platforms they break from tend to complement one another's normative and technical limits, each affording what the other cannot.

Chapter 11 looks at the engine of the rupture itself. Through an analysis of deplatformed and demoted YouTube videos, it examines how the platform moderated COVID-19 conspiracy narratives between April and October 2020. Its question is how far YouTube's own moderation actions are taken up as part of the conspiratorial narratives they target — how moderation, in other words, becomes entangled with questions of truth and visibility at once. To intervene in what counts as true is also to intervene in what can be seen; and where moderation is read as an external, arbitrary line drawn around belief, the act of moderating supplies fresh evidence for the very suspicion it meant to dispel. Read through the comments of the moderated, this yields what the chapter calls folk theories of Big Tech persecution: a sense that demotion and deplatforming are not corrections but proof of a system bent on controlling the flow of sensitive truths. Enforcement without deliberation, here, does not settle the dispute; it manufactures the distrust that sends users looking for somewhere else to speak.

Between them, the two chapters show that ruptures are not anomalies at the edge of the system but a predictable product of moderation that only enforces. A platform that draws and polices its lines with no shared venue for negotiating them does not resolve disagreement so much as displace it — into counter-spheres that constitute themselves precisely against the norms they were excluded by. Having traced this far the costs of moderation reduced to its censorial function, the thesis turns, in Part V, to what platforms have begun to do about it.
