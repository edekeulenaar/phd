# Abstract

In Internet studies, content moderation is typically framed as a question of platform regulation and governance. Yet the etymology and the older media uses of the term give reason to treat moderation more broadly, as one among several speech governance techniques — alongside censorship, gatekeeping, editorial convention, and, latterly, the alignment of AI text generation — through which public discourse is managed in relation to historically derived norms. So understood, moderation is not the property of platforms alone; it is a constitutive force that materialises the boundaries of expression, the "discursive architectures" within which some things may be said and others may not, and through which less permitted speech migrates to wherever it is best expressed.

This thesis argues that moderation holds two functions in tension. A censorial function frames and enforces the limits of acceptable speech; a dialogical function contextualises, mediates and keeps exchange moving for the sake of socio-informational coherence. Its distinctiveness, and its legitimacy, lie in calibrating the first to serve the second. Yet what counts as "problematic" speech is in constant negotiation, and the rise of social media disrupted earlier systems of debate management while concentrating — and at the same time redistributing — the means of governing speech. As platforms leaned on enforcement "at scale," the dialogical half of the repertoire atrophied, leaving moderation heavy on sanction and short of the consensus that would render its limits legitimate.

Drawing on digital methods — particularly trace research, which reconstructs what has been removed, demoted or relabelled from the residue it leaves behind — the thesis offers a landscape view of how speech norms shape moderation, how dissensus over those norms generates crises of legitimacy, and how moderation reorganises the wider geography of online public spheres. [Part I. What is moderation?](#/part-1) establishes the conceptual and methodological ground, reading moderation against censorship and debate management and developing techniques for capturing it as an ephemeral but structuring "platform effect". [Part II. Speech affordances](#/part-2) shows how speech norms materialise as speech affordances — the policies, techniques and user cultures that signal what may be said and govern how content circulates between more- and less-moderated regions of the Web — including in the moderate speech generated by large language models. [Part III. Moderation crises](#/part-3) traces crises of moderation as norms diverge between platforms and their users, legal institutions, legacy media, and national histories of political violence. [Part IV. Ruptures](#/part-4) follows the ruptures that unresolved disagreement produces: the counter-spheres and "alt-tech" platforms it brings into being, the rival encyclopaedias of "alternative facts" it generates, and the "folk theories" of persecution it breeds among the moderated. [Part V. Modulation and consensus](#/part-5) turns to what platforms have begun to do in response, tracing a shift from adjudicative moderation toward modular and outsourced forms, and finally toward consensus-building mechanisms such as Community Notes.

In concluding, the thesis offers a prescriptive reflection on the sustainability of moderation. The current appetite for a "prosocial" internet set against moderation identifies something real but mistakes the remedy: the trouble is not moderation as such, nor an excess of it, but the atrophy of its dialogical half. Sustainability asks not for less moderation but for the restoration of spaces in which speech norms can be articulated, contested and revised — moving the governance of public discourse from adjudication back toward negotiation.
