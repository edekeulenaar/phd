# Abstract

In Internet studies, content moderation is typically framed as a question of platform regulation and governance. Yet, the etymology and media uses of the term “moderation” give reason to broaden its definition as a social, political and legislative practice that modulates public discourse in relation to historically-derived norms. These norms have tended to shape the communication affordances of platforms and continue to define the boundaries of what may be said in public. 

However, what counts as "problematic" speech has shifted dramatically across social movements and public debates, placing speech norms in constant negotiation. The emergence of social media disrupted earlier moderation systems and enabled once-marginalized actors to re-enter the “marketplace of ideas” in defence of formerly taboo ideas. In the absence of strong consensus-building affordances, speech norms themselves — what is acceptable to say, when, and how — have become the very heart of public contention.

This PhD offers a landscape view of how content moderation practices have been shaped by speech norms, how dissensus over such norms generates crises of legitimacy, and how moderation evolves across different actors, platforms, and infrastructures. The study uses digital methods — particularly trace-based research — to reconstruct moderation effects and analyze how moderation unfolds as an ephemeral but structuring component of online public spheres.

**Chapter 1** examines the institutional and historical foundations of speech moderation, showing how post-war liberal democracies implemented modular systems of speech management to counter the normalization of historical extremes. 

**Chapter 2** develops a methodological framework for capturing moderation as a platform effect, offering techniques for tracing the disappearance and replatforming of objectionable content. 

**Chapter 3** theorizes how speech norms materialize as speech affordances — technical, cultural and policy signals that regulate expression and structure discursive circulation. 

**Chapter 4** maps crises of moderation across four contexts: between platforms and users; platforms and institutions; platforms and media organizations; and between large language models and controversial public discourse. 

**Chapter 5** focuses on counter-spheres — particularly alt-tech spaces — that emerge from ruptures in normative consensus, and examines their technical and ideological affordances. 

**Chapter 6** describes how platforms have begun adopting modular and outsourced forms of moderation, culminating in consensus-building systems like Community Notes. 

In concluding, the thesis offers a prescriptive reflection on how platforms might expand consensus-building affordances as part of their basic architecture, moving from adjudication toward negotiation in the governance of public discourse.
