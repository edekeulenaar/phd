# Abstract

In Internet studies, content moderation is typically framed as a question of platform regulation and governance. Yet, the etymology and the older media uses of the term give reason to treat moderation more broadly, as one among several speech governance techniques — alongside censorship, gatekeeping, editorial convention, and, latterly, AI alignment — through which public discourse is managed in relation to historically derived norms. So understood, moderation is not the property of platforms alone but a constitutive force that materialises the boundaries and cultures of platforms, and through which less permitted speech migrates to wherever it is best expressed. Drawing on digital methods — particularly trace research, which reconstructs what has been removed, demoted or relabelled from the residue it leaves behind — the thesis offers a landscape view of how speech norms shape moderation, how dissensus over those norms generates crises of legitimacy, and how moderation reorganises the wider geography of online public spheres. [Part I. What is moderation?](Part%20I.%20What%20is%20moderation?.md) establishes the conceptual and methodological ground, reading moderation against censorship and debate management and developing techniques for capturing it as an ephemeral but structuring "platform effect". [Part II. Speech affordances](Part%20II.%20Speech%20affordances.md) shows how speech norms materialise as speech affordances — the policies, techniques and user cultures that signal what may be said and govern how content circulates between more- and less-moderated regions of the Web — including in the moderate speech generated by large language models. [Part III. Moderation crises](Part%20III.%20Moderation%20crises.md) traces crises of moderation as norms diverge between platforms and their users, legal institutions, legacy media, and national histories of political violence. [Part IV. Ruptures](Part%20IV.%20Ruptures.md) follows the ruptures that unresolved disagreement produces: the counter-spheres and "alt-tech" platforms it brings into being, the rival encyclopaedias of "alternative facts" it generates, and the "folk theories" of persecution it breeds among the moderated. [Part V. Modulation and consensus](Part%20V.%20Modulation%20and%20consensus.md) turns to what platforms have begun to do in response, tracing a shift from adjudicative moderation toward modular and outsourced forms, and finally toward consensus-building mechanisms such as Community Notes. In concluding, the thesis offers a prescriptive reflection on the long-term sustainability of moderation.
