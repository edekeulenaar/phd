# **Part I. What is moderation?**

Part I sets out the conceptual and methodological ground for everything that follows. Before asking how moderation shapes platforms, how it fractures into crises, or how it redraws the geography of online speech, there are two prior questions to settle: what moderation is, and how a practice so often hidden can be studied at all. Chapters 1 and 2 take these in turn.

[Chapter 1. Censorship and moderation](#/chapter-1) takes up the concept itself. At this point in its history, the term "moderation" has increasingly become bound up with that of censorship, and the chapter argues that this association conceals more than it explains. As is common with controversies, what moderation has come to mean is unclear except through its contestation. To get behind it, the chapter proposes a distant reading of a large body of scholarship on content moderation and on its neighbouring speech-governance practices — censorship above all — reconstructing how each defines the practice ("what"), to whom it attributes it ("who"), how it justifies it ("why"), and through which techniques it is carried out ("how"). From this comes the claim that organises the thesis: that moderation bears traits of two practices at once. It censors, in that it frames and enforces the norms within which information may circulate, and delimits the reach and visibility of transgressive speech so as to slow the normalisation of historical extremes. But it also prompts, contextualises, mediates, redirects and otherwise modulates what is exchanged, for the sake of what I call socio-informational coherence — the older work of the moderator who keeps a plurivocal exchange intelligible and ongoing. Moderation, then, is best understood as a modular form of speech management that draws on both. When the balance between the two is lost, and moderation collapses into one technique or the other, it risks forfeiting its legitimacy; and that imbalance, I argue, signals a deeper loss of the consensus-building conventions through which a public arrives at the norms of its own debate — something approximating what Habermas called the "lifeworld" of dialogue.

[Chapter 2. After deplatforming](#/chapter-2) moves from definition to method. Moderation is, by design, ephemeral: content is quietly demoted, a label appears and then disappears, an account is withheld in one country but not another. This makes platform data hard to read at face value. When platforms were treated as mere "intermediaries", their data could be taken as a record of user behaviour observed, as it were, "in the wild"; but that data is in fact shaped on the platform side — by promotion and demotion, deplatforming, labelling and downranking — and on the user side, by obfuscation, the gaming of metrics, coded language, and migration to less stringently moderated spaces. Rather than treat these effects as noise that spoils the data, the chapter proposes to make them the object of study. Drawing on censorship scholars such as Darnton and Robert C. Post — and on their habit of reconstructing the "scene of disappearance" of suppressed information — it assembles a repertoire of trace-research methods for capturing moderation as a platform effect: charting the evolution of moderation policy through the Internet Archive; retaining removable content through what we term "dynamic archiving"; capturing overt traces such as flags and labels; reading the wording users deploy to skirt enforcement; and following content and users as they replatform on less moderated spaces.

A few commitments run through the part and are assumed by the rest of the thesis. Moderation is not a sub-problem of platform governance but a societal practice through which publics decide what may be said — a political technology in its own right. Its legitimacy turns on the balance between its censorial and dialogical functions, and it is the breakdown of that balance that the later chapters set out to document. And however hidden it is by design, it can still be studied empirically, through the kinds of trace methods gathered here. From here, Part II turns to how speech norms are materialised in the affordances of particular platforms.
